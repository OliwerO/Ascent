# Domain 9: Mobility & Flexibility for Mountain Athletes

---

## §9.1 Mountain athlete mobility needs assessment

**Core Principle.** Mountain athletes face a unique constellation of mobility demands driven by prolonged uphill postures, boot-constrained ankles, heavy pack carriage, and sport-specific overhead requirements. Six joint regions require systematic screening: ankle dorsiflexion, hip flexor length, thoracic extension/rotation, hip external rotation, shoulder complex mobility, and posterior chain extensibility. Deficits in any region cascade into compensatory movement patterns that increase injury risk during the high-force, high-variability environment of mountain terrain.

**Evidence Summary.** Ankle dorsiflexion is the most consequential mobility target for this athlete profile. The weight-bearing lunge test (WBLT, or knee-to-wall test) is the gold-standard field screen, with each 1 cm of toe-to-wall distance corresponding to approximately **3.6° of dorsiflexion** (Bennell et al. 1998). Male normative values for ages 20–30 are **14.2 ± 3.0 cm** (Gohil et al. 2022), and the test demonstrates excellent reliability (ICC = 0.96–0.99; MDC = 1.1–1.5 cm; Konor et al. 2012). In skiing populations, mean voluntary dorsiflexion reaches 42.7° in weight-bearing (Shealy & Miller 1987), yet most ski boots constrain ankle ROM to <30°. Alpine touring boots restore some ROM in walk mode, but the transition between uphill (walk) and downhill (ski) modes creates repeated exposure to both free and constrained ankle ranges. Ankle dorsiflexion ROM is a primary predictor of squat depth (R² = 0.435 combined with hip flexion; Kim et al. 2015), making it directly relevant to gym performance. Military load carriage studies confirm that backpack loads increase ankle dorsi-plantar flexion ROM as a compensatory strategy while simultaneously increasing inversion-eversion excursion, indicating greater instability (Lee, Yoon & Shin 2017).

Hip flexor length is assessed via the Modified Thomas Test (MTT). A negative (normal) result shows the thigh dropping to horizontal (0° extension) with the knee at ~90° flexion. Positive findings for iliopsoas tightness show the thigh remaining above horizontal; for rectus femoris tightness, knee extension beyond 90°. Pelvic tilt strongly confounds measurement (r = 0.98 correlation with error; Kim & Ha 2015), so lumbo-pelvic stabilization is essential for valid assessment (intra-rater ICC = 0.97–0.99 with stabilization; Eimiller et al. 2024). Sustained uphill walking at 25–45% grade maintains the hip in 40–60° flexion for extended periods, promoting adaptive shortening of the psoas and rectus femoris, which correlates with anterior pelvic tilt, increased lumbar lordosis, and low back pain.

Thoracic spine mobility shows normal rotation of **35–50° per side** and standing extension of approximately 8.7° from neutral (Johnson et al. 2011). Heavy backpacks increase trunk forward lean and thoracic kyphosis, with significant decreases in thoracic rotation at loads ≥40% body mass. Critically, hip belt use restores higher thoracic rotation versus no-belt conditions (narrative review, PMC9180465). A 15–20 kg pack represents 20–25% body weight for a 70–80 kg male — enough to produce meaningful postural effects. Paraglider harness posture compounds this by creating anterior shoulder compression and promoting kyphosis during flight.

For hip external rotation, passive benchmarks are **40–60°** at 90° hip flexion (AMA 1990: 50°), with a total rotation arc (IR + ER) of ≥70° considered healthy. Reduced total arc is a risk factor for hip, groin, and hamstring injuries (Sauers et al.). Asymmetry of ≥8° in hip internal rotation is associated with increased injury risk (Li et al., cited in PMC6253754). Ski touring stance, telemark-like movements, and split-stance skinning all require adequate external rotation to maintain knee alignment over the foot.

Shoulder mobility demands in hike-and-fly paragliding include sustained arm elevation at 100–140° for brake control, rapid overhead movements during turbulence handling, and powerful pulling during launch and ground handling. The most common overuse injury is supraspinatus tendonitis, exacerbated by protracted scapulae and forward shoulder posture from harness wear (Clifford, Cross Country Magazine). Normal shoulder flexion should reach 150–180°, with external rotation ≥45° for adequate brake-line management.

Posterior chain flexibility benchmarks include an Active Straight Leg Raise (ASLR) target of **≥70° hip flexion** with a straight leg. Descent loading generates 3–7× bodyweight eccentric forces per step (Faulhaber et al. 2020), with 75% of hiking accidents occurring during descent, making gastrocnemius-soleus and hamstring extensibility directly relevant to injury resilience.

A validated screening battery should include: (1) Overhead Squat Assessment for global movement quality (V-Aiken = 1.00 for 14/15 compensations; Aydin et al. 2023); (2) WBLT for ankle dorsiflexion (target ≥12 cm, <2 cm side-to-side asymmetry); (3) MTT with pelvic stabilization for hip flexor length; (4) ASLR for hamstring length (≥70°); (5) FMS Shoulder Mobility Test for combined IR/ER; (6) Y-Balance Test for dynamic balance (anterior asymmetry >4 cm = elevated risk; Plisky et al. 2006); and (7) Single-Leg Squat for hip stability and knee valgus detection. FMS composite scores ≤14 are associated with increased injury risk in military and athletic populations (Kiesel et al. 2007).

**Application Rule.** Conduct the full seven-test screening battery at four time points annually: pre-ski season (October), mid-ski season (January), pre-hike-and-fly season (April), and mid-summer (July). Flag any ankle WBLT <10 cm, side-to-side asymmetry >2 cm, positive Thomas Test (thigh above horizontal), ASLR <70°, or Y-Balance anterior asymmetry >4 cm for targeted intervention. Re-screen flagged deficits every 4 weeks until resolved. Use the Overhead Squat Assessment with heel-lift modification to differentiate ankle versus hip contributions to movement dysfunction.

**Key Sources.** Bennell et al. 1998; Konor et al. 2012; Gohil et al. 2022; Powden et al. 2015; Shealy & Miller 1987; Kim et al. 2015; Lee, Yoon & Shin 2017; Eimiller et al. 2024; Kim & Ha 2015; Johnson et al. 2011; Plisky et al. 2006; Kiesel et al. 2007; Aydin et al. 2023; Faulhaber et al. 2020.

## §9.2 Static versus dynamic stretching: current evidence and dose thresholds

**Core Principle.** The pre-2010 consensus that static stretching impairs strength and power has been substantially revised by dose-response research. Static stretching held for **<60 seconds per muscle group** does not meaningfully reduce performance, and when followed by dynamic activity, any trivial decrements are fully negated. Dynamic stretching remains the preferred pre-training modality for its dual benefits of ROM improvement and performance enhancement. For chronic ROM gains, static and PNF stretching are superior to dynamic stretching. Loaded stretching through full-ROM resistance training is equally effective as dedicated stretching for improving flexibility, making it the most time-efficient approach for concurrent training athletes.

**Evidence Summary.** The landmark systematic review by Kay & Blazevich (2012), covering 106 studies, established that stretches held <30 seconds produce no detrimental effect on force (pooled estimate −1.1%), stretches of 30–45 seconds show minimal impact (−1.9%), and only durations ≥60 seconds per muscle group produce consistent impairments, with a sigmoidal dose-response turning point at ~60 seconds. Only 14% of studies using <30-second holds found any significant impairment, compared with 63% of studies using >2-minute holds. Behm, Blazevich, Kay & McHugh (2016) confirmed: static stretching averages −3.7% performance change, dynamic stretching +1.3%, and PNF −4.4%, with the critical moderator being total stretch duration (≥60s = −4.6% vs. <60s = −1.1%). When post-stretching dynamic activities were included, performance effects were essentially eliminated.

The most recent multilevel meta-analysis by **Warneke et al. (2024)**, covering 83 studies with >400 effect sizes, found an overall static stretch-induced maximal strength loss of ES = −0.21 (p = 0.003) — a small effect. Durations ≥60 seconds showed a large effect (ES = −0.84, p = 0.004), but shorter durations did not reach statistical significance (p = 0.08–0.24). Most significantly, **stretching did not negatively influence complex athletic performance** (jumping, sprinting) versus passive or active controls, and actually showed a small positive effect on jumping performance (ES = 0.15, p = 0.006). The authors concluded that results "do not support previous recommendations to exclude static stretching from warm-up routines."

For post-training static stretching and recovery, a meta-analysis by Afonso et al. (2021) of 11 RCTs found **no significant benefit** for recovery of strength, ROM, or DOMS at any time point compared to passive recovery. A 2025 meta-analysis (PMC12521117) confirmed no statistically significant improvements in pain perception, pain threshold, or muscle strength from post-exercise stretching. The Warneke et al. (2025) Delphi Consensus Statement, uniting 20+ international experts including Behm, Kay, Blazevich, and Afonso, reached 100% agreement: "Based on current evidence, the panel does NOT recommend stretching as a post-exercise recovery routine." However, no detrimental effect was found either, so post-session stretching may be used according to individual preference.

For chronic ROM gains, Ingram et al. (2024) conducted the definitive meta-analysis (189 studies, 6,654 adults): acute flexibility is maximized at **4 minutes cumulative per session**, chronic flexibility at **10 minutes per muscle group per week**, with no additional benefit beyond these thresholds. Neither intensity, age, sex, nor training status moderated improvements. The Warneke et al. (2025) Delphi consensus recommends **2–3 sets daily of 30–120 seconds per muscle** for chronic flexibility. Bandy et al. (1997) established that 30-second holds are the effective minimum — increasing to 60 seconds produced no additional ROM gain.

Dynamic stretching as a warm-up produces small-to-moderate performance improvements (+1.3%; Behm et al. 2016). A 2023 network meta-analysis found dynamic stretching improved CMJ height by 1.60 cm, with optimal duration of **7–10 minutes**. The recommended warm-up sequence is submaximal aerobic activity → large-amplitude dynamic stretching → sport-specific dynamic activities (Behm & Chaouachi 2011).

PNF (contract-relax) stretching produces large ROM improvements chronically, similar to static stretching (Konrad et al. 2024 meta-analysis), but carries a −4.4% performance decrement and typically requires a partner. PNF is best positioned post-training or in dedicated sessions. The CRAC (contract-relax-agonist-contract) variant at 2 × 15 seconds appears more effective than longer durations due to fatigue accumulation (Babault et al. 2025).

Loaded stretching through full-ROM resistance training is strongly evidence-supported as a dual-purpose approach. Two independent meta-analyses confirmed **no significant difference** between resistance training and dedicated stretching for ROM improvements: Afonso et al. (2021, 11 RCTs, ES = −0.22, p = 0.206) and Konrad et al. (2023, 52 studies, ES = 0.08, p = 0.79). Training at long muscle lengths may be superior for hypertrophy: Kassiano et al. found lengthened partials produced approximately **twice the hypertrophy** of full ROM in calf raises (+14.9% vs. +7.3% for lateral gastrocnemius). Maeo et al. (2021) showed seated hamstring curls (which stretch the bi-articular hamstrings) produced considerably more hypertrophy than lying curls. Romanian deadlifts, deep goblet squats, and deficit lunges function as combined hypertrophy and mobility stimuli.

**Application Rule.** Pre-training: use dynamic stretching for 7–10 minutes; if static stretching is preferred for a specific restriction, keep holds ≤30 seconds per muscle and follow with dynamic activity. Post-training: static stretching is optional (neither beneficial nor harmful for recovery); use 30–60 seconds per muscle if desired for perceived benefit. For chronic ROM gains: accumulate 5–10 minutes per target muscle group per week through any combination of static holds (30s minimum), PNF (2 × 15s contract-relax cycles), or full-ROM resistance training. Prioritize loaded stretching (deep squats, RDLs, deficit lunges) as the primary flexibility strategy, supplemented by targeted static stretching only for areas not adequately addressed by training.

**Key Sources.** Kay & Blazevich 2012; Behm et al. 2016; Warneke et al. 2024; Warneke et al. 2025 (Delphi); Afonso et al. 2021 (recovery); Afonso et al. 2021 (RT vs. stretching); Ingram et al. 2024; Konrad et al. 2023; Konrad et al. 2024; Bandy et al. 1997; Schoenfeld & Grgic 2020; Maeo et al. 2021; Babault et al. 2025.

## §9.3 Mobility as recovery tool versus training stressor

**Core Principle.** Mobility work exists on a spectrum from restorative (light movement, gentle stretching, foam rolling) to stressful (aggressive end-range holds, high-volume stretching, intense PNF). For a concurrent training athlete already managing gym sessions and mountain days, mobility work must be deliberately positioned on the restorative end to avoid adding to total fatigue — except during deload periods when the training stress budget permits more aggressive ROM work.

**Evidence Summary.** Yoga and structured stretching show modest evidence for recovery benefits, primarily through parasympathetic activation rather than direct tissue repair. Tyagi & Cohen (2016) systematically reviewed 59 studies and found that 3 of 4 studies comparing yoga practitioners to non-practitioners showed enhanced parasympathetic activity (HRV time and/or frequency domain), though study quality was generally poor with small samples. A 2026 study published in Annals of Medicine found yoga practitioners demonstrated significantly greater parasympathetic reactivation after submaximal exercise versus aerobic and resistance training groups (HF power: F = 2.67, p = 0.001, ηp² = 0.11). The practical benefits of gentle mobility as recovery appear to be primarily psychophysiological — stress reduction, improved sleep quality, enhanced blood flow, and perceived readiness.

Foam rolling demonstrates a **large acute effect on ROM** (d = 0.76, 95% CI 0.55–0.98; Skinner et al. 2020 meta-analysis of 32 studies) and moderate chronic effects when maintained for >4 weeks (ES = 0.823; Konrad et al. 2022). For recovery specifically, Wiewelhove et al. (2019) conducted the first comprehensive meta-analysis of 21 foam rolling studies and found small positive effects on sprint performance (+2.7%), flexibility (+5.0%), and reduced muscle pain perception post-exercise. Foam rollers showed larger strength recovery effects (+5.6%, g = 0.27) than roller massagers. However, overall effects were characterized as "rather minor and partly negligible." Acute ROM gains from foam rolling are transient, typically lasting <30 minutes. The optimal acute dose is **90–120 seconds per muscle group** (Hendricks et al. 2019). Notably, foam rolling was not effective for ankle dorsiflexion improvements in the triceps surae (Konrad et al. 2022), so it should not be relied upon for this critical target.

The distinction between restorative and stressful mobility work is defined by intensity and volume. Behm et al. (2024) found that slow dynamic stretching post-fatigue improved quadriceps force recovery, ROM, and joint position sense versus control, while aggressive static stretching maintained ROM but did not improve force recovery. Higher-intensity stretching produces larger ROM increases but also greater strength decrements (systematic review, MDPI 2022: 8 of 16 studies showed greater ROM from higher intensity, while 2 of 4 strength studies showed greater decrements). At extreme volumes (>30 minutes per session), stretching can actually induce measurable hypertrophy (d = 0.20) and strength gains (d = 0.30) in plantar flexors (Warneke et al. 2023) — confirming that high-volume stretching functions as a training stimulus requiring recovery, not a recovery tool.

For concurrent training athletes, diminishing returns emerge quickly. Ingram et al. (2024) established that **chronic ROM gains plateau at ~10 minutes per week per muscle group** — additional volume provides no further flexibility benefit. Since full-ROM resistance training provides comparable ROM gains to dedicated stretching (Afonso et al. 2021; Konrad et al. 2023), athletes already performing deep squats, RDLs, and full-ROM pressing may need minimal additional stretching volume. When total training load is high (2–3 gym sessions + 1–3 mountain days), aggressive stretching should be minimized in favor of gentle movement flows, brief foam rolling as warm-up preparation, and reliance on full-ROM training for flexibility maintenance.

**Application Rule.** Classify all mobility work as either "recovery-grade" (RPE ≤4/10, gentle movement, holds ≤30s, foam rolling at moderate pressure) or "training-grade" (RPE ≥5/10, PNF, holds >45s at end range, loaded end-range positions). During normal training weeks, use only recovery-grade mobility on rest days and post-mountain days. Reserve training-grade mobility for the Thursday dedicated session and deload weeks. Foam rolling: 90–120 seconds per muscle group as warm-up preparation; do not use for ankle dorsiflexion (insufficient evidence for triceps surae). When weekly training load exceeds 6 sessions (gym + mountain combined), limit total dedicated mobility to one 30-minute session per week plus daily 12-minute maintenance.

**Key Sources.** Tyagi & Cohen 2016; Skinner et al. 2020; Konrad et al. 2022; Wiewelhove et al. 2019; Hendricks et al. 2019; Behm et al. 2024; Warneke et al. 2023; Ingram et al. 2024; Afonso et al. 2021; Konrad et al. 2023.

## §9.4 Periodization of mobility work across the annual plan

**Core Principle.** Mobility programming should follow the same periodization logic as strength and endurance training: higher-volume, more aggressive ROM work during lower-stress periods (off-season general preparation, deload weeks), and lower-volume maintenance during high-stress periods (in-season mountain activity, caloric deficit phases). The seasonal shift between ski touring and hike-and-fly also changes which mobility targets are prioritized.

**Evidence Summary.** Classical periodization theory supports concentrating flexibility development during preparatory phases when competing recovery demands are lowest. Full-ROM resistance training provides comparable ROM improvements to dedicated stretching (Afonso et al. 2021; Behm et al. 2023), meaning that gym-heavy off-season phases inherently maintain ROM through loaded training. However, specific mountain-sport demands (ankle dorsiflexion for boot interface, thoracic rotation for pack carriage) require targeted supplementation beyond what compound lifts provide.

Deload weeks represent the optimal window for aggressive ROM work. When training volume and intensity drop 40–60%, the reduction in accumulated mechanical tension allows connective tissues to better tolerate challenging end-range work without competing recovery demands. The fitness-fatigue model supports this: deloads allow fatigue to dissipate while fitness persists, and mobility work occupies the freed training "space" without adding meaningful systemic stress. Ogasawara et al. (2013) demonstrated that detraining periods followed by retraining restore mTOR signaling sensitivity, suggesting periodized recovery enhances subsequent adaptation. Practically, deload weeks should shift from 15–20-minute sessions to 30–45-minute sessions incorporating PNF, longer static holds (45–60s), and exploration of new end-ranges.

Caloric deficit phases demand caution. The 2023 IOC Consensus Statement on Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (REDs) establishes that low energy availability (LEA, ≤30 kcal/kg FFM/day) reduces IGF-1 secretion by **20–30% within several days**, decreasing muscle protein synthesis at rest by 27% within 5 days. The Journal of ISAKOS (2024) systematic review confirms that REDs reduces anabolic hormones affecting tendon repair, with nutrient deficiencies in vitamin D and collagen precursors impairing healing of microtrauma to cartilage, tendons, and ligaments. Athletes in LEA states face **2.5× greater risk of bone stress injury** (Mountjoy et al. 2023). Logue et al. (2019) suggests that hormonal disruptions from LEA can increase connective tissue stiffness. During deficit phases, aggressive end-range mobility work (PNF, intense loaded stretching) should be avoided in favor of gentle maintenance-level work, while ensuring energy availability remains above 30 kcal/kg FFM/day and protein intake ≥1.6 g/kg/day.

Post-multi-day mountain trips require staged recovery. Passive muscle stiffness increases immediately after eccentric exercise and remains elevated for **up to 120 hours (5 days)** (Hosoda et al. 2011). ROM decreases by 20–45° acutely, with swelling peaking at days 4–10 (Peake et al. 2017). The repeated bout effect attenuates stiffness increases in subsequent bouts, meaning regular mountain activity progressively builds tolerance. A 2025 study found eccentric contractions themselves produce more than twice the acute ROM increase (2.2–6.0°) compared to static stretching, with greater changes in tissue stiffness and stretch tolerance. Pack-induced thoracic kyphosis is confirmed by research showing loads >15% bodyweight create forward trunk lean with altered spinal curvature (PMC6887984).

**Application Rule.** Structure annual mobility periodization as follows:

- **Off-season strength blocks (general prep):** 2–3 dedicated sessions per week (15–20 min each) plus daily maintenance. Focus on ROM development for priority deficits. Include PNF and training-grade mobility.
- **In-season mountain activity:** Reduce to 1–2 dedicated sessions per week plus daily maintenance. Shift to recovery-grade mobility. Prioritize ski-season targets (ankle DF, hip flexors) November–April and hike-and-fly targets (thoracic rotation, shoulders) May–October.
- **Deload weeks (every 4–6 weeks):** Increase dedicated session duration to 30–45 min. Introduce PNF, longer holds (45–60s), and new end-range exploration. This is the primary window for ROM development.
- **Caloric deficit phases:** Maintain existing routines at moderate intensity only. No PNF, no aggressive end-range work. Prioritize collagen supplementation (10–15g + vitamin C pre-training; Shaw et al. 2017) and protein ≥1.6 g/kg/day.
- **Post-multi-day trips:** Days 1–2: gentle walking, low-pressure foam rolling, no intense stretching of damaged muscles. Days 2–4: introduce gentle dynamic mobility (cat-cow, seated twists, hip CARs, gentle calf stretches). Thoracic-specific recovery (open-book stretches, foam roller extensions, wall angels) to counter pack-induced kyphosis. Days 4–7: return to normal mobility programming.

**Key Sources.** Afonso et al. 2021; Behm et al. 2023; Ogasawara et al. 2013; Mountjoy et al. 2023 (IOC REDs Consensus); Hosoda et al. 2011; Peake et al. 2017; Shaw et al. 2017; Logue et al. 2019.

## §9.5 Timing and integration with the strength training microcycle

**Core Principle.** For a concurrent training athlete with 2–3 gym sessions, 1–3 mountain days, and one dedicated Thursday core-and-mobility slot per week, mobility must be embedded within existing training rather than stacked on top of it. Three integration strategies are strongly evidence-supported: (1) dynamic warm-ups as pre-session mobility, (2) full-ROM resistance training as intra-session loaded stretching, and (3) low-intensity dedicated work on rest or recovery days. Post-session static stretching is neither beneficial nor harmful for recovery or hypertrophy — it is optional.

**Evidence Summary.** Pre-session dynamic warm-up is well-supported for both ROM improvement and performance enhancement. A 2024 Frontiers study showed integrative movement warm-ups (combining SMR, dynamic stretching, activation) performed 2–3× per week over 6 weeks significantly improved hip ROM, ankle ROM, and squat quality. Ribeiro et al. (2020) demonstrated that progressive warm-up sets (40% → 80% of working load) optimized squat and bench press performance. The warm-up sequence should progress from general cardiovascular activation → targeted dynamic mobility for the session's primary movement patterns → progressive loaded warm-up sets.

Intra-session loaded stretching is the highest-leverage integration strategy. Behm et al. (2023) established in a systematic review and meta-analysis that **resistance training through full ROM induces ROM improvements comparable to stretching**, concluding: "When circumstances dictate (i.e., time restrictions), flexibility training benefits can be incorporated into RT." Schoenfeld, Wackerhage & De Souza (2022) proposed inter-set stretching as a time-efficient strategy, noting that both lengthening contractions and passive stretch can acutely activate anabolic signaling pathways. Van Every, Schoenfeld et al. (2022) found loaded inter-set stretch showed a potential hypertrophic benefit for the soleus (type-1 dominant muscle), though evidence for other muscles is equivocal (Wadhi et al. 2021 found no benefit for pectoralis major). Practically, deep pause squats, full-ROM RDLs with controlled eccentrics, and deficit reverse lunges simultaneously build strength at end range and improve flexibility — this counts as both training volume and mobility work.

The concern that post-exercise stretching interferes with hypertrophy via mTOR pathway disruption is **not supported by evidence**. Aoki et al. (2006) demonstrated that mTOR is involved in stretch-mediated hypertrophy in animal models — stretching activates rather than inhibits mTOR. Warneke et al. (2023) found high-volume stretching (≥30 min) can actually induce hypertrophy through mechanotransduction and mTOR activation, though practical volumes used in post-workout stretching (30–60s per muscle) are far below the threshold needed to produce any signaling effect in either direction. The Afonso et al. (2021) systematic review of post-exercise stretching for recovery found "no evidence that stretching enhanced recovery... [and] no evidence to the contrary." The Afonso et al. (2025) Delphi consensus confirmed that typical post-workout stretching doses are insufficient to meaningfully interact with mTOR signaling. Post-session stretching is purely optional.

Dedicated mobility on rest days does not impair recovery. Low-intensity mobility work (<50% effort) does not generate meaningful systemic or local fatigue, does not activate significant mTOR signaling, and does not produce muscle damage. Stretching frequency is a positive moderator for ROM gains (Warneke et al. 2023), with daily practice producing greater improvements than lower frequencies. A systematic review of 26 studies found 6–10 minutes of low-intensity exercise to be the "sweet spot" for enhancing recovery, and active recovery does not appear to reduce the post-exercise inflammation necessary for adaptation.

**Application Rule.** Integrate mobility into the weekly microcycle as follows:

| Day | Training | Mobility Integration |
|-----|----------|---------------------|
| Gym day (squat focus) | 10-min dynamic warm-up targeting ankles, hips, thoracic spine; full-ROM pause squats and deep lunges as working sets; optional 5-min post-session stretch | Warm-up + loaded training = ~25 min effective mobility |
| Gym day (hinge focus) | 10-min dynamic warm-up targeting hamstrings, hips, posterior chain; full-ROM RDLs with 3–4s eccentrics as working sets | Warm-up + loaded training = ~20 min effective mobility |
| Gym day (upper body) | 10-min warm-up targeting thoracic spine, shoulders, scapular activation; full-ROM pressing | Warm-up = ~10 min |
| Thursday (Core + Mobility) | 35–45 min dedicated session (Protocol C below) | Primary ROM development session |
| Mountain day | Post-activity: 10-min foam roll + gentle stretches targeting thoracic spine after pack days | Recovery-grade mobility |
| Rest day | Protocol A daily maintenance (12 min) | ROM maintenance |

Reserve PNF and aggressive end-range work exclusively for the Thursday dedicated session and deload weeks. Never perform training-grade mobility within 6 hours before a gym session. If total weekly sessions exceed 6 (gym + mountain combined), drop one dedicated mobility session and rely on warm-ups, loaded training, and daily maintenance only.

**Key Sources.** Behm et al. 2023; Schoenfeld, Wackerhage & De Souza 2022; Van Every et al. 2022; Wadhi et al. 2021; Aoki et al. 2006; Warneke et al. 2023; Afonso et al. 2021 (recovery); Afonso et al. 2025 (Delphi); Ribeiro et al. 2020.

## §9.6 Minimum effective dose and practical protocols

**Core Principle.** Chronic flexibility gains plateau at approximately **10 minutes per muscle group per week** with no additional benefit from higher volumes (Ingram et al. 2024). The minimum effective hold duration is **30 seconds** (Bandy et al. 1997). Frequency of ≥3 sessions per week prevents detraining of ROM gains (Nakamura et al. 2020). Consistency matters infinitely more than session intensity: 12 minutes daily outperforms 45 minutes once per week. Three protocols serve the complete mobility needs of this athlete profile, scaled by time investment and purpose.

**Evidence Summary.** Ingram et al. (2024) analyzed 189 studies and 6,654 adults to establish optimal stretching doses. Acute flexibility maximizes at 4 minutes cumulative per session per muscle group; chronic flexibility maximizes at 10 minutes per week per muscle group. Intensity does not moderate outcomes — stretching to mild tightness is sufficient, and stretching beyond the point of discomfort is unnecessary (confirmed by Arntz et al. 2024; Konrad et al. 2023). Thomas et al. (2018) reviewed 23 studies and established ≥5 minutes per muscle group per week as the minimum threshold for ROM gains, with ≥5 days/week frequency positively associated with improvements. Bandy et al. (1997) showed 30 seconds is the effective minimum hold duration with no additional gain from 60-second holds, and stretching once per day versus three times per day produced no significant difference over 6 weeks. Nakamura et al. (2020) demonstrated ROM gains are lost within ~5 weeks if frequency drops below twice per week. Foam rolling is effective as a warm-up preparation tool (large acute ROM effect, d = 0.76; Skinner et al. 2020) but is not effective for ankle dorsiflexion in the triceps surae (Konrad et al. 2022) and should not replace targeted stretching.

---

### Protocol A: Daily maintenance (12 minutes)

**When:** Every morning upon waking or before bed. Non-negotiable minimum.
**Equipment:** None required (wall or doorway optional).
**Design rationale:** Targets the four highest-priority areas for this athlete profile (ankle dorsiflexion, hip flexors, thoracic rotation, hip external rotation) at ~1 minute per side per area. Daily frequency accumulates 7–14 minutes per target per week, exceeding the 5–10 minute MED threshold.

| Order | Exercise | Prescription | Target |
|-------|----------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Cat-Cow | 1 × 60s (~8–10 cycles) | Spinal segmental wake-up, warm tissue for subsequent drills |
| 2 | Half-kneeling wall ankle mobilization | 2 × 30s each side | Ankle dorsiflexion — knee tracks over 2nd–3rd toe, heel stays planted. Critical for ski boot interface and squat depth |
| 3 | Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch with posterior pelvic tilt | 2 × 30s each side | Psoas and rectus femoris length — squeeze glute, tuck pelvis, reach same-side arm overhead for additional lat stretch |
| 4 | 90/90 hip stretch | 1 × 45s each side | Hip ER (front leg) + IR (back leg). Sit tall, lean forward with straight spine for front hip emphasis |
| 5 | Side-lying open book (thoracic rotation) | 5 reps + 5s end-range hold, each side | Thoracic rotation — addresses pack posture, overhead mobility for paragliding, ski rotational demands |
| 6 | Supine figure-4 stretch | 1 × 30s each side | Additional hip ER. Cross ankle over opposite knee, pull bottom leg toward chest |

**Weekly volume achieved:** Each target area receives 7 × 1–2 min = 7–14 min/week, meeting the Ingram et al. (2024) threshold for chronic ROM improvement.

### Protocol B: Pre-gym warm-up (10–12 minutes per variant)

**When:** Immediately before gym session, after 3–5 minutes general warm-up (rowing ergometer or stationary bike).
**Design rationale:** Dynamic-emphasis mobility targeting that session's primary movement patterns. No static holds >30 seconds. Foam rolling included where evidence supports it (not for ankle DF).

**B1 — Squat day** (ankle, hip, thoracic focus)

| Order | Exercise | Prescription |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Foam roll thoracic spine (segmental extension) | 2 min — pause at each segment 10–15s, extend back over roller |
| 2 | Half-kneeling ankle mobilization (banded if available) | 2 × 10 reps each side — drive knee forward over toes |
| 3 | Prying goblet squat (8–12 kg KB) | 3 × 15s hold at bottom — elbows pry knees, shift weight side to side |
| 4 | World's Greatest Stretch | 3 reps each side — lunge → elbow to instep → rotate and reach → straighten front leg |
| 5 | Bodyweight squat with 3s pause at bottom | 2 × 5 reps — sit tall, full depth, groove the pattern |

**B2 — Hinge day** (hamstring, hip, posterior chain focus)

| Order | Exercise | Prescription |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Foam roll hamstrings + glutes | 2 min — 30s per area per side, pause on tender points |
| 2 | 90/90 hip transitions (dynamic) | 2 × 5 transitions each direction — flow from one side to the other |
| 3 | Single-leg RDL (bodyweight) | 2 × 6 reps each side — active hamstring lengthening under control |
| 4 | Inchworm to downward dog | 5 reps — walk hands out → push hips high → pedal feet → walk feet to hands |
| 5 | Bodyweight good morning with reach | 2 × 8 reps — hinge with hands behind head, reach one arm to opposite foot for rotation |

**B3 — Upper body day** (thoracic, shoulder, scapular focus)

### Protocol C: Dedicated mobility session (40–45 minutes, 1–2×/week)

**When:** Thursday "Core + Mobility" slot or a rest day. **Equipment:** Foam roller, light kettlebell (8–16 kg), resistance band, optional yoga block.
**Design rationale:** Five-phase comprehensive session progressing from tissue preparation through dynamic movement, loaded stretching, sustained static holds, and sport-specific integration. This session accumulates the bulk of weekly volume for secondary target areas not addressed by daily maintenance.

**Phase 1 — Foam rolling preparation (5 min).** Tissue warming and acute ROM enhancement. Evidence supports moderate acute effect without performance detriment.

| Area | Duration | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Thoracic spine (segmental extension) | 90s | Pause at 4–5 segments; exhale and extend at each |
| Quads / hip flexors | 60s (30s/side) | Include rectus femoris along front of thigh |
| Glutes / piriformis | 60s (30s/side) | Cross ankle over opposite knee for deeper pressure |
| Lats | 60s (30s/side) | Side-lying, roller at armpit level |

Note: Foam rolling is not included for calves because evidence shows it is not effective for triceps surae or ankle dorsiflexion (Konrad et al. 2022).

**Phase 2 — Dynamic mobility flow (8 min).** Raise tissue temperature, rehearse movement patterns, prepare for loaded positions.

| Exercise | Prescription |
|----------|-------------|
| Cat-cow | 1 × 10 cycles — full spinal articulation |
| World's Greatest Stretch | 3 reps each side |
| 90/90 flow transitions | 2 × 6 transitions — actively lift and switch sides with control |
| Walking Spiderman lunges with overhead reach | 5 each side — deep lunge → reach same-side arm overhead (touring stride simulation with pack reach) |
| Leg swings (sagittal + frontal planes) | 10 each direction each side — standing, full ROM hip swings |

**Phase 3 — Loaded stretching (12 min).** Builds strength at end range while improving ROM. Evidence confirms this is as effective as dedicated stretching for flexibility (Konrad et al. 2023) with added strength and hypertrophy benefits.

| Exercise | Prescription | Load | Sport relevance |
|----------|-------------|------|-----------------|
| Prying goblet squat | 3 × 20s hold at bottom | 12–16 kg KB | Ankle DF, hip ER, adductors, thoracic extension — squat + touring prep |
| Deficit reverse lunge (2–4″ plate under front foot) | 2 × 6 reps/side, 3s pause at bottom | 8–12 kg KB goblet | Hip flexor stretch on trailing leg under load — touring stride simulation |
| Tempo RDL (4s eccentric) | 2 × 8 reps, 2s pause at bottom | Moderate load | Hamstring flexibility + eccentric strength — descent preparation |
| Jefferson curl (bodyweight → light load) | 2 × 5 reps (5s down, 5s up) | 0–5 kg | Segmental spinal control, posterior chain stretch. Light load, total control, stop at any pain |
| Half-kneeling overhead KB press | 2 × 5 reps/side | 8 kg KB | Hip flexor stretch (rear leg) + overhead shoulder mobility — paragliding brake control pattern |

**Phase 4 — Static stretching with longer holds (10 min).** Accumulates chronic ROM volume. Per Ingram et al. (2024), target ~4 min per muscle group per session. Moderate intensity (stretch sensation, not pain).

| Exercise | Prescription | Target |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| Couch stretch (rear foot elevated against wall) | 2 × 45s each side | Deep hip flexor/quad stretch — work toward upright torso |
| Standing calf stretch (straight leg + bent knee positions) | 2 × 30s each position, each side | Gastrocnemius (straight knee) + soleus (bent knee) for ankle DF |
| Pigeon pose (or supine figure-4 if pigeon uncomfortable) | 1 × 60s each side | Deep hip ER capsular stretch |
| Supine thoracic extension on foam roller, arms overhead in Y | 2 × 45s | T-spine extension, pec minor stretch, counteracts pack/harness posture |
| Doorway pec stretch | 1 × 45s each side | Counteracts protracted shoulder posture from desk, harness |

**Phase 5 — Sport-specific integration (5 min).** Links mobility to functional patterns relevant to ski touring and hike-and-fly paragliding.

| Exercise | Prescription | Sport relevance |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Touring stride simulation (alternating deep lunges with arm drive + overhead reach) | 2 × 8 strides | Mimics uphill touring: deep hip flexion, hip flexor stretch on rear leg, pole-plant arm pattern |
| Overhead band distraction with small circles | 2 × 30s each side | Paragliding: band anchored behind at waist height, loop around upper arm, step forward for traction, perform small circles. Opens subacromial space |
| Prone I-T-Y raises | 1 × 8 reps each position | Scapular stability for overhead endurance: lower trap, mid-trap, serratus anterior. Critical for brake toggle management and pole planting |

**Seasonal adjustments.** During ski touring season (November–April), add extra ankle and hip flexor emphasis to Protocol A and include half-kneeling ankle mobilization at the trailhead pre-tour (2 min). During hike-and-fly season (May–October), shift Protocol A emphasis toward thoracic rotation and shoulder work, and add 2 minutes of band-distracted shoulder mobilization to Protocol C. Pre-flight warm-up: wall slides + band pull-aparts (3 min scapular activation).

## §9.7 Injury prevention evidence specific to mountain descent and sport demands

**Core Principle.** The four highest-risk injury patterns for this athlete profile — ankle sprains on uneven terrain, patellofemoral/patellar tendon pain during descent, lower back pain from pack carriage, and shoulder overuse from paragliding — all have modifiable mobility-related risk factors. Ankle dorsiflexion restriction is the single most consequential deficit, linking directly to ankle instability, dynamic knee valgus, and patellofemoral pain. Thoracic mobility protects the lumbar spine during loaded carriage. Rotator cuff prehab addresses the specific demands of sustained overhead arm positioning in paragliding.

**Evidence Summary.** Decreased ankle dorsiflexion ROM is a hallmark deficit in chronic ankle instability (CAI; Martin et al. 2021, JOSPT Clinical Practice Guideline). Ankle dorsiflexion predicts anterior reach on the Star Excursion Balance Test, a validated measure of dynamic balance relevant to uneven terrain navigation (Hoch et al. 2012). Restoring dorsiflexion after sprains is critical to minimizing reinjury, with ankle sprain recurrence rates reaching **80%** in some populations (Green et al. 2002). Static stretching combined with home exercise showed the strongest post-sprain effect (Cohen d = 1.06; Hoch et al. 2012). Critically, residual posterior talar glide restriction persists after sprains even when gross ROM appears restored (Green et al. 2002), necessitating joint mobilization as part of rehabilitation. Backpack loads increase mediolateral instability on uneven terrain (Lee et al. 2017), compounding sprain risk for loaded mountain athletes. Neuromuscular training and proprioceptive exercises on unstable surfaces significantly reduce recurrence (Martin et al. 2021).

The link between ankle dorsiflexion and knee pain is well-established. A meta-analysis by Oliveira et al. (2017) found restricted ankle dorsiflexion significantly associated with dynamic knee valgus (DKV): **SMD = −0.65** (95% CI −0.88 to −0.41) across 17 studies, increasing to SMD = −1.25 in weight-bearing measurements. Limited dorsiflexion forces decreased knee flexion during descent, compensatory frontal-plane motion, and increased patellofemoral contact stress (Rabin et al. 2016; Mason et al. 2022). Backman & Danielson (2011) demonstrated prospectively that low ankle dorsiflexion predisposes to patellar tendinopathy over a one-year follow-up in athletes. Patellofemoral pain syndrome is also associated with decreased hip abductor and external rotator strength (Willy & Davis 2011), confirming the need to address both the ankle and hip as linked contributors to DKV. Downhill hiking generates **3–7× bodyweight eccentric forces** per step, with 75% of all hiking accidents occurring during descent (Faulhaber et al. 2020). Hiking poles reduce compressive knee forces by approximately 25%.

Lower back pain is the most common spinal disorder in military load-carrying populations, with spinal injuries representing 48.5% of military injuries (Hauret et al. meta-analysis). Increased trunk flexion is the primary compensation for heavy loads, generating connective tissue strain and altered spinal curvature (Liew et al. 2016). Intervertebral disc height decreases after load carriage training (Military Medical Research 2020). A stiff thoracic spine forces lumbar compensation under load — this theoretical model is supported by multiple clinical sources (Neumann 2010) and reinforced by research showing reduced thoracic rotation under load alters gait coordination. Hip belt use preserves pelvic-thoracic coordination and is recommended with all loads >15% bodyweight. Pack recommendations from the literature include high thoracic-level load placement and short, non-flexible shoulder straps (PMC9180465 review). Core stability training is an essential complement to thoracic mobility for spinal protection.

Paragliding injury epidemiology shows upper extremity involvement in 13–31% of injuries (Canbek et al. 2015), with landing phase accounting for 71% of injuries and take-off 11% (Schulze et al. 1991). The most common overuse injury is **supraspinatus tendonitis** from repetitive overhead loading in a narrow subacromial space (Clifford, Cross Country Magazine). The mechanism is exacerbated by forward shoulder posture, continuous arm elevation during active flying in turbulence, and the protracted-scapulae position induced by harness wear. Hike-and-fly combines heavy pack carriage (promoting kyphosis and protraction) with immediate overhead shoulder demands (paragliding), making this a uniquely high-risk combination. Rotator cuff prehab should target serratus anterior (push-up plus variations), middle and lower trapezius (Y-T-W raises), and external rotators (Cools et al. 2018). Harness adjustment for upright posture opens the subacromial space.

**Application Rule.** Implement the following targeted prehab strategies alongside the mobility protocols in §9.6:

- **Ankle sprain prevention:** Maintain WBLT ≥10 cm bilaterally. If history of ankle sprain, add posterior talar glide mobilization (Mulligan technique) + progressive single-leg balance training on unstable surfaces (2 × 30s each leg, 3×/week). Post-sprain protocol: static stretching + home exercise (d = 1.06 effect) + joint mobilization targeting posterior talar glide restriction.
- **Knee pain prevention:** Screen both ankle DF (WBLT) and hip ER/abduction strength. Address any WBLT <10 cm or DKV observed on single-leg squat. Eccentric quadriceps training (slow step-downs, split squats with controlled descent) is the primary prehab: begin 3–9 months before descent-heavy season. Use hiking poles during sustained descents to reduce compressive forces ~25%.
- **Lower back protection:** Thoracic extension and rotation mobility work as per Protocols A and C. Always use a hip belt with the 15–20 kg pack. Load carriage conditioning: 1 session every 10–14 days with progressive overload on speed, grade, and distance before increasing pack weight (Orr et al. 2021). Core stability training (anti-extension, anti-rotation, anti-lateral-flexion) in the Thursday dedicated session.
- **Shoulder injury prevention:** Year-round rotator cuff prehab: serratus anterior (push-up plus), mid/lower trapezius (prone I-T-Y raises), external rotation strengthening (band ER at 0° and 90° abduction). Pre-flight warm-up: wall slides + band pull-aparts (3 min). Adjust harness for upright posture to maintain subacromial space. During turbulence-heavy flying conditions, schedule post-flight shoulder mobility (band-distracted overhead circles, doorway pec stretch).
- **General eccentric resilience:** Alfredson protocol for calf strengthening (3 × 15 eccentric heel raises twice daily), Nordic hamstring curls for posterior chain, and slow eccentric split squats. Begin eccentric programs 3–9 months before season onset.

## Conclusion: integrating mobility into the mountain athlete system

This domain establishes that mobility work for mountain athletes is neither optional nor unlimited — it operates within defined dose-response boundaries that must be respected to avoid both under- and over-investment. Three findings reshape how this athlete should approach flexibility. First, **full-ROM resistance training is as effective as dedicated stretching for ROM maintenance**, which means the 2–3 weekly gym sessions already provide substantial mobility stimulus when exercises are performed through complete ranges with controlled tempos. Second, **chronic flexibility gains plateau at just 10 minutes per week per muscle group**, setting a clear ceiling on useful stretching volume. Third, the **four-region priority hierarchy** — ankle dorsiflexion, hip flexor length, thoracic extension/rotation, and hip external rotation — captures the vast majority of injury risk and performance limitation for ski touring, splitboarding, and hike-and-fly paragliding.

The practical system is deliberately minimal: a non-negotiable 12-minute daily routine, movement-specific dynamic warm-ups before each gym session, and one comprehensive 40–45 minute dedicated session per week in the existing Thursday slot. This totals approximately 100–120 minutes of weekly mobility investment without adding a single additional training session. Deload weeks serve as the primary windows for ROM development; in-season weeks and caloric deficit phases call for maintenance-only intensity. The loaded stretching exercises — prying goblet squats, deficit reverse lunges, tempo RDLs, half-kneeling overhead presses — are the system's most efficient tools, simultaneously building strength, hypertrophy, and flexibility in positions directly relevant to mountain performance. This approach transforms mobility from an ad-hoc afterthought into a structured, evidence-dosed component of the training system.
