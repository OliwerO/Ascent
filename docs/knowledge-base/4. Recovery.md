# Recovery and readiness markers for AI-driven training programming

**The most actionable insight across all five domains is this: no single recovery marker reliably predicts readiness in isolation.** An AI coaching system must triangulate HRV trends, sleep metrics, subjective wellness, nutritional status, and programmed recovery to make sound training decisions. The evidence consistently shows that subjective measures are more sensitive to daily load fluctuations than any wearable metric,  yet HRV trends detect maladaptation weeks before athletes consciously perceive it.  This knowledge base provides the specific numbers, thresholds, and decision rules an AI system needs to navigate these signals in real time for athletes balancing strength training with mountain endurance sports.

-----

## 1 — HRV as a readiness marker

### Core principle

Heart rate variability—specifically the natural log of rMSSD (ln rMSSD)—is the single best objective marker of autonomic recovery status when tracked as a **7-day rolling average against an individual baseline**. Single-day readings are unreliable; weekly trends in both the mean and coefficient of variation (CV) reveal whether an athlete is adapting, coping, or sliding toward maladaptation. HRV-guided training produces equal or superior performance outcomes compared to pre-planned training  while requiring fewer high-intensity sessions.

### Evidence summary

**HRV-guided vs. pre-planned training.** Kiviniemi et al. (2007) randomized 26 males into HRV-guided and predefined training groups over 4 weeks. The HRV group improved VO₂peak from 56 to 60 mL/kg/min (p = 0.002); the predefined group showed no significant VO₂peak change.  Nuuttila et al. (2017) found significantly greater improvements in Vmax and countermovement jump in HRV-guided athletes over 8 weeks.   Kurtz et al. (2021) showed HRV-guided high-intensity functional training achieved equivalent strength gains (squat +14.2%, deadlift +12.6%) with **significantly fewer high-intensity training days** (mean difference: −13.56 days, p < 0.001).  The Manresa-Rocamora et al. (2021) meta-analysis reported a moderate effect favoring HRV-guided training for cardiac-vagal modulation (SMD = 0.50, 95% CI: 0.09–0.91)  and small positive effects for aerobic capacity and endurance performance.

**Why rMSSD, not SDNN.** rMSSD captures beat-to-beat (parasympathetic/vagal) variability and is reliable in recordings as short as **60 seconds** (Esco & Flatt, 2014). SDNN reflects total variability including sympathetic and circadian components and requires ≥5-minute recordings for stability. rMSSD has an ICC of **0.917**  versus ~0.57 for SDNN. The natural log transformation (ln rMSSD) normalizes the positively skewed distribution of raw rMSSD, enabling parametric analysis and more interpretable day-to-day comparisons.

**7-day rolling CV thresholds.** The coefficient of variation of daily ln rMSSD within a rolling 7-day window reflects autonomic stability.  Flatt’s research identifies **~7%** as a practical boundary:  CV below 3–4% indicates a stable, well-adapted state; CV of 4–7% reflects normal training variation; CV above 7–10% paired with a declining weekly mean signals maladaptation risk. Plews et al. (2012) demonstrated this in elite triathletes: the athlete who developed non-functional overreaching (NFOR) showed CV declining at −0.65%/week alongside declining ln rMSSD  (slope = −0.17 ms/week), while the healthy control remained stable.  

**Individual baselines trump population norms.** Plews et al. (2013, 2014) established that isolated single-day HRV scores cannot detect training responses, but weekly averaged data reveals clear adaptation patterns. A minimum of **3 valid data points per week** is required for meaningful assessment.   The smallest worthwhile change (SWC) should be calculated as the individual’s baseline mean ± 0.5 × individual SD.

**Strength vs. endurance HRV responses.** Hypertrophic loading (5 × 10 at 70% 1RM) acutely suppresses rMSSD by **~64%**; maximal strength loading (15 × 1 at 100% 1RM) suppresses it by **~29%**.  Recovery to baseline takes **24–72 hours** after heavy resistance training  versus hours to 24 hours after sub-threshold aerobic work. Training below LT1 tends to increase HRV; training above LT2 suppresses it for 12–48 hours. 

**Morning measurement protocol.** Measure immediately upon waking, in a consistent seated position, for a minimum of 60 seconds using a validated device (chest strap or validated PPG).  Natural breathing is acceptable for rMSSD. Discard recordings with >5% ectopic/corrected beats. For highly trained endurance athletes with very low resting heart rates, seated or standing position avoids parasympathetic saturation artifacts (Plews et al., 2013).

### Application guideline

The AI system should implement a traffic-light decision framework. **Green (train as planned):** daily ln rMSSD within individual SWC, weekly CV < 5–7%, weekly mean stable or rising. **Yellow (modify intensity):** daily ln rMSSD outside SWC for 1–2 days or weekly CV 7–10%; prescribe low-intensity endurance or technique work instead of planned high-intensity sessions. **Red (recovery priority):** daily ln rMSSD below SWC for ≥3 consecutive days, weekly CV > 10% with declining mean, or weekly mean declining for ≥2 weeks; prescribe rest, mobility, or very light Zone 1 aerobic work. After heavy resistance training, expect HRV suppression for 24–72 hours—do not flag this as a readiness concern unless suppression persists beyond 72 hours. Always combine HRV with subjective wellness data; HRV cannot detect muscular soreness or mental fatigue.

## 2 — Sleep and performance

### Core principle

Sleep is the single most powerful recovery modality available. Below **7 hours**, measurable decrements emerge across hormonal profiles, muscle protein synthesis, strength endurance, and cognitive function. Athletes should target **8–9 hours** as a minimum during training phases, with sleep extension to 9–10 hours producing significant, measurable performance gains. For mountain athletes operating at **2,000–4,000 m**, altitude-induced sleep disruption is an unavoidable recovery cost that must be factored into load management.

### Evidence summary

**Dose-response relationship.** One week of 5 hours/night reduces daytime testosterone by **10–15%**, equivalent to 10–15 years of aging (Leproult & Van Cauter, 2011, *JAMA*). A single night of total sleep deprivation decreases testosterone by **24%**, increases cortisol by **21%**, and reduces muscle protein synthesis by **18%** (Lamon et al., 2021). Growth hormone, with 70–80% of daily secretion occurring during deep/slow-wave sleep, is directly impaired by sleep restriction. Thirty hours of sleep deprivation reduces pre-exercise muscle glycogen by **~33%** (Skein et al., 2011). 

**Strength performance impact.** The Craven et al. (2022) meta-analysis of 69 publications found an overall performance decrement of **−7.56%** (95% CI: −11.9 to −3.13) from sleep loss ≤6 hours.  Maximal strength decreases by **~3%** on average, while strength endurance drops by **~10%** and skill-based performance by **~21%**. Performance degrades approximately **0.4% per additional hour spent awake**.  Compound movements (squats, deadlifts) are more vulnerable than isolation exercises.  A single night of poor sleep largely preserves maximal single-effort strength, but 2+ nights of restriction produce reliable decrements.

**Endurance performance impact.** Time to exhaustion at 80% VO₂max drops by **11%** after 36 hours without sleep (Martin, 1981). Self-paced running distance decreases **~6%** after a single night of 4-hour sleep (Temesi et al., 2020). RPE is consistently elevated during exercise after all forms of sleep loss,  causing athletes to voluntarily reduce pacing— particularly relevant for long mountain days where pacing discipline matters.

**Duration thresholds.** Athletes sleeping **< 8 hours/night are 1.7× more likely** to sustain injury (Milewski et al., 2014, n = 112 adolescent athletes).  Von Rosen et al. (2017) found athletes averaging > 8 hours had **61% lower odds of new injury**.  The average elite athlete sleeps only 6.5–7 hours by actigraphy (Leeder et al., 2012), with sleep efficiency of just **80.6%** versus 88.7% in controls.

**Sleep quality benchmarks.** Based on elite athlete polysomnography data (Knufinke et al., 2018, n = 98): deep sleep **21 ± 8%**, REM **27 ± 7%**, sleep efficiency **88 ± 5%**. Functional benchmarks for the AI system: sleep efficiency > 85% (good), 80–85% (acceptable), < 80% (poor); deep sleep > 20% (good), 15–20% (acceptable), < 15% (poor); sleep onset latency < 15 min (good), > 30 min (poor).

**Sleep extension.** Mah et al. (2011) extended Stanford basketball players’ sleep from ~6.7 to ~8.5 hours over 5–7 weeks. Results: 282-ft sprint improved by **0.7 seconds (4.3%)**, free throw accuracy improved by **9%**, 3-point accuracy by **9.2%**, reaction time improved significantly, and self-rated performance jumped from 6.9 to 8.8/10.   Cunha et al. (2023) systematic review found extending sleep by **46–113 minutes** in athletes habitually sleeping ~7 hours is a general recommendation, supplemented by 20–90 minute naps to restore partial restriction effects. 

**Altitude disrupts sleep profoundly.** Sleep disturbance begins above **2,000 m** with the onset of periodic breathing. At 2,500–3,500 m, approximately 25% of sojourners develop central sleep apnea, deep sleep is markedly reduced, and nocturnal hypoxemia is significant. At 3,500–4,500 m, deep sleep is severely reduced, REM may be absent on the first night, and sleep fragmentation is near-universal (Bloch et al., 2012; Patrician, 2024). Sleep quality improves over 2–3 nights with acclimatization, but periodic breathing persists. The AI system should assume a **20–30% reduction in effective sleep quality** for any night spent above 2,500 m, regardless of reported duration.

### Application guideline

The AI system should enforce these sleep duration rules: **< 6 hours = red alert** (reduce next-day training volume by ≥50%, eliminate high-intensity work); **6–7 hours = yellow alert** (reduce intensity, favor aerobic base work); **7–8 hours = green** (adequate for most training); **8–9+ hours = optimal** (encourage during heavy training blocks). During periods of altitude exposure (hut nights, multi-day tours), automatically extend the next recovery window by 24–48 hours and reduce planned training load by 20–30%. When 3+ consecutive nights fall below 6 hours, recommend an unplanned deload regardless of other markers. Deep sleep percentage below 15% for 3+ nights should trigger a flag to investigate sleep hygiene, stress, or altitude effects.

## 3 — Subjective readiness markers

### Core principle

Subjective wellness measures—fatigue, soreness, sleep quality, stress, mood—are **more sensitive and consistent** than any objective marker for detecting daily training load responses  and predicting injury risk. The landmark Saw et al. (2016) systematic review of 56 studies concluded that “subjective self-reported measures trump commonly used objective measures.”  Device-based composite scores (Garmin Body Battery, Training Readiness) provide useful directional data but have no published independent validation and systematically miss muscular soreness and mental fatigue.  An AI coaching system must prioritize subjective input while using objective metrics as complementary early-warning signals.

### Evidence summary

**Garmin Body Battery** uses the Firstbeat Analytics engine to produce a 5–100 score based on rMSSD-derived stress/recovery balance, activity drain, and sleep recharge. Score zones: **76–100** (high energy), **26–75** (moderate), **5–25** (depleted). No independent validation study has been published for the composite score.  A 2025 study on Dutch police officers found Body Battery did not consistently align with self-reported recovery experiences (de Vries et al., 2025, *JMIR mHealth*). Doherty et al. (2025) evaluated 14 composite health scores across 10 wearable manufacturers and found no manufacturer disclosed algorithmic weights and none validated scores against clinical outcomes.  

**Garmin Training Readiness** (0–100 scale) combines six inputs: sleep score, HRV status versus 60-day baseline, recovery time (EPOC-based), acute training load, stress history, and sleep history.  Score zones: **60–100 green** (ready for hard work), **40–59 yellow** (consider easier training), **< 40 red** (prioritize recovery). Its critical blind spot is **strength training**: heavy resistance sessions are poorly captured, and the score systematically underestimates neuromuscular fatigue from lifting.  It does not incorporate subjective RPE despite Garmin collecting it.

**The Hooper Index** (Hooper & Mackinnon, 1995) is a 4-item questionnaire—sleep quality, stress, fatigue, and DOMS—each rated 1–7, summed for a composite of 4–28 (lower = better). Rabbani et al. (2019) found the Hooper Index had a **lower typical error/SWC ratio (3.1 vs. 4.4)** and **higher signal-to-noise ratio (5.5 vs. 1.5)** compared to HRV for monitoring match-induced fatigue.  Thorpe et al. (2015, 2017) found perceived fatigue and soreness were “clearly more sensitive than ln rMSSD to daily fluctuations in training load” in elite Premier League players.  Very large correlations (r = 0.72–0.89) exist between changes in wellness measures and changes in training load.

**REST-Q Sport** (Kellmann & Kallus, 2001) provides a deeper 36–76 item assessment across 12 stress and recovery subscales with a 3-day retrospective window. It is too long for daily use (5–10 minutes) but valuable as a **weekly or bi-weekly diagnostic** tool, particularly when daily wellness scores flag emerging problems. 

**When signals disagree.** HRV and subjective measures often do not correlate because they capture different constructs. When HRV shows recovery but the athlete feels fatigued, **trust the subjective report**—muscular damage and mental fatigue fall outside autonomic monitoring. When HRV is suppressed but the athlete feels fine, proceed cautiously: chronic HRV suppression (multiple days below baseline) may be an early warning sign that precedes conscious awareness.  Psychological symptoms of overtraining (mood disturbance, motivation loss, sleep disruption) typically appear **days to weeks before** physiological markers or performance decrements (Meeusen et al., 2013, ECSS/ACSM consensus).

**Injury prediction.** Hamlin et al. (2019) tracked 182 athletes over 4 years and found decreased mood (OR = 0.89), decreased sleep duration (OR = 0.94), and increased academic stress (OR = 0.91) predicted injury.  Laux et al. (2015) showed the greatest injury risk emerged from simultaneous training load increase and sleep duration decrease.  UEFA elite clubs rated wellness questionnaires among the **top 3 monitoring tools** for evaluating injury risk (McCall et al., 2016). 

### Application guideline

The AI system should implement a **4-item daily wellness check** (fatigue, soreness, sleep quality, stress on a 1–5 scale) administered each morning, taking < 30 seconds. All inputs should be normalized to individual Z-scores against rolling 7–14-day baselines.  The composite readiness score should weight inputs in three tiers. **Tier 1 (highest weight):** subjective fatigue, sleep quality + duration, muscle soreness. **Tier 2 (moderate weight):** HRV status relative to baseline, perceived stress. **Tier 3 (contextual modifiers):** Garmin Body Battery/Training Readiness, acute:chronic workload ratio, days since last high-intensity session, mood/motivation. When subjective and objective signals conflict, apply the **conservative rule**: default to the worse signal for high-intensity session decisions. Readiness must be interpreted relative to the planned session—a moderate score may permit easy aerobic work but not maximal strength testing.  Flag athletes whose rolling 7-day wellness scores decline > 1 SD below personal baseline, and when subjective fatigue scores remain elevated for > 3 consecutive days, automatically suggest volume/intensity reduction.

## 4 — Nutrition for recovery

### Core principle

For concurrent strength and mountain endurance athletes, protein intake of **1.6–2.2 g/kg/day** distributed across ≥4 meals at **0.4–0.55 g/kg per meal** maximizes muscle protein synthesis. Carbohydrate needs scale dramatically with training volume, from 5 g/kg/day on light days up to 8–12 g/kg/day during long mountain objectives. Altitude exposure above 2,000 m increases caloric expenditure, fluid losses, iron demands, and preferential carbohydrate utilization—all factors the AI system must adjust for automatically.

### Evidence summary

**Protein dosing.** Morton et al. (2018, *Br J Sports Med*) meta-analyzed 49 RCTs (n = 1,863) and identified **1.62 g/kg/day** as the inflection point beyond which additional protein produces no further fat-free mass gains from resistance training, with an upper 95% confidence interval of **~2.2 g/kg/day**.  Schoenfeld & Aragon (2018, *JISSN*) translated this into per-meal targets: **0.4 g/kg/meal** across 4 meals reaches the 1.6 g/kg minimum; **0.55 g/kg/meal** across 4 meals reaches the 2.2 g/kg upper bound.   For a 75 kg athlete, this means **30–41 g protein per meal** across 4 daily feedings. The leucine threshold for mTOR activation and MPS initiation is **~2.5 g** in young adults (Witard et al., 2014), corresponding to approximately 20–25 g of high-quality protein.  A pre-sleep bolus of **40 g casein** increases overnight MPS (Res et al., 2012; Snijders et al., 2015). 

**MPS refractory period.** After protein ingestion, MPS peaks at ~90–120 minutes and returns to baseline by ~180 minutes despite sustained amino acid availability  (Atherton & Smith, 2012). This “muscle full” effect is attenuated by resistance exercise, which extends muscle sensitivity to protein for **≥24 hours** (Burd et al., 2011).  Practical implication: space protein feedings **3–5 hours apart**. Areta et al. (2013) demonstrated that 4 × 20 g protein over 12 hours produced greater MPS than 2 × 40 g or 8 × 10 g.

**Carbohydrate needs by training context.** Light training days (30 min): **3–5 g/kg/day**. Moderate days (~1 hour strength or light touring): **5–7 g/kg/day**.  High-volume days (strength + 3–6 hour ski tour): **7–10 g/kg/day**. All-day mountain objectives (> 4 hours): **8–12 g/kg/day**  (Burke et al., 2001; Thomas et al., 2016 ACSM/AND/DC position statement). At altitude, preferential carbohydrate oxidation shifts fuel utilization further toward glycolytic substrates—one study showed elite athletes increased CHO intake from 6.5 to **9.3 g/kg/day** at 2,320 m (Koivisto et al., 2020).

**Glycogen replenishment.** The rapid-resynthesis window opens immediately post-exercise: optimal intake is **1.0–1.2 g/kg/hour** of high-GI carbohydrate for the first 4 hours  (Burke, van Loon & Hawley, 2017). When carbohydrate intake falls below 0.8 g/kg/hour, adding **0.3–0.4 g/kg/hour protein** enhances glycogen storage.  Full replenishment requires **24–48 hours** with adequate daily carbohydrate intake. Even 2% bodyweight dehydration can reduce glycogen storage by **25–30%**.

**Altitude-specific nutrition (2,000–4,000 m).** Basal metabolic rate increases during acclimatization. Athletes should add **200–600+ kcal/day** depending on altitude and activity level. Fluid needs rise to **3–5 L/day**  due to lower humidity, increased respiratory losses, and hypoxic diuresis—thirst is unreliable at altitude. Iron status is critical: altitude exposure drives a **3–5-fold increase in erythropoiesis**.  Ferritin should be screened 8–10 weeks before altitude training with a target of **> 50 µg/L** (IOC recommendation).  Athletes with ferritin < 35 µg/L should supplement with **210 mg elemental iron/day**; those at 35–100 µg/L need **105 mg/day** (Govus et al., 2015).   Energy availability must not drop below **30 kcal/kg FFM/day** (RED-S threshold), which is easily breached when appetite suppression at altitude meets increased expenditure. 

**Key micronutrients.** Vitamin D: target serum 25(OH)D of **> 40 ng/mL** for athletes,  with supplementation of 2,000–5,000 IU/day depending on status (Close et al., 2013). Deficient athletes (< 20 ng/mL) need 5,000–7,000 IU/day for 8 weeks. Winter mountain athletes are at high risk due to cold-weather clothing coverage. Magnesium: athletes require **10–20% above the RDA** (aim for 400–420 mg/day for men via food + 200–400 mg supplemental magnesium glycinate before bed). Magnesium activates GABA, lowers cortisol, and promotes deeper slow-wave sleep. Omega-3 fatty acids: **2–3 g/day combined EPA + DHA** reduces pro-inflammatory markers, attenuates muscle damage indices  (CK, LDH), and supports recovery from the eccentric loading inherent in mountain descents. Omega-3 index target is **> 8%**,  requiring ~13 weeks at 2 g/day to achieve (Walker et al., 2019). Effects take ≥2 weeks to manifest due to membrane incorporation. 

### Application guideline

The AI system should calculate daily macronutrient targets dynamically based on planned training volume. On rest days, set protein at 1.6 g/kg and carbohydrate at 3–5 g/kg. On moderate training days, shift to 1.8 g/kg protein and 5–7 g/kg carbohydrate. On high-volume mountain days, push to 2.0–2.2 g/kg protein and 8–10 g/kg carbohydrate. Distribute protein across ≥4 meals spaced 3–5 hours apart, with a 40 g slow-digesting protein dose before sleep. When altitude exposure is detected (GPS data or user input > 2,000 m), automatically add 200–600 kcal/day, increase fluid target to 3–5 L, increase carbohydrate allocation by 1–2 g/kg, and flag iron status check if not completed within the previous 8 weeks. Include a daily supplement recommendation: vitamin D 2,000–5,000 IU with a fat-containing meal, magnesium glycinate 200–400 mg before bed, and omega-3s 2–3 g/day split with meals.

## 5 — Deload and recovery weeks

### Core principle

Planned recovery weeks are not optional luxuries—they are the mechanism by which cumulative fatigue dissipates and supercompensation occurs. For concurrent strength and mountain endurance athletes, the evidence supports deloading every **3–4 weeks** with a **40–50% reduction in volume** while maintaining intensity at ≥85% of normal working loads. The optimal strategy is reducing volume, not intensity, because neuromuscular adaptations and aerobic endurance have residual training effects of **~30 days** (Issurin, 2008)—a 1-week deload will not meaningfully erode these qualities.

### Evidence summary

**Supercompensation physiology.** First formalized by Yakovlev (1949–1959) and grounded in Selye’s General Adaptation Syndrome (1956), the supercompensation model describes four phases: training stimulus → fatigue/recovery → supercompensation above baseline → detraining. Zatsiorsky’s complementary **fitness-fatigue (two-factor) model** refines this: the fitness effect is slow-changing and long-lasting; the fatigue effect is shorter but of greater magnitude. Training readiness = fitness minus fatigue. The practical implication is that deloads allow fatigue to dissipate while fitness remains elevated, producing a net performance peak.

**Deload frequency.** Bell et al. (2024, *Sports Medicine—Open*) surveyed 246 competitive strength/physique athletes (mean 8.2 ± 6.2 years training) and found an average deload frequency of every **5.6 ± 2.3 weeks** with a duration of **6.4 ± 1.7 days**. The Delphi consensus (Bell et al., 2023) recommends every 4–8 weeks, with coaches favoring a flexible approach combining pre-planned checkpoints with autoregulated reactive deloading. For concurrent training athletes managing two fatigue streams simultaneously, reduce the interval by 1–2 weeks. Recommended frequencies by training level: beginners (< 1 year) every 8–12 weeks, intermediate (1–3 years) every 6–8 weeks, advanced (3+ years) every 4–6 weeks, concurrent strength + endurance every 3–4 weeks, athletes in caloric deficit subtract 2 weeks from their standard interval.

**Deload structure.** The Bosquet et al. (2007) meta-analysis of 27 tapering studies established the gold standard: **volume reduction of 41–60% without modifying intensity or frequency** over 1–2 weeks produced the largest effect size (0.72 ± 0.36, p < 0.001). Bell et al. (2025, *Strength and Conditioning Journal*) confirm that maintaining intensity ≥85% 1RM during volume reduction best preserves neuromuscular adaptations. When the athlete is genuinely depleted, a fatigue-driven deload reducing both volume (40–60%) and intensity (by 20–30%) is appropriate. Frequency should generally be maintained. Coleman et al. (2024) found that 1-week complete training cessation produced equivalent hypertrophy and power outcomes to continuous training but **inferior strength gains**—confirming that active deloads are superior to complete rest for strength maintenance.

**Issurin’s residual training effects** provide the scientific basis for deload safety: aerobic endurance persists **30 ± 5 days**, maximal strength persists **30 ± 5 days**, anaerobic glycolytic endurance **18 ± 4 days**, strength endurance **15 ± 5 days**, and maximal speed only **5 ± 3 days**. A 1-week deload therefore poses zero risk to aerobic or maximal strength qualities but may require minimal speed/power stimulus maintenance.

**Reactive deload triggers.** No single diagnostic marker reliably indicates overtraining (Meeusen et al., 2013 ECSS/ACSM consensus). The evidence supports a multi-signal convergence approach. Key indicators: HRV (ln rMSSD) declining below 7-day rolling average for > 3 days; resting heart rate elevated ≥ 3–5 bpm above baseline for ≥ 3 days; sleep efficiency dropping below 85% for > 3 days; missed reps or RPE elevated ≥ 1.5 points versus expected for > 2 sessions; persistent soreness lasting > 72 hours; self-reported motivation < 3/5 for ≥ 3 days; elevated life stress. When **≥ 3 of these indicators converge**, the system should recommend an unplanned deload.

**Concurrent training deloads.** Both strength and endurance modalities should be deloaded in the same week. Deloading only one modality while pushing the other negates the recovery benefit. For strength: maintain exercises and loads, reduce sets by 40–50%, increase RIR by 2–3. For endurance: reduce volume (distance/vertical) by 40–60%, maintain Zone 1–2 intensity, eliminate intervals and heavy pack carries. Total weekly training stress (TRIMP/TSS) should drop to ~50% of normal.

**Mountain-specific recovery weeks.** The Uphill Athlete model (Steve House, Scott Johnston) programs recovery weeks every 3–4 weeks, reducing volume ~50% while maintaining easy aerobic base work. Muscular endurance (ME) workouts are eliminated entirely during recovery weeks due to their very high recovery demands. A practical template for mountain + strength athletes: 2 light gym sessions at 50% volume with maintained load, 1–2 easy Zone 1–2 aerobic sessions (30–45 minutes, flat or gentle terrain, no pack weight), 1–2 complete rest or mobility/yoga days. Total vertical gain and distance should be ~40–50% of normal. No intervals, no heavy pack carries, no ME circuits.

### Application guideline

The AI system should implement both scheduled and reactive deloading. **Scheduled:** default to every 4 weeks for concurrent training athletes (3:1 load:deload ratio), every 6 weeks for strength-only beginners, adjusting for age (> 40: subtract 1 week) and caloric deficit (subtract 2 weeks). **Reactive:** maintain a running trigger count across the 8 indicators listed above; when ≥ 3 converge, override the schedule and insert an immediate deload. During deload weeks, reduce strength volume by 40–50% while maintaining load intensity ≥ 85% of recent working weights, reduce endurance volume by 40–60% while maintaining Zone 1–2 only, eliminate all high-intensity interval sessions, deload both modalities simultaneously, and maintain training frequency. Post-deload, resume at previous intensity but ~90% of pre-deload volume in the first week back before building again. For mountain objectives, the final 1–2 weeks before a major goal (multi-day tour, expedition departure) should follow the taper protocol: reduce volume 41–60%, maintain intensity, remove ME work for the last 2 weeks.

## Conclusion: building the decision engine

The five domains converge into a unified readiness assessment framework. **HRV trends (7-day rolling ln rMSSD mean and CV) serve as the physiological backbone**, detecting autonomic perturbation days before conscious fatigue or performance loss. Sleep functions as the master recovery switch—below 7 hours, every other recovery process degrades measurably. Subjective wellness (a 4-item daily check taking 30 seconds) is the most sensitive daily signal and the only reliable detector of muscular fatigue and psychological strain. Nutrition provides the biochemical substrate without which adaptation cannot occur—protein distribution, carbohydrate periodization, and altitude-specific adjustments are non-negotiable for concurrent training athletes. Scheduled deloads every 3–4 weeks with 40–50% volume reduction prevent the fatigue accumulation that, left unchecked, leads to overreaching and injury.

The most important implementation principle is **multi-signal convergence**: never make a binary train/rest decision from a single metric. When ≥3 readiness indicators are simultaneously degraded—HRV suppressed, sleep poor, subjective fatigue elevated—the system should intervene with load reduction regardless of the programmed plan. When signals conflict, trust subjective reports for daily decisions and HRV trends for longitudinal trajectory. This approach mirrors the evidence: the athletes who avoid injury and sustain long-term progression are those whose training is responsive to their actual recovery state, not just their calendar.
