# Periodization knowledge base for a recreational mountain athlete

**This document provides evidence-based periodization guidance for an AI coaching system programming strength training and mountain endurance for a recreational athlete based in Innsbruck, Austria, who ski tours November–April, hikes and paraglides May–October, and strength trains year-round.** Each section follows a consistent structure: core principle, evidence summary with specific numbers, application guidelines for automated plan generation, and key sources. All thresholds and ranges are drawn from peer-reviewed research and established sports science textbooks.

-----

## 1. Annual planning (macrocycle): structuring a dual-season year

### Core principle

The annual plan follows a **modified bi-cycle model** — two distinct macrocycles (winter/ski touring and summer/hike-and-fly), each containing general preparation, specific preparation, competition/performance, and transition phases. Volume and intensity maintain an inverse relationship across phases: high-volume/low-intensity preparation gradually shifts to low-volume/high-intensity performance.  Endurance training follows a **polarized intensity distribution** (~80% Zone 1 / ~20% Zone 3, minimizing Zone 2), while strength training sequences through accumulation → intensification → maintenance across each macrocycle.

### Evidence summary

**Classic periodization structure (Matveyev, 1977; Bompa & Buzzichelli, 2019):** The preparation phase occupies **60–75%** of each macrocycle, the competition/performance phase **20–30%**, and the transition phase **3–5 weeks**. Within preparation, the general sub-phase (GPP) takes >50% of that time, with the specific sub-phase (SPP) filling the remainder.  Volume decreases ~32 ± 15% from GPP to the peaking phase.  Bompa’s six strength phases — anatomical adaptation → hypertrophy → maximum strength → sport-specific conversion → maintenance → taper — provide the year-round strength backbone.  

**Block periodization (Issurin, 2008, 2010):** Concentrated 2–4 week mesocycle-blocks focusing on minimal fitness qualities sequentially.  The accumulation → transmutation → realization (A-T-R) sequence  forms a “mini annual plan” that can repeat multiple times per year.   Residual training effects govern sequencing:  **aerobic endurance persists ~30 ± 5 days**, **maximal strength ~30 ± 5 days**,  anaerobic glycolytic endurance ~18 ± 4 days, strength endurance ~15 ± 5 days, and maximal speed only ~5 ± 3 days.  These residuals mean aerobic and strength qualities can be “banked” and maintained with minimal dosing while other qualities are developed.  

**Polarized training model (Seiler, 2010; Stöggl & Sperlich, 2014):** In a three-zone model (Zone 1 below LT1, Zone 2 between LT1–LT2, Zone 3 above LT2), the optimal distribution is **~75–80% Zone 1, ~5% Zone 2, ~15–20% Zone 3**.  An RCT by Stöggl & Sperlich (2014) found polarized training produced the greatest improvements in VO₂max, peak power, and lactate threshold compared to threshold-heavy or high-intensity-only approaches.  **Two high-intensity sessions per week** typically suffice for recreational athletes.   Seiler’s hierarchy ranks total training volume as the single most important factor, followed by intensity distribution, then periodization details. 

**Undulating periodization:** Meta-analyses (Harries et al., 2015; Grgic et al., 2017) show **no significant difference** between linear, undulating, and block periodization for strength or hypertrophy when volume is equated  (pooled Cohen’s d = −0.02 for hypertrophy). Daily undulating periodization (DUP) is useful for maintaining multiple qualities during in-season phases.  

### Application guideline

The AI coach should structure the annual calendar as follows:

|Month  |Phase                    |Strength emphasis                         |Endurance emphasis                               |
|-------|-------------------------|------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
|May    |Transition (2–3 wk)      |Active recovery, mobility, light general  |Easy aerobic, unstructured                       |
|Jun–Jul|Summer GPP (8 wk)        |Hypertrophy → max strength (3×/wk)        |Aerobic base: high volume Z1–2 hiking/cycling    |
|Aug–Sep|Summer SPP (6–8 wk)      |Muscular endurance + maintenance (2×/wk)  |Threshold work, weighted hikes, hike-and-fly days|
|Oct    |Transition (2–3 wk)      |Deload → begin ski-specific prep          |Brief recovery, begin eccentric leg emphasis     |
|Nov–Dec|Winter GPP (8 wk)        |Max strength (2–3×/wk), eccentric leg work|Aerobic base via ski touring, Z1–2               |
|Jan–Feb|Winter SPP (6–8 wk)      |Maintenance (1–2×/wk)                     |Peak touring volume, muscular endurance          |
|Mar–Apr|Performance + late season|Maintenance (1–2×/wk)                     |Taper before big objectives, touring IS training |

**Priority-shifting rule:** When endurance is the primary goal (in-season), reduce strength to **1–2 maintenance sessions per week** at 75–85% 1RM, 2–3 sets × 3–6 reps. The 30-day residual for maximal strength means this minimal dose preserves gains.  When strength is prioritized (off-season GPP), limit high-intensity endurance to **2 sessions per week** and keep remaining endurance at Zone 1. Program recovery weeks every 3rd or 4th week, reducing volume ~40–50%.  For athletes over 40 or under high life stress, default to 2:1 loading (2 weeks hard, 1 week recovery).

## 2. Mesocycle design: block lengths, phases, and loading parameters

### Core principle

Each mesocycle follows the **accumulation → intensification → realization** (A-T-R) sequence, whether targeting strength or endurance. Accumulation blocks build work capacity through high volume at moderate intensity; intensification blocks convert that capacity into sport-specific fitness through reduced volume at higher intensity; realization blocks shed fatigue to express peak performance.  Block duration typically ranges from **3–6 weeks**,  with a 3+1 (3 weeks loading, 1 week deload) structure as the evidence-based default. 

### Evidence summary

**Strength training parameters by phase:**

|Phase                              |Sets × Reps |Load (%1RM)                     |Rest   |Weekly sets/muscle|Duration          |
|-----------------------------------|------------|--------------------------------|-------|------------------|------------------|
|Anatomical adaptation              |2–3 × 12–15 |40–60%                          |60–90s |8–12              |4–6 wk            |
|Hypertrophy (accumulation)         |3–5 × 6–12  |67–85%                          |60–120s|12–20+            |3–6 wk            |
|Max strength (intensification)     |3–5 × 1–6   |80–95%                          |3–5 min|6–12              |3–4 wk            |
|Power (realization)                |3–5 × 1–5   |85–100% heavy / 30–60% explosive|3–5 min|4–8               |1–3 wk            |
|Muscular endurance (sport-specific)|2–3 × 15–30+|30–60%                          |<90s   |Variable          |4–8 wk            |
|Maintenance (in-season)            |2–3 × 3–6   |75–85%                          |2–3 min|4–8               |Duration of season|

These ranges come from the NSCA Essentials of Strength & Conditioning (4th ed.),  the ACSM Position Stand (Ratamess et al., 2009), and Schoenfeld et al.‘s re-examination of the repetition continuum (2021).  The key insight from Schoenfeld’s work is that hypertrophy occurs across a broader rep range (5–30 reps) than traditionally believed, provided sets approach failure  — but the **6–12 rep range remains the most efficient** for hypertrophy when programming multiple goals concurrently.

**Endurance training parameters by phase:**

|Phase                            |Duration|Z1–2 volume    |High-intensity sessions|Key sessions                                |
|---------------------------------|--------|---------------|-----------------------|--------------------------------------------|
|Base building (accumulation)     |8–16 wk |80–90% of total|0–1/wk                 |Long slow distance, easy touring/hiking     |
|Threshold/build (intensification)|4–8 wk  |75–80%         |2–3/wk                 |3–4 × 8–15 min at threshold, weighted uphill|
|Peak/realization                 |1–3 wk  |Reduced total  |Maintain some intensity|Race-specific intervals, short sharp efforts|

**Optimal block length evidence:** The 4-week mesocycle (3 loading + 1 deload) is the most widely used and studied structure.  Survey data from competitive strength athletes (Bell et al., 2024, *Sports Medicine — Open*) found an average deload frequency of every **5.6 ± 2.3 weeks**.   Block periodization studies (Rønnestad et al., 2012, 2014) in elite cyclists and cross-country skiers  used 4-week blocks and demonstrated superior VO₂max and power outcomes compared to traditional periodization. For recreational athletes under concurrent training stress, **shorter 3-week blocks (2+1)** may be preferable to prevent excessive fatigue accumulation.

**Deload parameters (Bell et al., 2025; Bell et al., 2023 Delphi consensus):** Duration averages **6.4 ± 1.7 days**.   Volume reduction should scale with recovery need: **25–45% for low**, **40–60% for moderate**, **60–90% for high** recovery need.  Intensity should be maintained or moderately reduced. Training frequency should be preserved.   Schoenfeld et al. (2024) confirmed that a 1-week deload after 4 weeks of high-volume training resulted in no loss of strength or muscle size.

**Residual training effects (Issurin, 2010)** dictate that aerobic endurance and maximal strength persist ~30 days after focused training ceases, enabling sequential block design.  Strength endurance (~15 days) and anaerobic capacity (~18 days) require more frequent stimulation.  

### Application guideline

**Default mesocycle template:** Use 4-week blocks (3+1) as the standard.  Switch to 3-week blocks (2+1) when the athlete is over 40, in a caloric deficit, under high work/life stress, or concurrently training high endurance volume. Use 5–6 week blocks only for novice trainees in a pure strength-focus phase.

**Sequencing rules for strength:** In an off-season GPP, run anatomical adaptation (4 wk) → hypertrophy (4–6 wk) → max strength (4 wk) → sport-specific conversion (4 wk).  During in-season, run a single maintenance block repeating until the season ends. For the mountain athlete’s muscular endurance phase (sport-specific conversion), program weighted box step-ups, loaded carries, and steep uphill hiking at 15–40% bodyweight, following the Uphill Athlete methodology. 

**Sequencing rules for endurance:** Base building should consume at least 8 weeks before threshold work is introduced.  Maintain the 80/20 intensity distribution even during intensification blocks — increase the proportion of high-intensity work to ~20% but never exceed this for sustained periods.  The AI should track weekly training stress and ensure progressive overload does not exceed **~10% volume increase per week** during accumulation.

**Deload decision:** Pre-plan a deload every 4th week, but allow autoregulation to override — if readiness markers are strong at week 3, continue loading and deload at week 5. If markers decline before week 3, insert an early deload. (See Section 5 for specific autoregulation thresholds.)

## 3. Microcycle templates: weekly structures for concurrent training

### Core principle

Concurrent strength and endurance training creates an interference effect — primarily affecting **power and explosive strength**  (effect size −0.28 to −0.55), with minimal impact on maximal strength or hypertrophy in recreational athletes. The interference is driven by molecular cross-talk (AMPK suppresses mTOR)  and residual fatigue.  Practical mitigation centers on three strategies: **separate sessions by ≥6 hours** (ideally 24), **perform strength before endurance** when on the same day,  and **favor cycling/skiing over running** as the endurance modality due to reduced eccentric muscle damage. 

### Evidence summary

**The interference effect is real but manageable.** Hickson’s original study (1980) showed concurrent training impaired strength gains  after week 7 — but used an extreme 11 sessions/week protocol.  Wilson et al.’s meta-analysis (2012, 21 studies, 422 effect sizes) found the interference was most pronounced for **power** (strength-only ES = 0.91 vs. concurrent ES = 0.55), driven by endurance frequency (r = −0.35) and duration (r = −0.29). Critically, **cycling did not cause significant strength decrements while running did**, attributable to eccentric muscle damage from running.   The most recent meta-analysis (Schumann et al., 2022, 43 studies, 1,090 subjects) confirmed that concurrent training **does not significantly reduce maximal strength** (SMD = −0.06, p = 0.45) or hypertrophy (SMD = −0.01, p = 0.92) but **does impair explosive strength** (SMD = −0.28, p = 0.007).   The explosive strength interference disappeared when sessions were separated by ≥3 hours. 

**Molecular mechanisms (Baar, 2014; Coffey & Hawley, 2017):** Endurance exercise activates AMPK, which inhibits mTOR-driven muscle protein synthesis.  AMPK returns to baseline within **1–3 hours** post-exercise, while mTOR remains elevated for **~18 hours** post-resistance training. High-intensity endurance and glycogen-depleted states cause the greatest AMPK activation. Low-intensity Zone 1–2 endurance causes minimal interference — and may even enhance the endurance response when combined with strength training.

**Session spacing (Robineau et al., 2016):** In 58 rugby players over 7 weeks, same-session concurrent training produced the worst outcomes for both strength and VO₂max. The **6-hour separation** group improved lower-body strength ~12.8% more than the same-session group. The **24-hour separation** group achieved the highest VO₂max gains and highest-velocity strength gains. Force production remains impaired for 6–8 hours after high-intensity endurance (Sporer & Wenger, 2003).

**Session ordering (Murlasits et al., 2018; Eddens et al., 2018):** Two independent meta-analyses confirmed that **strength → endurance** ordering is more effective for lower-body strength development. Aerobic capacity is unaffected by either order.  If the goal is endurance and strength equally, strength-first is slightly preferred.

**Training frequency for recreational athletes:** Schoenfeld et al. (2016) established that training each muscle group **≥2×/week** is superior to 1×/week for hypertrophy.  Grgic et al. (2018) showed effect sizes of 0.74 (1×/wk), 0.82 (2×/wk), and 0.93 (3×/wk)  — diminishing returns beyond twice weekly. For endurance, Wilson et al. (2012) found that limiting endurance to **≤3 sessions/week** minimizes interference.

## 4. Tapering for mountain objectives: peaking for multi-day efforts

### Core principle

A taper is a **progressive nonlinear reduction of training volume** while maintaining intensity and frequency, designed to shed accumulated fatigue and express fitness built during prior blocks. For mountain objectives, the critical distinction is between single-day events and **multi-day objectives** (touring trips, traverses) — the latter require sustained performance over consecutive days rather than a single peak, demanding a modified taper that preserves glycogen stores, maintains terrain-specific movement patterns, and prioritizes arriving well-rested over arriving maximally sharp.

### Evidence summary

**The landmark taper framework (Mujika & Padilla, 2003)** established that tapers improve performance by a mean of **~3%** (range 0.5–6.0%) through positive changes in cardiorespiratory, hormonal, neuromuscular, and psychological status.  The three cardinal rules: **reduce volume up to 60–90%**,  **maintain training intensity** (the single most critical variable), and **reduce frequency no more than 20%**.   Low-intensity tapers produce negative mood states and worse performance.  

**The meta-analysis (Bosquet et al., 2007)** of 27 studies quantified optimal parameters with effect sizes. An **exponential taper** over **2 weeks** with **41–60% volume reduction**, no change in intensity, and no change in frequency produced the largest effects  (ES = 0.72 ± 0.36 for the volume reduction component).  A 2023 update (Wang et al., *PLOS One*, 14 studies) confirmed that **8–14 days** is the optimal taper duration (SMD = −1.47, p < 0.05), and tapering preceded by an overload block produces greater benefits than tapering alone. 

**Taper types compared:** Thomas & Busso (2005) used nonlinear modeling to show exponential and step tapers significantly outperform linear tapers.  The **exponential fast-decay taper** (aggressive initial reduction, leveling off at ~20–30% of normal volume) has the strongest evidence.  Step tapers also work well for shorter periods (7–10 days). For powerlifting, Seppänen & Häkkinen (2022) found a step taper (54% immediate volume reduction) improved squat 1RM by **3.4 ± 2.1%** versus 1.7% for a gradual taper. 

**Strength training taper (Travis et al., 2020):** Reduce strength volume by **30–50%** while maintaining or slightly reducing intensity (≥85% 1RM). Drop accessory exercises first, preserve primary compound movements. Complete cessation of strength training beyond 5 days may decrease upper-body strength;  **3 days of rest appears safe**.  The overreach-then-taper model produces greater performance improvements than taper alone (Häkkinen et al., 1991).

**Multi-day mountain objective taper vs. single-day event:** No controlled studies exist specifically for multi-day ski touring or hike-and-fly tapers, but practitioner consensus (Uphill Athlete/Steve House methodology; RMI Guides; ProCyclingCoaching for stage races) converges on several key modifications. Multi-day objectives need a **longer taper (10–14 days)** with less aggressive volume reduction (~50–60% rather than 60–90%) to avoid detraining while still shedding fatigue.  Terrain specificity must be maintained throughout — continue hiking or skinning on similar terrain during taper. Muscular endurance training should be dropped **≥2 weeks** before departure due to its high recovery demand.  Include a **back-to-back simulation** (two consecutive moderate days) 10–14 days out.   Glycogen loading is particularly important for multi-day performance.  

**Residual training effects support taper confidence:** Aerobic endurance persists ~30 days (Issurin, 2010), so a 2-week taper will not meaningfully reduce aerobic fitness.  Maximal strength also persists ~30 days,   with 1/3 of normal training volume maintaining strength over 32 weeks (Bickel, 2011). 

### Application guideline

**Standard taper protocol for a multi-day mountain objective (3–7 day ski touring trip or hike-and-fly route):**

|Timing       |Strength training                                                   |Endurance training                                                                      |Other                                        |
|-------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
|3–4 weeks out|Normal volume; optional brief overreach (+10–20% load)              |Normal volume; include event-simulation days                                            |Final hard training block                    |
|2 weeks out  |Reduce volume ~40–50%; maintain intensity ≥85% 1RM; drop accessories|Reduce to ~60% volume; maintain interval intensity; include back-to-back simulation days|Taper begins                                 |
|1 week out   |One brief session early in week (Mon/Tue), then cease               |Reduce to ~30–40% volume; short sessions, maintain terrain specificity                  |Prioritize sleep, nutrition, glycogen loading|
|2 days out   |None                                                                |Active recovery only (easy walk, stretch)                                               |Final gear check, packing                    |
|1 day out    |None                                                                |Brief opener: 20–30 min easy with 3–4 short intensity bursts                            |Pre-load carbohydrates                       |

**Taper for a single big day (long summit push, ambitious hike-and-fly route):** Use a **7–10 day exponential taper** with more aggressive volume reduction (60–70%). Maintain 1–2 short high-intensity sessions in the first half of the taper. Full rest final 2 days except for a brief opener.

**Decision rules for the AI:** Taper only for “A” objectives (the athlete’s most important goals). For regular weekend touring, no taper is needed — simply ensure a lighter training day on Friday. For “B” objectives (moderately important trips), reduce volume by ~30% in the preceding 5–7 days.  The AI should classify each objective as A, B, or C based on duration, altitude gain, and athlete-stated importance.

## 5. Autoregulation at the plan level: when to modify versus abandon

### Core principle

Autoregulation uses objective physiological markers (HRV, velocity), subjective indicators (RPE, wellness), and performance trends to dynamically adjust training in real  time. The strongest signal comes from **triangulating multiple markers** — no single metric is sufficient. An AI system should implement a three-tier response framework: **micro-adjustments** (10–15% volume changes for transient fatigue), **deload insertion** (full recovery week for accumulated fatigue), and **plan restructuring** (complete replanning for signs of non-functional overreaching). Combine pre-planned deload scheduling with reactive adjustments for the most robust approach.

### Evidence summary

**HRV monitoring (Plews et al., 2012, 2013; Flatt & Esco, 2015–2021):** The preferred metric is **ln rMSSD** measured upon waking over a 60-second recording. The 7-day rolling average and **coefficient of variation (HRV-CV)** are the two most actionable derived metrics. The Smallest Worthwhile Change (SWC) threshold — defined as the individual mean ± 0.5 × SD of baseline HRV (following Buchheit, 2014) — serves as the primary decision boundary. When the 7-day rolling average falls below the lower SWC band, training should be modified. HRV-CV is inversely correlated with fitness (r = −0.74 between CV change and performance improvement in athletes studied by Flatt). A critical red flag is **declining mean HRV with simultaneously declining CV** — this paradoxical “parasympathetic saturation” pattern may indicate non-functional overreaching.

**HRV-guided vs. pre-planned training:** A 2024 meta-analysis (Plews et al.) found HRV-guided athletes achieved **4–7% better endurance outcomes** over ≥6 months with **31% lower risk** of injury and overtraining. Critically, HRV-guided groups achieved these results with **reduced training volume** (e.g., 13 fewer high-intensity days per 30-day block), making the approach more time-efficient. The benefit was greatest in **recreational athletes** balancing sport with work responsibilities — precisely this athlete profile.

**RPE-based monitoring (Foster et al., 2001; Haddad et al., 2017):** Session RPE (Borg CR-10 × session duration in minutes) provides training load in arbitrary units. RPE inflation — when the same external workload consistently rates **1–2 RPE points higher** than previously — signals accumulated fatigue. Training monotony (mean daily load / SD of daily load) exceeding **2.0** is a risk factor: Foster’s data showed **89% of illnesses and injuries** were preceded by spikes in training strain in the prior 10 days.

**Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio (ACWR):** Originally proposed with a “sweet spot” of 0.8–1.3 (Gabbett, 2016), this metric has faced significant criticism (Wang et al., 2020). The mathematical coupling problem (acute load is embedded within chronic load) weakens the ratio’s validity. **The AI should use ACWR as a heuristic flag, not a hard threshold.** Practical rule: weekly load increases exceeding **~10–15%** are associated with 21–49% increased injury risk. Spikes above ACWR 1.5 should trigger review.

**Velocity-based training (VBT) thresholds (Weakley et al., 2021; González-Badillo et al., 2017):** Within-set velocity loss of **≤20%** provides the best balance of stimulus and recovery. Between-session readiness can be assessed by comparing first-rep velocity at a standardized load to the 30-day rolling average: **>95% = green** (train as planned), **90–95% = amber** (reduce volume), **<90% = red** (recovery session).

**Overreaching spectrum (Meeusen et al., 2013 ECSS/ACSM consensus):** Functional overreaching (FOR) produces a performance decrement lasting **days to ~2 weeks**, followed by supercompensation. Non-functional overreaching (NFOR) requires **weeks to months** for recovery with no supercompensation. Overtraining syndrome (OTS) takes **months to years** for recovery and is diagnosed only by exclusion. Le Meur et al. (2014) found that acutely fatigued athletes who were NOT overreached showed **greater supercompensation (2.6%)** than those classified as functionally overreached — challenging the assumption that deliberate overreaching is necessary for recreational athletes. Warning signs specific to recreational athletes include persistent sleep disruption, motivation loss, DOMS lasting >72 hours, recurrent upper respiratory infections, and elevated resting HR >5 bpm above baseline.

### Application guideline

**The AI should implement a three-tier decision framework:**

**Tier 1 — Micro-adjustment (10–15% volume reduction for 2–3 sessions).** Trigger: any one of these — HRV 7-day average dips below SWC for 1–2 days; RPE inflated by 1 point for equivalent workload; first-rep velocity 90–95% of 30-day average; ACWR approaching 1.3; single night of poor sleep. Action: reduce working sets by 1–2 per exercise, swap a high-intensity session for moderate, add 10 minutes of mobility work.

**Tier 2 — Deload week insertion (40–60% volume reduction for 5–7 days).** Trigger: two or more of these persisting >5–7 days — HRV below SWC for a full week; HRV-CV trending upward over 2+ weeks; RPE inflated by ≥2 points consistently; velocity <90% of average across multiple sessions; performance decrement >3–5% in key lifts; monotony >2.0; persistent soreness, sleep disruption, or motivation loss. Action: reduce volume 40–60%, maintain intensity and frequency, maintain primary movements but drop accessories, increase reps in reserve by 2–3.

**Tier 3 — Plan restructuring (complete replanning).** Trigger: multiple red flags persisting >2–3 weeks despite a deload — declining HRV mean with declining CV (parasympathetic saturation); performance decrements >5–10% not recovering after deload; persistent RPE elevation despite reduced loads; recurrent illness; sustained motivation loss. Action: 1–2 weeks of complete rest or very light active recovery, then rebuild at 40–50% of prior volume. Reassess non-training stressors (work, sleep, nutrition, energy availability). Consider medical screening.

**Daily readiness scoring algorithm for the AI:**

The system should compute a composite score (0–100) weighted as: **HRV component (40%)** — morning rMSSD vs. 7-day rolling average and SWC bands, plus CV trend; **subjective wellness (30%)** — sleep quality, energy, soreness, stress (1–5 scales); **performance/load component (30%)** — previous session RPE vs. intended, ACWR, velocity trends if available.

- Score >75 + ACWR <1.3 → execute planned session (green)
- Score 50–75 or ACWR 1.3–1.5 → reduce volume 10–20%, maintain intensity on primary lifts (amber)
- Score <50 or ACWR >1.5 → replace with recovery session; if red for 3+ consecutive days, trigger deload (red)

**Pre-planned vs. reactive deloading:** Survey data shows 47% of athletes use pre-planned deloads, 13% purely autoregulated, and ~39% a combination. **The combined approach is recommended.** Pre-schedule a deload every 4th week as the default, but allow the autoregulation system to shift this forward (deload at week 3 if markers deteriorate) or backward (extend to week 5 if markers remain strong). No athlete should go beyond 6 weeks without a deload during progressive loading phases.

**Recreational athlete considerations:** Non-training stressors (work deadlines, family, travel, poor sleep, altitude exposure in the Innsbruck area) often exceed training as recovery-limiting factors. HRV captures total allostatic load — this is a feature, not a bug, and makes HRV particularly valuable for this population. The AI should weight subjective wellness markers more heavily during known high-stress periods (e.g., work travel, illness in household) even if HRV data is unavailable.
