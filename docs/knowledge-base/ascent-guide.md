## Prologue: The Twenty-Four Rules That Govern Everything

Before we get into the details of sets, reps, zones, and heart rate variability, there are twenty-four cross-domain rules that sit above everything else in this system. Think of them as the constitutional law of your training — when any specific recommendation from a later chapter conflicts with one of these rules, these rules win. They exist because training doesn't happen in isolated domains. Your legs don't know whether they're tired from squats or from skinning up a 1,400-meter couloir approach. Your nervous system doesn't compartmentalize "strength fatigue" and "endurance fatigue." Everything talks to everything.

Let's walk through the most important ones, because they'll keep coming up throughout this guide.

**The eight-hour rule.** Never schedule heavy lower-body strength work within eight hours after a long or hard mountain day. This isn't arbitrary — research from Doma and colleagues showed that running economy and lower-body power take a measurable hit when strength and endurance sessions are crammed too close together. Robineau's study with rugby players found that same-session concurrent training produced the worst outcomes of any spacing arrangement. The practical version: if you did a big ski tour on Saturday morning, your squat session belongs on Sunday afternoon at the earliest, and Monday evening is better.

**The strength-first rule.** When you absolutely must train both in the same day, lift before you ski or hike. Two independent meta-analyses — Eddens in 2018 and Murlasits the same year — found roughly a 7% advantage for lower-body strength when resistance training comes first. Here's the interesting part: endurance gains are completely unaffected by either order. So putting strength first is a free lunch — you protect your strength without sacrificing your aerobic development. You need at least a six-hour gap between sessions, though three hours is acceptable if the endurance work is genuinely easy.

**Intensity is the last thing you cut.** This might be the single most counterintuitive rule in all of exercise science, and it's backed by rock-solid evidence. When life gets busy and you need to trim your training down to the minimum — maybe you're deep in ski touring season and can only hit the gym once a week — the thing you protect is load on the bar, not volume, not frequency. Bickel's landmark 2011 study showed that strength can be maintained for up to 32 weeks on just one session per week with as few as one set per exercise, an 89% volume reduction, but only if the weight stays heavy. The moment you start "going light to save energy," you lose the stimulus that maintains your strength. Keep the bar heavy. Cut everything else.

**Deload both or deload neither.** When you take a recovery week, take it across the board. Dropping your gym volume by 50% while pushing through a hard week of ski touring doesn't give your body the recovery signal it needs. Both streams of training — strength and endurance — generate fatigue that accumulates in the same nervous system, the same hormonal milieu, the same connective tissues. Reduce strength volume by 40–50% and endurance volume by 40–60% in the same week.

**Altitude lies to your heart rate monitor.** At 2,000 meters, your VO2max drops roughly 9–10%. At 3,000 meters, it's down 15–17%. That means the same absolute effort represents a much higher percentage of your maximum capacity. If your Zone 1 ceiling is normally 145 bpm at valley level, it's closer to 140 at 2,500 meters. When your heart rate and your perceived effort disagree at altitude, trust how you feel. Your body knows what altitude is doing even when your watch doesn't.

**Respect the "easy" mountain day.** This one catches people constantly. Walking at 5 kilometers per hour on a 20% grade — a moderate skinning pace on a moderate slope — demands about 91% of VO2max for a recreational athlete with a VO2max of 45 ml/kg/min. Add a 12-kilogram pack, 2,500 meters of altitude, and wind chill, and your "casual Saturday tour" might genuinely be a maximal workout. The system has to calculate effective intensity from GPS data — speed, grade, altitude, pack weight — because your subjective sense of "this wasn't that hard" can be wildly wrong.

**The gray zone is the enemy.** If you take nothing else from the endurance chapter, take this: the polarized training model works because most of your sessions are genuinely easy and a small fraction are genuinely hard. The single most common error recreational mountain athletes make is letting every session drift into the 78–88% HRmax range — too hard to recover from quickly, too easy to drive meaningful adaptation. Any session where your average heart rate lands in that zone should be flagged. On your easy tours, slow down. On your hard sessions, push harder.

**Your RPE is probably off by one rep.** Halperin's 2022 meta-analysis found that trainees underpredict how many reps they have in reserve by about one repetition on average. When you think you have two reps left, you probably have one. This matters because the system programs most hypertrophy work at RPE 7–8, which means you're likely actually at RPE 8–9 — right in the sweet spot. Don't try to correct for this consciously; just know that the programming accounts for it.

**Resort snowboarding isn't what you think it is.** A full day at the resort feels exhausting, but the data tells a nuanced story. Roberts' 2020 study found the whole-session average heart rate is only 64% of max — because you're spending two-thirds of the time on lifts, in line, or resting. During active riding, though, heart rate averages 76% of max, which is meaningfully higher. And the eccentric quad loading from a day of turns is significant. The bottom line: don't count resort days toward your high-intensity training quota, but don't ignore them either. They're moderate-intensity intermittent activity with heavy eccentric loading, and you need at least 24 hours before hitting legs in the gym.

**Three weeks without heavy lifting is the red line.** This comes directly from Rønnestad's research program spanning 2010 to 2022: one heavy strength session every seven to ten days maintains your gains. One session every two weeks is not enough. After three to four weeks without touching heavy weights, you start losing strength that takes roughly half the detraining duration to rebuild. During your busiest touring weeks, you can skip almost everything in the gym except one focused 25-minute session of heavy compound movements.

**Sleep under six hours is a red alert.** Not a yellow flag — a red alert. Reduce the next day's training volume by at least 50% and eliminate all high-intensity work. Compound movements like squats and deadlifts are particularly vulnerable. After two or more nights of restricted sleep, you'll see roughly a 3% hit to maximal strength, 10% to strength endurance, and a striking 21% to skill-based performance. If you slept badly before a planned heavy session, swap it for something lighter.

**Never rely on a single metric.** No single number — not your HRV, not your sleep score, not your morning mood — should drive a training decision in isolation. The system uses multi-signal convergence: when three or more indicators are simultaneously degraded, it intervenes regardless of what the plan says. Subjective measures are more sensitive day-to-day, while HRV detects maladaptation weeks before you consciously feel it. Together, they're far more powerful than either alone.

**Carbohydrate needs are wildly variable.** On a rest day, 3–5 grams per kilogram of bodyweight is plenty. On a long touring day, you might need 8–12 grams per kilogram — a threefold increase. Altitude makes this worse because your body preferentially burns carbohydrates at elevation. Underfueling at altitude is the primary driver of low T3 syndrome and relative energy deficiency in mountain athletes.

The remaining rules cover ferritin requirements for altitude readiness, the residual training effects that make block periodization work, exercise selection by training phase, the priority screening of ferritin and vitamin D for mountain athletes, schedule disruption management, illness protocols, multi-day touring load management, and the interaction between caloric deficit and training adaptation. We'll cover each of them in context throughout the chapters that follow.

Now let's start building the picture from the ground up, beginning with how to get stronger.

## Chapter 1: Building Strength That Serves the Mountain

### How You Get Stronger Over Time

Progressive overload — systematically increasing the demands you place on your body — is the single most fundamental driver of strength and muscle growth. Everything else in this chapter, from volume landmarks to deload protocols, is just organizational scaffolding around that core principle. The 2025 ACSM Position Stand, which synthesized 137 systematic reviews, confirmed what many coaches have long suspected: the choice of periodization model matters far less than whether you're consistently and progressively challenging your body. Missing a mesocycle transition isn't a crisis. Failing to progressively overload is.

The way you progress depends on where you are in your training journey. If you've been lifting for less than six months, simple linear progression works beautifully: add 2.5 to 5 pounds to your upper body lifts and 5 to 10 pounds to your lower body lifts every session. This works because your nervous system is rapidly learning to recruit existing muscle fibers more efficiently, and the gains come fast. You don't need anything fancy. You just need to show up and put a little more weight on the bar each time.

That honeymoon period typically stalls after three to six months. When you can't add weight every session anymore, it's time for double progression: work within a rep range — say 8 to 12 reps — and only increase the weight when you can hit the top of that range on all your sets. So if you're doing three sets of squats and your target is 8–12 reps, you'd keep the weight the same until you can do 3 sets of 12, then bump the weight up and start back at 3 sets of 8. Tighter rep ranges, like 4–6, work better for big barbell lifts where you're adding small increments. Wider ranges, like 8–15, suit isolation and machine work where even the smallest plate jump represents a bigger percentage increase.

After a year or so of consistent training, undulating periodization becomes valuable. Instead of doing the same rep range every session, you vary it within the week: one heavy day in the 3–5 rep range at 85–90% of your max, one moderate day at 8–12 reps, one lighter day at 12–15 reps. A meta-analysis by Moesgaard in 2022 found that undulating periodization produces significantly faster strength gains than linear periodization in trained lifters, with an effect size of 0.61. A Stronger By Science re-analysis estimated undulating models produce strength gains about 28% faster.

For advanced trainees — those with three or more years of serious lifting — block periodization becomes the tool of choice. You dedicate entire mesocycles to specific goals: an accumulation block focused on hypertrophy at moderate loads and high volume, followed by an intensification block focused on maximal strength at heavier loads and moderate volume, followed by a realization block where you peak. This works because of residual training effects — the hypertrophy you build persists for about 30 days, long enough to carry over into the strength block that follows.

The key progression rates to expect: a novice might add 5–10 pounds per session on lower body lifts. An intermediate, six months to three years in, is looking at 5–10 pounds per month on major lifts. An advanced lifter is doing well to add 2.5–5 pounds per month or per mesocycle. And regardless of your level, cap your weekly increase in load or volume at 10%.

### How Much Volume You Actually Need

Volume — measured in hard working sets per muscle group per week — follows a curve of diminishing returns. More is generally better, up to a point, after which you're just accumulating fatigue without meaningful additional growth. Three landmarks define the usable range.

**Minimum Effective Volume (MEV)** is the floor — the least you can do and still see measurable growth. For most intermediate lifters, this is around 6–10 sets per muscle group per week, depending on the muscle. **Maximum Adaptive Volume (MAV)** is the sweet spot where you get the best returns on investment, typically 12–20 sets for most muscle groups. **Maximum Recoverable Volume (MRV)** is the ceiling — beyond this, you can't recover between sessions and you start going backward.

What do these numbers actually look like in practice? Let's take quads as an example, since they're so important for mountain sports. The MEV for quads is about 8 sets per week for an intermediate lifter. That's roughly two exercises at 3–4 sets each, twice per week — say, 3 sets of squats on Monday and 3 sets of leg press on Thursday. The MAV range is 12–18 sets. At the high end, that might look like 3 sets of squats plus 3 sets of leg press on Monday, and 3 sets of front squats plus 3 sets of leg extensions on Thursday, plus maybe 3 sets of Bulgarian split squats on Saturday — 15 sets total. The MRV is 20 or more sets, which is a lot of quad work and requires high training frequency to distribute it without crushing per-session quality.

The most comprehensive analysis to date — Pelland's 2025 meta-regression across 67 studies and over 2,000 participants — found that the volume-hypertrophy relationship follows a square root curve: substantial diminishing returns beyond about 12–20 sets, with no clear ceiling. They also introduced a clever "fractional" counting method: compound exercises that indirectly train a muscle, like bench press for triceps, count as roughly 0.5 direct sets. So if you're doing 6 sets of pressing movements per week, your triceps are getting about 3 "effective" sets from those compounds alone, before you add any isolation work.

For maintenance — when you're deep in touring season and just trying to hold onto what you've built — the floor drops dramatically. As few as 4–8 sets per major muscle group per week will maintain your strength and size, and Bickel showed that even 2–4 sets per muscle per week can hold the line, as long as the intensity stays high.

A practical mesocycle, then, works like this: start week 1 near your MEV, add 1–3 sets per muscle group each week, and by week 3 or 4 you're approaching your MRV. Then deload, drop back down, and start again — potentially with new exercise variations to keep connective tissues happy.

Individual variation here is substantial, on the order of 30–50% based on genetics, sleep quality, stress levels, and nutrition. Treat all these numbers as starting estimates that get refined as the system learns your responses.

### Intensity, RPE, and Knowing How Hard to Push

There are two ways to prescribe intensity: fixed percentages of your one-rep max, or autoregulated RPE — Rate of Perceived Exertion, measured on a scale where 10 means you couldn't have done another rep and 7 means you had about three reps left in the tank.

Percentage-based training provides structure. If the plan says 4 sets of 5 at 82% of your squat max, you know exactly what weight to load. The problem is that your capacity fluctuates daily — after a poor night's sleep, or the day after a big ski tour, 82% might feel like 90%. RPE-based training solves this by letting the load float: the plan says "4 sets of 5 at RPE 8," and you pick a weight that leaves you with about two reps in reserve. On a good day, that might be 85% of your max. On a rough day, it might be 78%. Either way, you're getting the right stimulus.

The research supports autoregulation. Graham and Cleather's 2021 study found that autoregulated training produced significantly greater strength gains than fixed loading: 11.7% vs 8.3% in front squat and 10.8% vs 7.1% in back squat over twelve weeks. Mann's earlier study found even larger advantages for the APRE system over linear periodization.

A critical piece of newer research — Robinson and colleagues' 2024 meta-regression in Sports Medicine — revealed that the dose-response relationship between proximity to failure and adaptation is different for hypertrophy versus strength. For hypertrophy, there's a nonlinear benefit to training closer to failure: 1–2 RIR produces meaningfully more growth than 3–5 RIR. But for strength, gains are similar across a wide RIR range — training at 2–4 RIR gives you comparable strength gains to training at 0–1 RIR.

This has direct practical implications. During hypertrophy phases, the system pushes you closer to failure — RPE 8–9, or 1–2 reps in reserve. During strength phases, and especially during concurrent training phases where you need to preserve recovery for mountain endurance, it backs off to RPE 7–8, or 2–4 reps in reserve. You're not sacrificing strength gains by leaving a few reps on the table, and you're preserving recovery capacity for everything else you need to do.

Remember the one-rep underprediction error: when you think RPE 8, you're likely actually at RPE 9. The programming accounts for this. A prescribed RPE 8 is close to the hypertrophy sweet spot once you factor in the systematic bias.

If you have access to velocity tracking — and many modern gym apps can estimate this from video — there's another readiness tool available. Compare your first-rep velocity at a standardized load to your 30-day rolling average. Above 95% of average is green: train as planned. Between 90–95% is amber: consider reducing volume. Below 90% is red: make it a recovery session.

### How Often to Train Each Muscle

Training frequency per muscle group matters primarily as a vehicle for distributing volume, not as an independent growth variable. When total weekly volume is equated, the research is clear: training a muscle twice per week versus three times per week produces nearly identical hypertrophy. Schoenfeld's updated 2019 meta-analysis across 25 studies found no significant difference between higher and lower frequencies on a volume-equated basis.

The practical minimum is twice per week per muscle group. The practical maximum is determined by how many sessions you need to distribute your target weekly volume without exceeding about 10 sets per muscle per session — after that point, the quality of your later sets degrades.

For small, recovery-resilient muscles like side delts, biceps, calves, and abs, you can go as high as three to six sessions per week, which helps distribute the high volumes these muscles can tolerate. For large, fatiguing muscle groups like quads and back, cap it at two to three sessions with at least 48 hours between.

There is one caveat for strength specifically: Grgic's 2018 meta-analysis found a frequency effect for compound lift strength, likely driven by motor learning. If you're trying to improve your squat max, squatting three to four times per week gives you more practice with the movement pattern. But the effect disappears when volume is equated, suggesting the benefit is about extra volume and practice, not frequency per se.

Your split should match your available training days. Three days per week? Full body, hitting each muscle three times. Four days? Upper-lower, each muscle twice. Five or six days? Push-pull-legs, each muscle twice.

### When and How to Deload

A deload is a planned five-to-seven-day period of reduced training stress designed to let accumulated fatigue dissipate while your fitness stays intact. Think of it through the fitness-fatigue model: at any given moment, your performance equals your fitness minus your accumulated fatigue. Fatigue dissipates quickly — its time constant is about 7–11 days — while fitness persists much longer, on the order of 20–50 days. During a deload, fatigue drops off rapidly while fitness barely budges, producing a performance "supercompensation" effect. Tapering research shows roughly 2–8% improvement in maximal strength from an effective deload.

The preferred method is a volume-reduction deload. Cut your total weekly sets by 40–60%, but keep the weight on the bar the same. This preserves your neural adaptations and technical proficiency while giving your joints, connective tissues, and hormonal systems a breather. Coleman's 2024 study confirmed that a week of complete training cessation actually hurt lower-body strength compared to an active deload — so keep showing up, just do less.

For concurrent training athletes — which is what you are — the default schedule is a deload every fourth week, giving you a 3:1 loading-to-recovery ratio. Advanced or highly stressed athletes might need a 2:1 ratio. Beginners can often push to six or even eight weeks before needing one.

But the schedule should be flexible. Autoregulation can override the calendar in both directions: extend to week 5 if your readiness markers are still strong, or pull forward to week 3 if things are deteriorating. The reactive triggers include RPE creeping up more than one point at the same loads for two or more sessions, wellness scores declining for two consecutive weeks, HRV dropping more than one standard deviation from your baseline for a week or more, or resting heart rate elevated more than 5 bpm for three or more days. When any of these fire, take the deload regardless of where you are in the cycle.

After a deload, restart your next mesocycle at your MEV — potentially with fresh exercise selections — resuming at pre-deload intensity but roughly 90% of pre-deload volume in the first week back.

### Fatigue: The Two Axes and the SFR Concept

Training fatigue operates on two axes that recover at very different rates. Acute neuromuscular fatigue peaks within 24 hours and resolves in 48–72 hours. Muscle damage from DOMS peaks at 24–72 hours and resolves in 3–7 days. But joint and connective tissue stress accumulates gradually over weeks and requires a full deload to dissipate — you can't just take a day off and fix it.

This is where the Stimulus-to-Fatigue Ratio comes in. Every exercise provides some adaptive stimulus to the target muscle and generates some amount of systemic fatigue. A hack squat gives your quads a tremendous stimulus while generating relatively little fatigue elsewhere — high SFR. A conventional deadlift gives your posterior chain a great stimulus but absolutely hammers your nervous system, spinal erectors, grip, and overall recovery — low SFR.

During hypertrophy phases, when you're doing high volumes and need to recover from lots of sets, anchor your program around high-SFR exercises: machines, cables, chest-supported rows, hack squats, leg presses, leg extensions. Save the big barbell compounds — heavy back squats, conventional deadlifts, bent-over rows — for strength phases where the total-body loading and neural demand is the point.

One reassuring note: true overtraining syndrome from resistance training alone is extraordinarily rare. Bell's 2020 scoping review found minimal evidence it has ever actually occurred from lifting weights. What people call "overtraining" is almost always non-functional overreaching, and 92.5% of those cases are accompanied by additional non-training stressors — poor sleep, work stress, relationship problems, inadequate nutrition. The training was the last straw, not the sole cause.

### Choosing Your Exercises

Exercise selection should be driven by three factors: your training goal (hypertrophy vs. strength), the SFR of the exercise, and your individual biomechanics and injury history.

For squats, the research points toward front squats for maximal quad activation — Coratella's 2021 study found 24% greater rectus femoris activation compared to back squats, with an effect size of 1.21 — while generating less knee compression than back squats. But for raw loading and glute/hamstring development, low-bar back squats are superior. Bulgarian split squats at 50% of your max match the glute and quad activation of back squats at 85%, with far less spinal loading. And hack squats and leg presses offer the highest SFR of any squat pattern.

For deadlifts, the conventional pull has the lowest SFR of any common exercise — massive systemic fatigue for what amounts to a posterior chain stimulus. Trap bar deadlifts let you lift 5–10% heavier with more quad involvement and significantly less spinal stress. Romanian deadlifts provide constant hamstring tension at submaximal loads and are the best SFR choice for hamstring hypertrophy.

For bench press, the research is clear that 30 degrees of incline is optimal for upper pec activation — Rodríguez-Ridao's 2020 study and Lauver's 2016 study both confirmed this. Beyond 45 degrees, the anterior deltoid takes over. Grip width has minimal effect on pec activation.

For rows, chest-supported variations produce greater lat and rhomboid activation than free-standing bent-over rows while eliminating the erector spinae fatigue entirely — making them the highest SFR pulling exercise. This is especially relevant for concurrent athletes who are already loading their spines with hiking and touring.

The exercise selection hierarchy flips between phases. During hypertrophy blocks, bias toward high-SFR variations: hack squats, Romanian deadlifts, 30-degree incline dumbbell press, chest-supported rows. During strength blocks, shift to the big barbell movements: low-bar back squats, conventional deadlifts, flat bench, bent-over rows. And here's a practical tip for concurrent athletes: when programming a deadlift and a row in the same session, pair conventional deadlifts with chest-supported rows to avoid stacking spinal fatigue.

Rotate your exercise variations every one to two mesocycles. This isn't about "muscle confusion" — it's about connective tissue management and preventing the repetitive stress that comes from hammering the same movement pattern for months.

Now that you understand how strength programming works in isolation, let's talk about what happens when you layer three to six hours of mountain endurance on top of it.

## Chapter 2: Endurance for Vertical Terrain

### The Polarized Model: Why 80/20 Works

If there's a single concept that should reshape how you think about endurance training, it's Stephen Seiler's polarized model. The core observation is deceptively simple: elite endurance athletes across nearly every sport converge on an intensity distribution where roughly 80% of their sessions are below the first ventilatory threshold — genuinely easy, conversational-pace effort — and about 20% are above the second ventilatory threshold — genuinely hard, race-pace or faster. The middle zone — that "comfortably hard" effort between the two thresholds — gets almost nothing, maybe 0–5% of training time.

The 80/20 number was originally described by session count. Measured by time-in-zone, the split is more like 90/5/5, because high-intensity intervals occupy only minutes within a session even though the session itself counts as "hard."

The evidence is compelling. Stöggl and Sperlich's 2014 study compared polarized, threshold, high-volume, and high-intensity-only training in 48 well-trained athletes. The polarized group improved VO2peak by 11.7%, time to exhaustion by 17.4%, and power at 4 mmol/L lactate by 8.1%. The threshold and high-volume groups showed no significant improvements. Neal's 2013 crossover study with trained cyclists found polarized training outperformed threshold training on every metric despite lower total volume.

But here's the important nuance: the most recent and methodologically strongest evidence — Rosenblat and Seiler's 2025 individual-participant-data network meta-analysis across 13 studies — found no difference between polarized and pyramidal distribution for VO2max or time-trial performance in recreational athletes. The benefit of strict polarization appears to emerge mainly at the competitive level.

What does this mean for you? It means you shouldn't be dogmatic about the exact split. What matters is three things: keep your easy sessions genuinely easy, make your hard sessions genuinely hard, and accumulate sufficient total volume. Seiler's hierarchy is clear: total training volume is the most important factor, followed by intensity distribution, followed by the details of periodization.

The practical enemy is the gray zone. Every mountain athlete gravitates toward it. You're skinning up a beautiful morning, the sun is out, your partner sets a pace that's just a bit quick, and before you know it your heart rate is sitting at 82% of max for two hours. Too hard to recover from quickly, too easy to drive meaningful aerobic or anaerobic adaptation. Flag any session where your average heart rate lands in the 78–88% HRmax range. On your easy tours, deliberately slow down. On your hard sessions, commit to genuine discomfort.

For a recreational mountain athlete training five to ten hours per week, the prescription looks like this: two high-intensity sessions per week (usually one gym-based interval session and one hard mountain session), with everything else at Zone 1 — below your first ventilatory threshold, at a pace where you can comfortably hold a conversation. If your weekly volume drops below five hours, make sure at least one session is dedicated high-intensity work. If it drops below three hours, a single weekly interval session may be enough.

And if you're just starting out or returning from a break, spend the first 8–12 weeks doing nothing but aerobic base training — all sessions below VT1. No intervals. No tempo work. Build the foundation before you try to put a roof on it.

### Setting Your Zones Without a Lab

In a perfect world, you'd have a lactate curve from a sports physiology lab pinpointing your exact threshold heart rates. In the real world, you need to work with what you've got.

The three-zone model is the simplest and maps directly to the polarized philosophy. Zone 1 is below your first ventilatory threshold — blood lactate under 2 mmol/L, heart rate typically below 77–82% of max, RPE below 12 on the Borg scale. You can talk easily. Zone 2 is between the two thresholds — 2 to 4 mmol/L lactate, 82–88% HRmax, you can speak in short phrases. Zone 3 is above the second threshold — above 4 mmol/L, above 88% HRmax, you can only manage a few words.

To set initial zone boundaries, use the Tanaka formula for max heart rate: 208 minus 0.7 times your age. This is better than the old 220-minus-age formula, particularly for athletes over 40, but all formulas carry a standard error of about 10 bpm — which can cascade into significant zone errors. The Karvonen method, which uses heart rate reserve (max minus resting), maps more accurately to VO2 reserve.

The real solution is a field test. Warm up for 15 minutes, then do three all-out 3-minute efforts on a steep hill with 3 minutes of easy jogging recovery between them. Your peak heart rate on the third effort is a practical estimate of your HRmax — far more accurate than any formula. If you can get a lab-tested lactate threshold heart rate, use that as your primary anchor and derive everything from it.

One critical concept: RPE isn't just a fallback for when your watch dies. RPE correlates with blood lactate more strongly than heart rate does — r = 0.83 versus r = 0.74 — making it a valuable complementary signal. This is especially true in mountain sports, where altitude, cold, variable terrain, and cardiac drift all conspire to make heart rate data unreliable. The simple cues: Zone 1 means you can talk easily. Zone 2 means short phrases. Zone 3 means only a few words.

### The Problem of Cardiac Drift

During sustained exercise, your heart rate drifts upward even at constant workload. Rising core temperature, plasma volume loss from sweating, and reduced stroke volume all contribute. Typical drift runs 10–20 bpm over 30–60 minutes, roughly 3–5 bpm per half hour. On a four-hour ski tour, that's enough to shift your heart rate from solidly Zone 1 into what looks like Zone 2 — even though your actual metabolic intensity hasn't changed.

The fix: base your zone classification on first-hour heart rate data for long mountain sessions. For every hour beyond the first, subtract 3–5 bpm from recorded heart rate before classifying the zone. When prescribing long easy sessions, aim for the lower end of Zone 1 at the start, leaving room for drift.

The Uphill Athlete test offers a useful benchmark: if your heart rate drifts less than 3.5% over 60 minutes at a steady aerobic pace, you're below your aerobic threshold. More than 5% drift means you're above it.

### The Physics of Going Up

The metabolic cost of uphill walking doesn't increase linearly with gradient — it accelerates. At a 10% grade, the energy cost is about 2.9 times flat-ground walking. At 20%, it jumps to 4.9 times. At 30%, 6.9 times. At 45%, you're looking at 10.6 times the cost of walking on flat ground. This data comes from Minetti's definitive 2002 study, and it transforms how you should think about mountain training.

Here's the practical implication. Using the ACSM metabolic equation, walking at 5 km/h on a 15% grade demands about 34 ml/kg/min of oxygen — roughly 9.7 METs. On a 20% grade at the same speed, it's 41 ml/kg/min. If your VO2max is 45 ml/kg/min, that 20% grade at a moderate pace is demanding 91% of your maximum aerobic capacity. You're essentially redlining without realizing it, because the pace feels "slow."

This is why gradient is the most powerful intensity lever in mountain training. For Zone 1 training, stick to 10–15% grades at moderate speed. For Zone 3 intervals, head for terrain steeper than 20%. And when the system flags a session as high-intensity even though you felt like you were going slow, this is why.

Add a pack and the picture gets even more demanding. A 15-kilogram load on a 75-kilogram person increases energy expenditure by roughly 20–25% at any given speed and grade. The Pandolf equation, the standard tool for estimating loaded march metabolic cost, actually under-predicts by 12–33% for modern load configurations. A practical rule: multiply estimated energy expenditure by a factor of 1 plus 0.015 times the load in kilograms.

For vertical gain as a training metric, the equivalence is roughly 100 meters of vertical gain costing the same energy as 1.0–1.5 kilometers of flat running. Track your weekly vertical meters the same way a runner tracks weekly mileage, and apply the same acute-to-chronic workload ratio: keep your weekly vertical within 0.8–1.3 times the four-week rolling average. Never let a single session exceed 1.5 times the average of the previous four similar sessions.

### Altitude: The Invisible Training Variable

Living and training around Innsbruck means altitude is a constant factor. The valley sits at about 570 meters, but your training takes you regularly to 2,000–3,000 meters, with occasional days higher. Each thousand meters costs you roughly 6–7% of your VO2max.

The acclimatization timeline follows a predictable pattern. Days 1–3 bring immediate VO2max decline, resting heart rate elevation of 4–5 bpm, hyperventilation, sleep disturbances, and possible acute mountain sickness symptoms. Days 3–7 see ventilatory acclimatization beginning, EPO release starting (requiring at least 1,800 meters of elevation), and HRV beginning to normalize. By days 7–14, red blood cell production is increasing and VO2max is recovering at about 4% per week. Full hematological adaptation to 3,000 meters takes about 34 days.

For day trips from Innsbruck to 2,000–3,000 meters, each trip is an acute exposure without overnight acclimatization. You won't get meaningful acclimatization from day trips alone, but frequent exposure — three or more times per week — provides cumulative benefit. Track your total hours above 2,000 meters per week to modulate heart rate adjustments.

The altitude adjustment for zone boundaries is straightforward: subtract 5 bpm for 1,500–2,500 meters and 8–10 bpm for 2,500–3,000 meters. So if your Zone 1 ceiling is normally 145 bpm, at 2,500 meters it drops to 140. At 3,000 meters, to about 137. And when RPE and heart rate disagree at altitude — which they will — trust RPE.

Recovery at altitude is impaired. Sleep architecture is disrupted: light sleep decreases, respiratory rate increases, periodic breathing begins above 2,000 meters. HRV is acutely suppressed. Hydration needs jump to 3–4 liters per day minimum. After sessions above 2,000 meters, plan on 20–30% more recovery time than equivalent sessions at valley level.

### Your Sports: What the Science Says

**Ski touring and splitboarding** are among the most demanding endurance activities in existence. Elite ski mountaineers carry VO2max values of 71 ml/kg/min. Even sub-elite athletes average 62.5. During racing, mean heart rate sits at 92–93% of max, with 42% of race time above the second ventilatory threshold. Energy expenditure during recreational touring runs 400–700 kcal per hour, with roughly 500–600 kcal per 1,000 meters of vertical for an 80-kilogram person with gear.

Cold adds another layer. Even a 0.5°C drop in core temperature measurably lowers self-paced endurance output. More than half of elite cross-country skiers experience exercise-induced bronchoconstriction. The optimal performance range is 4–20°C, meaning most ski touring happens below it.

Your winter periodization should follow a clear progression: build aerobic base through high-volume Zone 1 work in October and November, add one to two weekly interval sessions — steep skinning repeats, 4×4 minutes at above 90% HRmax — in December and January, reach peak specificity with long tours and maintained intervals in February and March, then maintain through April with reduced volume.

**Hike-and-fly paragliding** transforms leisure hiking into race-effort loaded uphill work. Standard paraglider kits weigh 12–22 kilograms, though ultralight setups can come under 5 kilograms. Speed hiking with a 12–15 kilogram load at 5–6 km/h uphill is estimated at 8–12 METs — 600 to 900+ kcal per hour depending on gradient and altitude. The key training prescriptions are building aerobic base with standard Zone 1 hiking, adding loaded speed hikes at 80–85% HRmax one to two times per week, and including grip and forearm endurance work plus core stability for the flying phase. And for race preparation, practice "fatigued launches" — complete a hard hike, then immediately practice wing inflation and ground handling.

**Resort snowboarding** we covered in the prologue: moderate-intensity intermittent activity with significant eccentric quad loading. Active riding heart rate averages 76% of max, but only a third of session time is active riding. The whole-session load of a four-hour day is roughly equivalent to 60–90 minutes of moderate continuous exercise. Don't count it toward high-intensity quotas, but do respect the eccentric loading — no heavy leg day within 24 hours.

### Multi-Day Touring: When Fatigue Compounds

Multi-day hut-to-hut traverses create a unique physiological challenge. Cumulative fatigue doesn't add linearly — it compounds exponentially. Koller's 2018 study showed that concentric and eccentric quadriceps and hamstring strength decline progressively across consecutive ski mountaineering days, with insufficient recovery in the 16–20 hours between stages. Postural control — your ability to balance on exposed terrain — deteriorates progressively, peaking around day 3.

The preparation starts four to eight weeks before departure. Build eccentric leg strength with slow-negative Bulgarian split squats, walking lunges, and Nordic hamstring curls. Include at least two or three back-to-back weekend touring days during preparation to stress your cumulative fatigue systems. Taper strength training in the final week. Glycogen-load for two days before departure: 8–10 grams of carbohydrate per kilogram of bodyweight per day.

During the traverse, plan rest days every third day. Front-load easier stages in the first one to two days. Target 60–75% HRmax for sustainable touring — resist competitive pacing with partners. Fuel aggressively: 50–70 grams of carbohydrate per hour, beginning within the first 30 minutes. Daily caloric intake should hit 8–12 grams of carbs per kilogram of bodyweight. Use trekking poles on all descents to reduce eccentric quad loading by roughly 25%.

After completing a three-to-five-day traverse, prescribe two to three full rest days. For longer trips, four to five. The first session back should be light Zone 1 on flat terrain, 30–45 minutes. No lower-body strength for at least 72 hours after the final day. Expect DOMS to peak 48–72 hours later — this is normal, not an injury signal.

Each day of multi-day touring should be classified as a high-stress endurance session in your load calculations, regardless of pace. A four-day traverse represents roughly four high-stress sessions — reduce the following week's volume by 60–80% and eliminate all high-intensity work for the subsequent 7–10 days.

With endurance training covered, let's tackle the elephant in the room: what happens when you try to get stronger and build endurance at the same time.

## Chapter 3: The Interference Problem — Training Both Without Losing Either

### The Interference Effect Is Real, But Probably Not What You Think

In 1980, Robert Hickson published a study showing that adding endurance training to a strength program reduced strength gains by about 43%. This result scared an entire generation of lifters away from cardio. But Hickson's study used eleven training sessions per week — a volume that no recreational athlete would ever approach — and subsequent research has painted a much more nuanced picture.

Wilson's 2012 meta-analysis, which most people cite, reported that concurrent training reduced strength effect sizes by about 18% and hypertrophy effect sizes by about 31%. Those sound alarming. But Schumann's 2022 meta-analysis, which used more rigorous between-group comparisons across 43 studies, told a different story: the interference effect on maximal strength was an effect size of negative 0.06 (not significant), and on hypertrophy it was negative 0.01 (essentially zero). Only explosive power showed consistent, significant interference, at negative 0.28.

What changed? Methodology. Wilson used within-group effect sizes, which inflate apparent interference. Schumann used between-group standardized mean differences, which is the more valid comparison. The current scientific consensus: for recreational athletes, maximal strength and muscle size are not significantly compromised by concurrent training. Only explosive power is meaningfully affected.

The molecular mechanism is real but dose-dependent. Endurance exercise activates AMPK, a cellular energy sensor that directly inhibits mTOR, the pathway that drives muscle protein synthesis. But the dose matters enormously. Coffey's 2009 study found that 30 minutes of moderate-intensity cycling did not inhibit mTOR activation at all, while ten sets of 6-second maximal sprints completely abolished the mTOR signal. Lundberg showed that 45 minutes of cycling at 70% VO2max performed six hours before resistance exercise caused zero mTOR inhibition. AMPK returns to baseline within one to three hours after endurance exercise.

The practical implication: low-to-moderate intensity endurance — the bread and butter of mountain sport — poses minimal molecular interference risk. Your long Zone 1 ski tours are not killing your gains. High-intensity intervals and glycogen-depleted states cause the most interference. The modality matters too: running causes significantly more interference than cycling due to eccentric muscle damage. Ski touring and hiking are concentric-dominant on the ascent, which is actually an advantage.

For a recreational mountain athlete training three to five total sessions per week, interference is likely negligible for strength and hypertrophy, and small even for power. Program both modalities confidently.

### Session Spacing: The Most Actionable Lever

If the interference effect is dose-dependent and timing-dependent, then session spacing becomes your most powerful tool. Robineau's 2016 study with 58 rugby players over seven weeks tested three spacings: same-session, six-hour separation, and 24-hour separation. The results were unambiguous. Same-session training produced the worst strength outcomes. Six-hour separation was better but still suboptimal for maximal strength. Twenty-four-hour separation matched strength-only training outcomes and also produced the highest VO2peak gains.

Sporer and Wenger found that leg press work capacity remained diminished at both four and eight hours after endurance training, recovering fully only at 24 hours. And critically, the interference was localized to exercised muscles — upper body was unaffected after cycling.

When same-day training is unavoidable, the "strength first" rule applies. Two meta-analyses confirmed roughly a 7% advantage for lower-body strength when resistance training precedes endurance. Endurance gains are unaffected by either order. But when sessions are separated by three or more hours, order becomes less critical.

Imagine a typical Thursday during ski touring season. You have a gym session planned and an easy 60-minute bike ride. The ideal scenario: lift in the morning, ride in the evening, with at least six hours between. If that's impossible, lift at lunch and ride at dinner. If you can only do one session, lift — a single strength session protects more of your hard-won muscle than a single endurance session. The endurance can wait; the strength maintenance is time-sensitive.

For power-focused work — jump training, Olympic lifts — enforce at least 24 hours of separation from any endurance session. Power is the quality most vulnerable to interference, and there's no need to take the risk.

### Planning Your Year in Blocks

Block periodization is the preferred annual structure for concurrent mountain athletes because it leverages residual training effects. The key insight: aerobic endurance persists for about 30 days after you stop training it directly, and so does maximal strength. This means you can spend four weeks emphasizing strength, confident that your aerobic fitness won't meaningfully decay, then shift to emphasizing endurance for four weeks without losing the strength you just built.

Rønnestad's research showed that block periodization of strength and endurance produces superior adaptations to mixing both at equal emphasis every week. García-Pallarés demonstrated that block periodization achieved similar or better aerobic improvements with half the endurance volume in world-class kayakers.

The four-phase annual model for a winter mountain athlete looks like this:

**Off-season strength focus (June through September, roughly 8–12 weeks).** This is when you build. Three strength sessions per week, progressing from anatomical adaptation at lighter weights and higher reps, through hypertrophy at moderate weights, to max strength at heavy weights. You maintain two endurance sessions per week throughout — never drop endurance entirely — but one of those should be a genuine interval session to maintain your high-end aerobic capacity.

**Pre-season concurrent (October through November, 6–8 weeks).** Strength transitions to muscular endurance and sport-specific work: weighted uphill carries at 20% or more of bodyweight, one to two times per week. Endurance volume increases to four or five sessions per week with polarized distribution. Strength drops to two sessions per week. The key during this phase: don't train to failure. Izquierdo-Gabarren's research showed that stopping short of failure in concurrent training phases better preserves both strength and endurance adaptations.

**In-season sport priority (December through April, 16–20 weeks).** Your mountain activity becomes the primary training stimulus. Strength drops to maintenance: one heavy session every seven to ten days, never exceeding three consecutive weeks without heavy lifting. This is where those minimum-effective-dose rules from the prologue become critical. Your maintenance session might be just 25–40 minutes: 2–3 sets of squats at 80–85% max, 2–3 sets of Romanian deadlifts at 75–80%, 2–3 sets of pull-ups, and 2 sets of overhead press. Total: 8–11 sets, four exercises. That's enough to hold everything for months.

**Transition and recovery (May, 2–4 weeks).** Active rest, unstructured activity, nothing programmed. But keep it short — Mujika and Padilla showed that time to exhaustion drops 9–25% after just two weeks of complete detraining.

Within each phase, use 3+1 mesocycles: three weeks of progressive loading, one week of deload. And enforce the 10% weekly cap on volume progression.

### The Minimum Dose That Holds Everything Together

Let's be specific about what maintenance actually looks like, because this is where the rubber meets the road during your busiest touring months.

**Tier 1 — Standard in-season maintenance, one to two sessions per week, 25–40 minutes:** Back squat or front squat, 2–3 sets of 4–6 reps at 80–85% of your max, RPE 8–9. Romanian deadlift or hip thrust, 2–3 sets of 6–8 reps at 75–80%, RPE 7–8. Pull-ups or barbell rows, 2–3 sets of 6–8 reps. Overhead press or bench press, 2 sets of 6–8 reps. That's 8–11 total sets, four exercises, done in 40 minutes or less. This preserves your strength for months.

**Tier 2 — Peak touring or expedition mode, one session per week, 20–30 minutes:** Three compound exercises, 2 sets each, at your previous working loads. This is sustainable for up to 32 weeks based on Bickel's data. You lose nothing except perhaps some hypertrophy stimulus.

**Tier 3 — Taper or multi-day mountain objectives, zero sessions per week, maximum 2–3 weeks:** No strength training at all. The clock starts ticking at three weeks — if you pass that mark, schedule a session immediately upon return.

The three non-negotiables, always: keep intensity at or above 70% of your max, train at least once per week during extended endurance phases, and use compound movements exclusively during maintenance. Cut total sets by 67–89%, cut exercise count from six or eight down to three or four, eliminate all isolation work, and slash session duration from 60–90 minutes down to 20–40. But never reduce the weight on the bar.

Everything in this chapter has been about managing the interaction between two kinds of training. But there's a third variable that's at least as important as either of them: your body's ability to recover.

## Chapter 4: Recovery, Readiness, and Knowing When to Back Off

### Heart Rate Variability: Your Best Objective Signal

Heart rate variability — specifically the natural log of rMSSD, measured first thing every morning — is the single best objective marker of your autonomic recovery status. But only if you use it correctly, and most people don't.

The critical mistake is reacting to individual readings. A single morning HRV value tells you almost nothing. What matters is the trend: your seven-day rolling average compared to your personal baseline, and your coefficient of variation (CV) — the day-to-day scatter. When your weekly average is stable or rising and your CV is below 5–7%, you're in good shape. When the average starts declining while the CV climbs above 7–10%, you're accumulating more stress than you can absorb. And there's a particularly ominous pattern: when both the mean and the CV decline simultaneously — so-called "parasympathetic saturation" — that can signal non-functional overreaching.

The evidence for HRV-guided training is strong. Kiviniemi showed that athletes who modified their training based on HRV improved VO2peak significantly while a pre-planned group showed no change. A 2024 meta-analysis by Plews found that HRV-guided athletes achieved 4–7% better endurance outcomes over six months or more with 31% lower injury risk and reduced training volume — they trained smarter, not harder. Kurtz's study found that HRV-guided training achieved equivalent strength gains with significantly fewer high-intensity days. The benefit was greatest in recreational athletes balancing sport with work — which is exactly your profile.

Measure first thing upon waking, in a consistent seated position, for at least 60 seconds, with a validated device. Chest straps are the gold standard; some optical sensors are acceptable but less accurate. Natural breathing is fine for rMSSD. You need at least three valid data points per week for meaningful assessment. Discard recordings with more than 5% artifact.

The decision framework is simple. Green: your daily reading is within your individual smallest worthwhile change, weekly CV under 5–7%, mean stable or rising. Train as planned. Yellow: outside your SWC for one to two days, or weekly CV 7–10%. Swap planned high-intensity work for low-intensity or technique. Red: below SWC for three or more consecutive days, weekly CV above 10% with declining mean, or mean declining for two or more weeks. Prescribe rest, mobility, or very light Zone 1.

One important caveat: after heavy resistance training, expect HRV suppression for 24–72 hours. Hypertrophic loading — five sets of ten at 70% max — suppresses rMSSD by about 64%. Maximal strength loading suppresses it by about 29%. Don't flag this as a problem unless the suppression persists beyond 72 hours.

And HRV has a blind spot: it can't detect muscular soreness or mental fatigue. That's why you need subjective markers too.

### Sleep: The Non-Negotiable Recovery Tool

Sleep is the most powerful recovery modality available. Below seven hours, measurable decrements emerge across nearly every relevant system. A week of five-hour nights reduces testosterone by 10–15% — equivalent to 10–15 years of aging. A single night of total deprivation cuts muscle protein synthesis by 18% and elevates cortisol by 21%. 70–80% of daily growth hormone secretion happens during deep sleep.

The strength performance data is sobering. Craven's 2022 meta-analysis across 69 publications found an overall decrement of about 7.5% from sleep loss under six hours, with maximal strength down about 3%, strength endurance down about 10%, and skill-based performance down about 21%. Performance degrades roughly 0.4% per additional hour awake. Compound movements are more vulnerable than isolation.

A 2024 meta-analysis by Gong ranked performance domains by sleep vulnerability. High-intensity intermittent exercise is the most affected — effect size of negative 1.57. Skill-based performance is next at negative 1.06, then speed, then aerobic endurance, then explosive power. This ranking is directly actionable: if you slept badly, the session most worth rescheduling is your high-intensity interval workout. Moderate steady-state aerobic work is the most resilient to poor sleep.

The injury data is even more striking. Athletes sleeping under eight hours are 1.7 times more likely to get injured. Von Rosen found that athletes averaging over eight hours had 61% lower odds of new injury. The target during training phases is 8–9 hours.

Napping is a high-yield countermeasure when nighttime sleep is compromised. Mesas' 2023 meta-analysis in the British Journal of Sports Medicine found that 30–60 minute naps improve physical performance with an effect size of 0.99 and cognitive performance at 0.69. For mountain athletes who slept poorly in a hut at altitude, a mid-day nap before an afternoon training session is one of the best investments you can make.

And speaking of altitude: sleep quality drops dramatically above 2,000 meters. Periodic breathing — cycles of hyperventilation and apnea — begins around 2,000 meters. At 2,500–3,500 meters, about a quarter of people develop central sleep apnea, deep sleep is markedly reduced, and nocturnal hypoxemia becomes significant. Assume a 20–30% reduction in effective sleep quality for any night above 2,500 meters. This means extending your recovery window by 24–48 hours after altitude sleep and reducing planned training load by 20–30%.

### Subjective Markers: What the Devices Can't See

Here's something that might surprise you: a 2016 systematic review of 56 studies by Saw concluded that subjective self-reported measures are more sensitive and consistent than any commonly used objective marker for detecting daily training load responses and predicting injury risk. Your own sense of how you feel trumps your watch.

The practical implementation is a four-item daily wellness check, done each morning in under 30 seconds: rate your fatigue, soreness, sleep quality, and stress on a scale of 1 to 5. That's it. Normalize these against your own rolling 7–14-day baseline using Z-scores, and you have a signal that's more reliable than HRV for daily decisions.

Thorpe's research with Premier League soccer players found that perceived fatigue and soreness were "clearly more sensitive than ln rMSSD to daily fluctuations in training load," with very large correlations between wellness changes and load changes.

When subjective and objective signals conflict — and they will — apply the conservative rule: default to the worse signal for high-intensity decisions. If your HRV looks fine but you feel terrible, back off. The HRV can't see muscular damage or mental fatigue. If your HRV is suppressed but you feel great, proceed cautiously — chronic autonomic suppression may be an early warning your conscious mind hasn't registered yet.

Garmin's Body Battery and Training Readiness scores are useful directional data, but they have no published independent validation. A 2025 study found Body Battery didn't consistently align with self-reported recovery in Dutch police. And Training Readiness has a critical blind spot: strength training is poorly captured, so it systematically underestimates your fatigue after heavy lifting sessions. Use these scores as one data point among many, never as the sole decision-maker.

### Eating to Recover

For a concurrent strength and mountain endurance athlete, protein intake of 1.6–2.2 grams per kilogram of bodyweight per day, distributed across at least four meals at 0.4–0.55 grams per kilogram per meal, maximizes muscle protein synthesis. For a 75-kilogram athlete, that's 30–41 grams of protein per meal across four daily feedings.

The leucine threshold for triggering muscle protein synthesis is about 2.5 grams, which is found in roughly 20–25 grams of high-quality protein. Space your meals 3–5 hours apart to respect the muscle-full effect — MPS peaks at about 90–120 minutes and returns to baseline by 180 minutes despite sustained amino acids. A 40-gram casein protein bolus before sleep increases overnight MPS.

Carbohydrate needs are where things get interesting, because they scale dramatically with your training day:

On a light day — maybe a 30-minute recovery walk — 3–5 grams of carbs per kilogram is plenty. On a moderate day with a strength session or an easy tour, 5–7 grams per kilogram. On a high-volume day combining strength with a 3–6 hour tour, 7–10 grams per kilogram. On an all-day mountain objective — four or more hours — you need 8–12 grams per kilogram. At altitude, these numbers go up further because your body preferentially burns carbohydrate in hypoxic conditions.

For a 75-kilogram athlete, the difference between a rest day and a big mountain day is the difference between roughly 225 grams and 750 grams of carbohydrate — a threefold increase. Underfueling on mountain days is the primary driver of low T3 syndrome and relative energy deficiency in mountain athletes.

Post-exercise glycogen replenishment is optimized with 1.0–1.2 grams of high-glycemic carbs per kilogram per hour for the first four hours. If you can't hit that much carb, adding 0.3–0.4 grams of protein per kilogram per hour enhances glycogen storage. Full replenishment takes 24–48 hours with adequate daily intake, and a 2% bodyweight dehydration can reduce glycogen storage by 25–30%.

For supplements, the daily baseline recommendation is vitamin D at 2,000–5,000 IU with a fat-containing meal, magnesium glycinate at 200–400 mg before bed (it activates GABA, lowers cortisol, and promotes slow-wave sleep), and omega-3s at 2–3 grams per day of combined EPA and DHA. The ISSN's 2025 Position Stand confirmed that omega-3 effects take at least two weeks to manifest because they need to incorporate into cell membranes.

### Training When You're Sick

The traditional "neck check" — above-neck symptoms means train light, below-neck means rest — lacks supporting scientific evidence and may be hazardous. Harju's 2022 review in Sports Medicine recommended abandoning it entirely, because cardiogenic viruses can present with only upper respiratory symptoms. For mountain athletes, the safety margin is thinner still: even mild illness at altitude compounds immune stress and impairs judgment in avalanche terrain and during paraglider launches.

The conservative rule: if in doubt, rest. Any fever above 38°C means complete rest, no exceptions. Systemic symptoms — body aches, chills, fatigue, GI disturbance — mean complete rest. Below-neck symptoms — chest congestion, productive cough — mean complete rest. Only isolated mild nasal congestion with no fever, no fatigue, and no body aches permits light Zone 1 activity, indoors, flat, 30 minutes maximum. And no mountain-specific activity during any illness, period. Impaired judgment plus altitude plus cold plus technical terrain equals unacceptable safety risk.

Return to training only after 2–3 days completely symptom-free. For a mild cold lasting under five days, start back at 50% volume, Zone 1 only, and ramp to normal over about four days. For moderate illness with fever lasting 3–7 days, take a week to ramp back. For severe illness, plan on a three-week rebuild.

The most important psychological rule: illness should never trigger a "catch-up" mentality. Missed sessions are gone. Resume the plan where it would naturally be, reduced by one tier of volume.

### Training During a Caloric Deficit

Murphy and Koehler's 2022 meta-analysis delivered a finding that should reshape how you think about cutting weight: energy deficits impair lean mass gains but not strength gains during resistance training. The critical threshold is approximately 500 kcal/day — larger deficits prevented lean mass gains entirely. But strength gains were preserved regardless of deficit size.

The practical protocol: cap your deficit at about 500 kcal/day, or 0.5–0.7% of bodyweight loss per week. For a 75-kilogram athlete, that's 375–525 grams per week. Increase protein to 2.0–2.4 grams per kilogram per day — higher than during eucaloric phases. Maintain at least 3 grams of carbs per kilogram per day even during the cut, reducing fat preferentially.

Never schedule deficit phases during peak training season, planned multi-day objectives, altitude exposure, or high-volume endurance blocks. The optimal timing for a cut is the early off-season — May or June for a winter mountain athlete — when training demands are lowest.

During the deficit, the non-negotiable is maintaining strength training intensity at 80% of max or above. Reduce training volume by 20–30%, limit high-intensity endurance to once per week, and deload more frequently — subtract two weeks from your standard deload interval.

Monitor with weekly weigh-ins using a seven-day average, and monthly skinfold or waist measurements. If the rate exceeds 1% of bodyweight per week, reduce the deficit. If strength declines more than 5% across two or more sessions, exit the deficit.

## Chapter 5: Periodization — Organizing Your Year So It Actually Works

### The Annual Plan

Your training year follows a modified bi-cycle model — two distinct macrocycles, winter and summer, each with its own preparation, performance, and transition phases. Volume and intensity maintain an inverse relationship across phases: when volume goes up, average intensity comes down, and vice versa.

Let's map it out for a winter mountain athlete based in Innsbruck:

**May** is your transition. Two to three weeks of active recovery, mobility work, unstructured activity. Light general exercise, easy aerobic work, nothing programmed. This is genuinely important — your body and mind need the reset.

**June and July** are summer general preparation. This is your strength-building window — three sessions per week, progressing from hypertrophy to max strength. Endurance stays as high-volume Zone 1–2 hiking and cycling, building your aerobic base. This is when you're doing 12–20 sets per muscle group per week and eating in a slight surplus.

**August and September** shift to summer specific preparation. Strength transitions to muscular endurance and maintenance at two sessions per week. Endurance adds threshold work, weighted hikes, and hike-and-fly days. This is where you're doing sport-specific loaded uphill intervals.

**October** is another brief transition — two to three weeks. Deload from the summer, begin ski-specific preparation with eccentric leg emphasis. Brief recovery before the winter build begins.

**November and December** are winter general preparation. Max strength at two to three sessions per week with heavy eccentric work for descent preparation. Aerobic base building via ski touring, all Zone 1–2. Build your touring volume and vertical capacity.

**January and February** are winter specific preparation. Strength drops to maintenance at one to two sessions per week. This is your peak touring volume with muscular endurance sessions. The mountain sport itself becomes the primary training stimulus.

**March and April** are your performance months. Strength stays at maintenance. Taper before big objectives. Your touring is your training. Late season conditions often allow the longest and most ambitious days of the year.

The priority-shifting rule operates throughout: when endurance is the primary focus (in-season), reduce strength to one to two maintenance sessions per week at 75–85% max. When strength is the priority (off-season), limit high-intensity endurance to two sessions per week and keep the rest at Zone 1. Program recovery weeks every third or fourth week, with volume reduced to 40–50%.

### Mesocycle Design: The Building Blocks

Each mesocycle follows the accumulation-transmutation-realization sequence. Accumulation builds work capacity through high volume at moderate intensity. Transmutation converts that raw capacity into sport-specific fitness at higher intensity and lower volume. Realization sheds fatigue for peak performance at the lowest volume but highest intensity.

The default block length is four weeks: three weeks of progressive loading, one week of deload. Under high stress — caloric deficit, heavy work demands, high concurrent endurance volume — shorten to three-week blocks with a 2+1 structure. Only use five to six week blocks for novice trainees in pure strength focus.

The parameters shift by phase. During hypertrophy accumulation, you're working at 67–85% of max for sets of 6–12, with 60–120 seconds rest, accumulating 12–20+ sets per muscle group per week. During max strength intensification, it's 80–95% for sets of 1–6, with 3–5 minutes rest, at 6–12 sets per muscle group. During maintenance, 75–85% for sets of 3–6, with 2–3 minutes rest, at 4–8 sets per muscle group — and that's it for the entire in-season.

For endurance, base phases last 8–16 weeks with 80–90% of training in Zone 1–2 and zero to one high-intensity sessions per week. Build phases add two to three high-intensity sessions per week with threshold intervals and weighted uphill work. Peak phases reduce total volume while maintaining some intensity.

### Weekly Templates That Actually Work

Let me give you four concrete templates that cover the most common scenarios.

**Template A — The Minimum Effective Dose (4 days, for high-stress periods or deload weeks):** Monday is full-body strength — squat, hinge, press, pull, core. Tuesday is 60–90 minutes of Zone 1–2 cycling, hiking, or skiing. Wednesday is rest or active recovery and mobility. Thursday is full-body strength B — deadlift, lunge, overhead press, row. Friday through Sunday: one long endurance session (2–4 hour ski tour or hike) and two rest days, arranged as weather and life allow.

**Template B — Endurance Priority (5 days, in-season default):** Monday is full-body strength maintenance at 45 minutes — just 2–3 sets of heavy compounds. Tuesday is easy 60–90 minutes Zone 2. Wednesday is interval or threshold work, 45–60 minutes. Thursday is a second full-body strength session emphasizing weak links. Friday is rest. Saturday is your long mountain day — ski tour or hike-and-fly, 3–6 hours. Sunday is rest. Mountain days always count as endurance sessions. Don't add gym-based endurance on top.

**Template C — Strength Priority (5 days, off-season):** Monday is lower body strength — squat, deadlift, lunges, core. Tuesday is 60–90 minutes Zone 2 endurance. Wednesday is upper body — press, pull, rows, carries. Thursday is rest. Friday is full-body power — Olympic variations, plyometrics. Saturday is your long Zone 1–2 mountain day. Sunday is rest.

**Template D — Weekend Mountain Integration (in-season, when you have weekly objectives):** Monday is full-body strength, allowing 48 hours from Saturday's mountain day. Tuesday is rest or easy active recovery. Wednesday is a second full-body strength session. Thursday is moderate 45–60 minute Zone 2–3 endurance. Friday is rest or very light prep. Saturday is your big mountain day. Sunday is rest.

The selection rule: default to Template B during preparation phases, switch to Template D when you have weekly mountain objectives, use Template A during deload weeks or high-stress periods. Mountain days always replace gym-based endurance, never add to it.

### Tapering for Your Big Objectives

For your "A" goals — the objectives that matter most to you — a proper taper produces a mean performance improvement of about 3%, with a range of 0.5–6%. The research is clear on the three cardinal rules: reduce volume by up to 60–90%, maintain intensity, and reduce frequency by no more than 20%. The optimal taper is exponential, lasting about two weeks, with 41–60% volume reduction and no change in intensity or frequency.

Three to four weeks before your objective, train normally — you might even include a brief overreach of 10–20% above normal to provide the fatigue that subsequent tapering will dissipate. Two weeks out, reduce strength volume by 40–50% while keeping loads at or above 85% of max, and reduce endurance volume by about 60% while maintaining interval intensity. One week out, do one brief strength session early in the week and then stop. Endurance drops to 30–40% volume with short sessions that maintain terrain specificity. Two days out, nothing but active recovery. One day out, a brief 20–30 minute opener with a few short intensity bursts to prime the nervous system, plus carbohydrate pre-loading.

For regular weekend touring — not a taper situation. Just make sure Friday training is lighter. For "B" objectives, reduce volume about 30% for five to seven days before. Save real tapering for the days that genuinely matter.

### When the Plan Falls Apart: Schedule Disruption Management

Schedule disruptions — missed sessions due to work, travel, illness, unexpected weather windows — are the most common real-world challenge for recreational athletes. The cardinal rule is shift, don't skip: consolidate the highest-value training elements into available sessions rather than attempting to replicate missed ones or doubling up out of guilt.

Imagine this scenario: it's a busy week at work. You planned five sessions but can only manage two. What do you do? Identify the single highest-value session for your current phase. If you're in a strength-priority phase, that's your heavy compound lift session. If you're in an endurance-priority phase, it's your long endurance session. Get that one done. If you can fit a second session, add a complementary one. Do not try to cram five sessions into two by making each one absurdly long.

The detraining timelines should put your mind at ease. Missing one to three days has zero impact. Missing a full week: zero measurable impact on either strength or endurance. Resume at full intensity with 90% of pre-break volume. Missing two weeks: minimal — maybe 1–3% strength loss, 3–6% VO2max decline. Resume at full intensity, 80% volume, and take a deload after three weeks of rebuilding. Missing three to four weeks: moderate — 5–8% strength, 8–15% VO2max. You need a three-week ramp. More than four weeks: treat it as a return-to-training event with a four to six week ramp, starting at 50% volume.

The critical psychological point: consistency research shows that perceived failure to follow a plan is the strongest predictor of adherence collapse. Framing disruptions as normal, expected, and manageable — rather than failures — protects the thing that matters most over the long term, which is staying in the game.

One beautiful rule: unplanned mountain opportunities always take priority. If perfect weather opens up for a big objective, take it. The touring is training. Retrospectively classify it by HR, RPE, and vertical, integrate it into your weekly load, and adjust the remaining week's plan forward. Don't try to "make up" what was disrupted. Adjust forward, always.

## Chapter 6: Blood Work and Biomarkers — What Your Numbers Mean

### The Two Screenings That Matter Most

If you're going to do only two blood tests per year — and for most recreational mountain athletes, that's a reasonable approach — test ferritin and vitamin D. Screen in autumn and spring at minimum.

**Ferritin** is the rate-limiting mineral for oxygen transport and aerobic enzyme function. Clinical labs flag values below 12–15 ng/mL as abnormal, but athlete performance begins to suffer well above that. Iron depletion without anemia — ferritin below 30 ng/mL with normal hemoglobin — measurably impairs VO2max adaptation. Burden's 2015 meta-analysis found that iron supplementation in this population improves VO2max with a moderate effect size of 0.61. The optimal range for athletes is 50–100 ng/mL. The minimum performance threshold is 35–40.

For a mountain athlete training at altitude, ferritin is even more critical. Altitude drives a 3–5-fold increase in erythropoiesis, rapidly depleting iron stores. If your ferritin is below 40 before you start spending consistent time above 2,000 meters, your body can't capitalize on the altitude stimulus. Screen 8–10 weeks before any planned altitude-heavy period.

If your ferritin is below 35, initiate oral supplementation: 100 mg of elemental iron per day, ideally as ferrous sulfate or iron bisglycinate. Morning dosing is preferred, ideally within 30 minutes after exercise when hepcidin — the hormone that blocks iron absorption — is still low. Alternate-day dosing shows comparable efficacy with fewer GI side effects. Recheck at 6–8 weeks. If oral supplementation fails to raise ferritin above 35 after two months, IV iron becomes worth discussing with your doctor.

**Vitamin D** deficiency affects 33–90% of athletes at latitudes above 35° in winter — and Innsbruck, at 47° north, is right in that zone. The optimal range for athletes is 40–60 ng/mL. Peak neuromuscular function occurs around 50. Below 30, stress fracture risk jumps 3.6 times. Below 20, respiratory infection risk increases substantially.

During the long alpine winter with limited sun exposure and heavy clothing coverage, assume insufficiency unless you're supplementing. The default maintenance recommendation is 1,000–2,000 IU per day year-round. If you test below 30, increase to 2,000–4,000 IU daily for 8–10 weeks and recheck. Below 20, go to 4,000–6,000 IU for 8–12 weeks with medical follow-up.

### The Full Panel: What to Test and When

The recommended comprehensive panel for a mountain athlete includes: complete blood count with differential, full iron panel (ferritin, serum iron, transferrin saturation, TIBC), vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D), thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4), high-sensitivity CRP, morning cortisol, testosterone (total and free), metabolic panel, liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT), creatine kinase, and creatinine with eGFR — the last one is essential if you're taking creatine.

Timing matters for accuracy. Blood draws should happen between 7:00 and 10:00 AM, fasted 8–12 hours, with at least 48 hours since your last strenuous session — and ideally 7 or more days since heavy resistance training if you want accurate CK and liver enzyme readings. Sit for 15 minutes before the draw. Be well-hydrated. And if you're taking biotin supplements above 1,000 mcg, stop them 3–5 days before testing — biotin interferes with TSH and thyroid hormone immunoassays.

For routine screening in a healthy athlete, once or twice per year is sufficient. During intensified training blocks, check ferritin and hs-CRP every 4–6 weeks. When tracking a specific issue — iron repletion, vitamin D recovery, hormonal concerns — follow the issue-specific intervals: every 6–8 weeks for iron on oral supplementation, 8–12 weeks for vitamin D repletion, 4–8 weeks for hormonal recovery.

### Interpreting the Numbers

**Testosterone-to-cortisol ratio:** A 30% decline from your personal baseline has traditionally been considered a threshold for excessive stress. But the ECSS/ACSM consensus clarified that this indicates strain, not diagnosis. Transient 30% drops are normal after heavy exercise and resolve within 24–48 hours. Only persistent declines at rest are concerning. Important context: long-term endurance training naturally reduces testosterone by 20–40% — this is a stable adaptation, not pathology.

**High-sensitivity CRP:** Trained athletes typically run very low — 0.1–0.5 mg/L. After a marathon, CRP can spike 2,000%. But at rest, more than 48 hours after exercise, values above 1.0 mg/L warrant a training load review, above 3.0 warrant volume reduction and investigation, and above 10.0 suggest acute illness and require medical evaluation.

**Thyroid markers:** Heavy training frequently produces "low T3 syndrome" — low free T3, elevated reverse T3, with near-normal TSH and T4. This is a hypothalamic adaptation to energy deficit, not thyroid disease, and it does not warrant thyroid hormone replacement. The critical question is whether the low T3 reflects acceptable adaptation or problematic low energy availability. If free T3 drops more than 15% from baseline or falls below 2.5 pg/mL, assess your energy intake versus expenditure. The most likely cause in a mountain athlete is simply not eating enough on big training days.

**Energy availability** — calories consumed minus exercise expenditure, divided by fat-free mass — should never drop below 30 kcal per kilogram of fat-free mass per day. For a 75-kilogram male at 12% body fat with roughly 66 kilograms of fat-free mass, that's a minimum daily intake of about 1,980 kcal even on a cut. The 2023 IOC consensus now explicitly recognizes relative energy deficiency in males — this isn't just a female athlete issue.

### Creatine: What It Does to Your Blood Work

If you're taking creatine monohydrate — and the evidence strongly suggests you should be — your creatinine levels will be elevated, and this is not a kidney problem. Creatine spontaneously converts to creatinine at a rate of about 1.7% of body stores per day. Supplementation increases intramuscular creatine by 20–40%, proportionally increasing creatinine production. On maintenance dosing of 3–5 grams per day, expect your serum creatinine to be 0.1–0.3 mg/dL above baseline. Values of 1.2–1.5 mg/dL are common and expected in creatine users.

Naeini's 2025 meta-analysis across 21 studies found a pooled creatinine increase of 0.07 µmol/L — clinically trivial — with no significant change in GFR. Gualano's study actually found that cystatin C — the alternative kidney marker unaffected by creatine — decreased during 12 weeks of supplementation. If your creatinine is above 1.5 on creatine, request a cystatin C test before worrying. The true red flags are creatinine rising progressively after your dose has stabilized, proteinuria, or a cystatin-C-based eGFR that's also reduced.

The case for year-round creatine supplementation goes beyond strength. A 2024 meta-analysis by Xu found significant cognitive improvements — memory, attention, and processing speed — with effects most pronounced under sleep deprivation and cognitive stress. For a mountain athlete making safety-critical decisions in avalanche terrain, route-finding in poor visibility, and paraglider launch assessments while physiologically fatigued, at altitude, and potentially sleep-deprived, this cognitive benefit is directly relevant. The brain consumes about 20% of the body's ATP at rest, and creatine contributes to brain phosphocreatine reserves.

For endurance specifically, creatine is ineffective for steady-state VO2max. But Forbes' 2023 review found it increases time to exhaustion during high-intensity endurance and benefits performances requiring multiple surges, pace changes, and finishing kicks — which matches the variable-intensity demand of ski touring with steep skin tracks, splitboarding with repeated climbs, and hike-and-fly racing with rapid ascents.

The body mass increase of 1–2 kilograms is a potential concern for weight-sensitive activities, but a study on 8% incline climbing at 90% VO2max found no detrimental effect from creatine-related mass gain. For most mountain athletes, the combined strength, cognitive, and variable-intensity endurance benefits outweigh the small mass penalty.

The dosing protocol: either load at 0.3 g/kg/day (typically 20 grams in four 5-gram doses) for 5–7 days, then maintain at 3–5 grams per day, or simply start at 3 grams per day and reach the same saturation after about 28 days. Co-ingest with carbohydrate and protein. Post-exercise timing is slightly superior. Use creatine monohydrate exclusively — no other form has demonstrated superiority despite decades of marketing claims. Antonio's 2025 update by 20 leading creatine researchers reaffirmed monohydrate's safety profile at up to 30 grams per day for five years in healthy individuals.

### Red Flags: When to Modify Training and When to See a Doctor

The system uses a three-tier alert structure.

**Tier 1 — Training modification triggers** include ferritin below 35 (reduce high-intensity volume, start supplementation), hemoglobin below 13.5 (reduce intensity, investigate iron), total testosterone below 400 (assess energy availability, reduce load by 30%), T:C ratio declining more than 20% from baseline (increase recovery), hs-CRP persistently above 3.0 (reduce volume), free T3 dropping more than 15% (increase caloric intake), CK above 1,000 at rest more than 72 hours post-exercise (reduce eccentric loading), or white blood cells persistently below 3.5.

**Tier 2 — Medical referral triggers** include ferritin below 12 with low hemoglobin (urgent — IV iron), ferritin above 300 (hemochromatosis screening), hemoglobin below 11.0 (urgent evaluation), testosterone persistently below 300 (endocrine workup), cortisol outside 3–23 µg/dL range (adrenal evaluation), hs-CRP above 10.0 at rest (medical evaluation), TSH above 10 or below 0.1 (endocrine referral), vitamin D below 10 (supervised repletion), CK above 5,000 (rhabdomyolysis workup), or creatinine above 2.0 or acutely rising (nephrology referral — stop creatine).

**Tier 3 — Supplementation triggers** include ferritin below 50 (dietary optimization plus 65–100 mg elemental iron daily), vitamin D below 30 (supplementation at appropriate dose for severity), and low T3 with normal TSH (increase energy intake by 300–500 kcal/day, target energy availability above 30 kcal/kg FFM).

Always interpret multiple markers together. Two or more markers trending in the same direction demand immediate action — a declining ferritin paired with rising CRP and falling T3 is a much louder alarm than any single abnormal value.

## Epilogue: What We Don't Know Yet

Every evidence-based system has edges where the evidence thins out. Honesty about these gaps is what separates good coaching from dogma.

**Mobility and flexibility training** has no domain-specific coverage in this system. We don't have clear answers on the minimum mobility routine that prevents injury in mountain athletes with heavy eccentric loading, how mobility should be periodized alongside strength and endurance, or whether mobility work helps recovery or simply adds fatigue. This is a gap that will be addressed in a future version.

**Wearable device validation** is another soft spot. The system relies heavily on Garmin metrics — Body Battery, Training Readiness, HRV Status — but none of these have published independent validation against clinical outcomes. The optical HRV on Garmin watches hasn't been rigorously compared to chest-strap rMSSD for the specific use cases that matter here. Use them as directional data, not gospel.

**Sport-specific strength transfer** lacks hard evidence. Is there a squat-to-bodyweight ratio that predicts ski touring performance? How do unilateral strength deficits affect mountain sport injury risk? What's the optimal eccentric training protocol for descent preparation? We're working from plausible extrapolation, not direct evidence.

**Hike-and-fly specific evidence** is nearly nonexistent in the scientific literature. Almost everything is extrapolated from military load carriage, trail running, or general hiking. No studies have measured the physiological demands of competitive hike-and-fly events, the effect of fatigue on paraglider launch safety margins, or the optimal training periodization for a hike-and-fly season.

**Nutrition periodization** is partially addressed but has gaps around practical meal planning for split mountain and gym days, intra-workout nutrition protocols specific to cold environments, and the long-term effects of high-protein diets combined with creatine on kidney markers.

**Environmental factors beyond altitude** — cold exposure effects on gym performance, UV-driven vitamin D synthesis at altitude with snow reflection, wind chill effects on energy expenditure, and seasonal affective disorder impacts on training adherence in alpine locations — all lack systematic coverage.

And the **psychological and cognitive domain** may be the most important gap of all. Decision-making quality under physiological fatigue is critical for safety in avalanche terrain, paragliding, and exposed mountain travel. How do you distinguish motivational fatigue from physical overtraining in an automated system? How do you maintain long-term adherence to AI-coached training? The creatine cognitive benefit evidence addresses part of this, and the schedule disruption management protocols address another part, but a full treatment remains for future work.

These gaps don't invalidate the system. They define its boundaries. Where evidence is thin, the system should be conservative, use the best available extrapolation, and flag its uncertainty. Where evidence is strong — and across the core domains of strength programming, endurance training, concurrent training management, recovery, periodization, and biomarker interpretation, the evidence base is very strong — it should act with confidence.

Every number in this guide is a population-level starting estimate. Your individual response will differ. The real magic of the Ascent system isn't in any single threshold or protocol — it's in the closed loop that collects your data, compares it to these evidence-based starting points, and progressively personalizes everything to you. The science provides the map. Your body provides the territory. The system learns to navigate one from the other.
