# Strength training programming: a scientific knowledge base for AI coaching

**This knowledge base distills the strongest available evidence on seven core programming variables into actionable decision rules for an AI coaching system.** Each section follows a consistent structure: core principle, quantified evidence, application guideline, and key sources. All volume prescriptions assume “hard working sets” (0–4 RIR, 5–30 reps, 30–85% 1RM). All recommendations assume adequate protein intake (1.6–2.2 g/kg/day) and sufficient sleep (7–9 hours).

-----

## 1. Progressive overload models

### Core principle

Progressive overload — systematically increasing training demands over time — is the single most fundamental driver of adaptation. The optimal overload model depends on training age: novices respond to simple linear load increases every session, intermediates benefit from double progression or undulating schemes, and advanced trainees require block periodization with autoregulated loading. **No periodization model is dramatically superior to another when volume is equated**; the primary evidence is that any structured periodization outperforms non-periodized training (effect size 0.43–0.84).

### Evidence summary

**Linear progression** works via session-to-session load increases of **2.5–5 lbs (upper body) and 5–10 lbs (lower body)**  for novices. The ACSM Position Stand (2009) recommends a 2–10% load increase when the trainee can perform 1–2 reps beyond the target RM. Linear progression typically stalls after **3–6 months** of consistent training as neural adaptations plateau.

**Double progression** increases reps within a fixed range before increasing load. A standard protocol: train at a weight until all sets hit the top of the range (e.g., 3×12), then increase load by the smallest available increment and resume at the bottom of the range (e.g., 3×8).  Tighter rep ranges (2-rep gaps like 4–6) suit barbell compounds; wider ranges (4–6-rep gaps like 8–15) suit isolation and machine exercises where percentage jumps are smaller.

**Undulating periodization (DUP)** varies intensity/volume within each week. A typical structure: Day 1 heavy (4–6 sets × 3–5 reps @ 85–90% 1RM), Day 2 moderate (3–4 sets × 8–12 reps @ 65–80%), Day 3 light (3–4 sets × 12–15 reps @ 50–65%). The Moesgaard et al. (2022) meta-analysis found DUP favored over linear periodization for 1RM strength in trained participants **(ES = 0.61, p = 0.04)** but showed no difference for hypertrophy.  The Williams et al. (2017) meta-analysis confirmed periodized > non-periodized (ES = 0.43), with undulating models slightly more favorable.  Stronger By Science re-analysis estimated undulating periodization produced strength gains **~28% faster** than linear (weekly gains: 2.19–2.24% vs. 1.57–1.58%). 

**Block periodization** sequences mesocycles with concentrated training emphases: Accumulation (hypertrophy, 50–70% 1RM, high volume, 2–6 weeks) → Transmutation (strength, 75–90% 1RM, moderate volume, 2–4 weeks) → Realization (peaking, 90%+ 1RM, low volume, 1–3 weeks). The rationale relies on residual training effects — hypertrophy adaptations persist ~30 days, enabling them to carry over into subsequent strength and peaking phases.

**Progression rates by training level:**

|Level       |Training age|Progression rate                       |Frequency           |
|------------|------------|---------------------------------------|--------------------|
|Novice      |0–6 months  |5–10 lbs/session lower, 2.5–5 lbs upper|Every session       |
|Late novice |6–12 months |5 lbs/session → weekly                 |Weekly              |
|Intermediate|1–3 years   |5–10 lbs/month on major lifts          |Every 1–3 weeks     |
|Advanced    |3+ years    |2.5–5 lbs/month or per mesocycle       |Monthly or per cycle|

### Application guideline

The AI coach should select the progression model based on training age. For novices (<6 months), default to linear progression with session-to-session load increases. When a trainee fails to progress for 2 consecutive sessions at the same load, transition to double progression. For intermediates (1–3 years), implement DUP or weekly undulating periodization with RPE-based autoregulation; expect weekly-to-biweekly load progression. For advanced trainees (3+ years), use block periodization with mesocycles of 3–6 weeks, progressing volume within each block and load across blocks. Always cap weekly load/volume increases at **≤10%**.

- Harries, Lubans & Callister (2015). “Systematic review and meta-analysis of linear and undulating periodised resistance training programmes on muscular strength.” *JSCR*, 29(4), 1113–1125.
- Williams et al. (2017). “The effect of periodized resistance training on strength.” *Sports Medicine*, 47, 2083–2100.

## 2. Volume landmarks per muscle group

### Core principle

Training volume — measured in hard working sets per muscle group per week — follows a dose-response curve with **diminishing returns**. Three landmarks define the actionable range: **MEV** (Minimum Effective Volume, the floor for measurable growth), **MAV** (Maximum Adaptive Volume, the zone of best returns), and **MRV** (Maximum Recoverable Volume, the ceiling beyond which recovery fails).  A mesocycle should start near MEV and progressively ramp toward MRV over 4–6 weeks, then deload.   These landmarks shift upward with training experience as muscles become harder to stimulate but work capacity increases.

### Evidence summary

The Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2017) meta-analysis established the dose-response relationship: each additional weekly set produced an effect size increase of **0.023, corresponding to +0.37% muscle size gain**.  The categorical analysis showed **10+ sets/week** produced the greatest hypertrophy.  Krieger (2010) found multiple sets produced **40% greater hypertrophy effect sizes** than single sets, with a dose-response pattern: ES of 0.24 (1 set), 0.34 (2–3 sets), 0.44 (4–6 sets per exercise).   Baz-Valle et al. (2022) found **12–20 weekly sets per muscle group** to be the optimal range,  with 20+ sets only benefiting certain muscles (triceps, ES = −0.50).   The most comprehensive analysis — Pelland et al. (2025, *Sports Medicine*, 67 studies, 2,058 participants) — found the volume-hypertrophy relationship best fit by a **square root model** (diminishing returns with no clear ceiling),  with substantial diminishing returns beyond ~12–20 sets. 

**Israetel/Renaissance Periodization volume landmarks (intermediate-level estimates, direct sets per week):**

|Muscle group   |MV|MEV|MAV range|MRV|Frequency|
|---------------|--|---|---------|---|---------|
|Quads          |6 |8  |12–18    |20+|1.5–3×/wk|
|Hamstrings     |4 |6  |10–16    |20+|2–3×/wk  |
|Glutes         |0 |0  |4–12     |16+|2–3×/wk  |
|Chest          |8 |10 |12–20    |22+|1.5–3×/wk|
|Back           |8 |10 |14–22    |25+|2–4×/wk  |
|Side/rear delts|0 |8  |16–22    |26+|2–6×/wk  |
|Front delts    |0 |0  |6–8      |12+|1–2×/wk  |
|Biceps         |5 |8  |14–20    |26+|2–6×/wk  |
|Triceps        |4 |6  |10–14    |18+|2–4×/wk  |
|Calves         |6 |8  |12–16    |20+|2–4×/wk  |
|Abs            |0 |0  |16–20    |25+|3–5×/wk  |
|Traps          |0 |0  |12–20    |26+|2–6×/wk  |

MV/MEV of 0 means the muscle receives sufficient indirect stimulation from compound work to maintain or even grow without dedicated isolation.  These values shift with training level: **beginners** need ~6–10 sets/muscle/week within MAV and can sustain accumulation for up to 12 weeks; **advanced trainees** may require 15–25+ sets/week and reach MRV within 3–4 weeks.  

### Application guideline

The AI coach should initialize volume at each muscle group’s MEV estimate adjusted for training level (beginners start lower, advanced start higher). Progress by **adding 1–3 sets per muscle group per week** across the mesocycle.  Monitor recovery signals weekly: if soreness resolves before the next session and performance improves, add 2–3 sets; if performance stagnates, hold volume steady; if performance declines for 2+ sessions, initiate a deload to MV (~6 sets/muscle). When per-session volume exceeds **~10 sets for a single muscle group**, increase training frequency rather than session volume. Individual variation is substantial (±30–50% based on genetics, sleep, stress, nutrition), so treat all numbers as starting estimates requiring personalized adjustment.

## 3. Intensity programming and RPE autoregulation

### Core principle

Intensity can be prescribed via fixed percentages of 1RM or autoregulated via RPE/RIR (Rate of Perceived Exertion / Reps in Reserve). Percentage-based training provides structure and predictability but cannot account for daily readiness fluctuations.  RPE-based autoregulation naturally adjusts load to the trainee’s current capacity, producing **comparable or slightly superior strength outcomes**. The hybrid approach — using percentages for planning and RPE for daily adjustment — is the practical optimum for intermediates and advanced trainees. **Proximity to failure matters more for hypertrophy than for strength**: most hypertrophy sets should terminate at 1–3 RIR, while strength sets can be effective across a wider RIR range. 

### Evidence summary

The RIR-based RPE scale, formalized by **Zourdos et al. (2016)** and popularized by Mike Tuchscherer, maps cleanly to reps in reserve: RPE 10 = 0 RIR (failure), RPE 9 = 1 RIR, RPE 8 = 2 RIR, RPE 7 = 3 RIR.  Halperin et al. (2022) meta-analyzed 13 publications (414 participants) and found trainees underpredict RIR by **~0.95 repetitions** on average.   Accuracy improves closer to failure and with lower-rep sets (≤12 reps).  Notably, training status did **not** significantly predict accuracy in this meta-analysis,  though Zourdos et al. (2016) found experienced squatters reported more accurate RPE at 1RM (9.80 ± 0.18 vs. 8.96 ± 0.43 for novices, p = 0.023). Refalo et al. (2023) found trained individuals were within **0.40 (±0.68) reps of a 1-RIR target** and 0.90 (±0.81) reps of a 3-RIR target.  

For autoregulation outcomes, **Mann et al. (2010)** found APRE significantly outperformed linear periodization in bench press 1RM (+93.4 N vs. −0.40 N, p = 0.02) and squat 1RM (+192.7 N vs. +37.2 N, p = 0.05) over 6 weeks.  **Graham & Cleather (2021)** showed autoregulated training produced significantly greater strength gains than fixed loading: front squat **+11.7% vs. +8.3%** (p = 0.004), back squat **+10.8% vs. +7.1%** (p = 0.006) over 12 weeks. 

**RPE/RIR targets by goal:**

|Goal                |Optimal RPE|Optimal RIR|Evidence basis                                                                                                                                                |
|--------------------|-----------|-----------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Hypertrophy         |7–9        |1–3        |Robinson et al. (2024): hypertrophy increases non-linearly closer to failure; Refalo et al. (2023): failure not superior to non-failure (ES = 0.12, p = 0.343)|
|Strength            |7–10       |0–3        |Robinson et al. (2024): strength gains similar across wide RIR range                                                                                          |
|Most training volume|7–8        |2–3        |Minimizes fatigue accumulation; 1-RIR shows ~13% velocity loss vs. failure ~25%                                                                               |
|Peaking/testing     |9.5–10     |0–1        |Reserved for competition or 1RM attempts                                                                                                                      |

### Application guideline

For novices (<1 year), use fixed percentages or double progression — RPE accuracy is insufficient for reliable autoregulation. Introduce RPE after **6–12 months** of training with a 2–3 week familiarization period where RPE is tracked alongside percentage-based work.   For intermediates and advanced trainees, prescribe working sets with target RPE ranges (e.g., “4×6 @ RPE 8”) and allow the trainee to self-select load. Use the hybrid approach for structured peaking cycles: plan loads as percentages, adjust ±5% based on daily RPE. For in-season athletes or post-injury trainees, default to full autoregulation. Account for the systematic ~1 rep RIR underprediction by programming most hypertrophy volume at RPE 7–8 (the trainee is likely actually at RPE 8–9).

- Halperin et al. (2022). “Prediction of repetitions to failure from bar velocity during the set.” *Sports Medicine*, 52(2), 377–390.

## 4. Training frequency as a volume distribution tool

### Core principle

Training frequency per muscle group primarily matters as a **vehicle for distributing weekly volume**, not as an independent growth variable. When total weekly volume is equated, training a muscle 2×/week vs. 3×/week produces nearly identical hypertrophy.   However, higher frequency may benefit **strength** through improved motor learning on compound lifts. The practical minimum is **2×/week per muscle group** for hypertrophy; the practical maximum is determined by how many sessions are needed to distribute the target weekly volume without exceeding ~10 sets per muscle per session.

### Evidence summary

The landmark **Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2016)** meta-analysis (10 studies) found **2×/week promotes superior hypertrophy to 1×/week** (significant effect when pooling all measures). Whether 3×/week provides additional benefit remained undetermined.  The updated **Schoenfeld, Grgic & Krieger (2019)** meta-analysis (25 studies) found **no significant difference** between higher and lower frequencies on a volume-equated basis  (p > 0.05 for all subanalyses). The conclusion: “Individuals can choose weekly frequency per muscle group based on personal preference.” 

For strength, **Grgic et al. (2018)** found a significant overall effect of frequency (p = 0.003), with effect sizes rising from 0.74 (1×/wk) to 0.82 (2×/wk) to 0.93 (3×/wk) to 1.08 (4+×/wk).  However, **when volume was equated, the effect disappeared** (p = 0.421) — indicating the strength benefit is mediated primarily through additional volume,  with a possible secondary motor-learning component for multi-joint exercises (significant for compounds, p < 0.001; not for isolation, p = 0.324). 

The **Pelland et al. (2025)** meta-regression confirmed: frequency has a **negligible independent effect on hypertrophy** but a **consistently identifiable positive effect on strength**.  Muscle protein synthesis data from Damas et al. (2015) shows MPS elevation lasts only **~24–48 hours** in trained individuals (shorter than in untrained),   theoretically supporting 2–4×/week to maximize cumulative anabolic signaling  — though the practical literature shows this doesn’t translate to meaningful hypertrophy differences beyond 2×/week.

### Application guideline

Default to **2×/week** per muscle group as the base frequency. Increase to 3×/week when weekly volume exceeds ~12–14 sets for a muscle group and per-session quality degrades. For small, recovery-resilient muscles (side delts, biceps, calves, abs), 3–6×/week is viable and may be necessary to distribute high MRV volumes. For large, fatiguing muscle groups (quads, back), cap at 2–3×/week with ≥48 hours between sessions. For strength-focused programming on compound lifts, consider 3–4×/week on the target lift to enhance motor learning. Match split type to available training days:

- **3 days/week** → Full body (each muscle 3×/wk)
- **4 days/week** → Upper/Lower (each muscle 2×/wk)
- **5–6 days/week** → Push/Pull/Legs (each muscle 2×/wk)

### Key sources

- Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2016). “Effects of resistance training frequency on measures of muscle hypertrophy.” *Sports Medicine*, 46(11), 1689–1697.
- Schoenfeld, Grgic & Krieger (2019). “How many times per week should a muscle be trained to maximize muscle hypertrophy?” *Journal of Sports Sciences*, 37(11), 1286–1295.
- Grgic et al. (2018). “Effect of resistance training frequency on gains in muscular strength.” *Sports Medicine*, 48(5), 1207–1220.
- Ralston et al. (2018). “Weekly training frequency effects on strength gain: a meta-analysis.” *Sports Medicine — Open*, 4(1), 36.
- Pelland et al. (2025). “The resistance training dose response.” *Sports Medicine*, 56(2), 481–505.
- Damas et al. (2015). “A review of resistance training-induced changes in skeletal muscle protein synthesis.” *Sports Medicine*, 45, 801–807.

## 5. Deload protocols and supercompensation

### Core principle

A deload is a planned period of **reduced training stress (typically 5–7 days) designed to dissipate accumulated fatigue while preserving fitness**. The preferred method is a volume reduction deload — cutting weekly sets by 40–60% while maintaining load — because this preserves neural adaptations and technical proficiency. Deloads should be planned proactively every 4–6 weeks for intermediates  and every 3–4 weeks for advanced trainees,  with reactive triggers available to override the schedule when fatigue markers deteriorate sooner.

### Evidence summary

The **Bell et al. (2023)** international Delphi consensus (experts across strength/physique sports) agreed that deloads should reduce volume through fewer sets, fewer reps, or reduced frequency, while intensity either stays the same or decreases by **~10% 1RM**.  The **Rogerson et al. (2024)** cross-sectional survey of 246 competitive athletes found the average deload occurred every **5.6 ± 2.3 weeks** and lasted **6.4 ± 1.7 days**. Athletes commonly reduced both reps per set and total weekly sets. 

**Coleman et al. (2024)** tested a 1-week complete training cessation mid-program and found it **negatively impacted lower body strength** (~6% lower improvement) but had **no effect on hypertrophy, power, or muscular endurance** — confirming that active deloads are preferable to full rest for strength maintenance.

The theoretical basis comes from the **Banister Fitness-Fatigue Model**: performance = fitness − fatigue. The fatigue after-effect has a short time constant  (**τ₂ ≈ 7–11 days**) while the fitness after-effect persists much longer (**τ₁ ≈ 20–50 days**). During a deload, fatigue dissipates rapidly while fitness persists, producing a performance “supercompensation” effect. Tapering research shows **~2–8% improvement in maximal strength** from an effective taper.

**Deload prescription summary:**

|Parameter         |Recommendation                                         |
|------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
|Duration          |5–7 days (1 week)                                      |
|Volume reduction  |40–60% fewer total sets                                |
|Intensity/load    |Maintain or reduce ≤10%                                |
|Frequency         |Maintain or slightly reduce                            |
|RPE target        |5–7 (3–5 RIR)                                          |
|Exercise selection|Keep primary movement patterns; swap accessories freely|

**Deload frequency by training level:** Beginners every 8–12 weeks (low fatigue accumulation); intermediates every 4–6 weeks;  advanced every 3–4 weeks.   Common mesocycle ratios: 3:1 (advanced/aggressive), **4:1 (most common for intermediates)**, 5:1–7:1 (beginners/conservative).

### Application guideline

The AI coach should schedule proactive deloads based on training level and mesocycle length, but implement reactive override triggers: if RPE creeps >1 point at the same load for 2+ sessions, if wellness scores decline for 2+ consecutive weeks, or if HRV mean drops >1 SD from baseline for 1+ week — trigger an immediate deload regardless of schedule position. Default to volume-reduction deloads (cut sets by 50%, maintain load).  Switch to combined reduction (cut load 10% AND sets 50%) when joint pain is elevated. After the deload week, restart the next mesocycle at MEV with potentially new exercise selections.

### Key sources

- Bell et al. (2023). “Integrating deloading into strength and physique sports training programmes: an international Delphi consensus approach.” *Sports Medicine — Open*, 9:87.
- Bell et al. (2025). “A practical approach to deloading.” *Strength & Conditioning Journal*.
- Coleman et al. (2024). “Gaining more from doing less? The effects of a one-week deload period during supervised resistance training.” *PeerJ*, 12:e16777.
- Rogerson et al. (2024). “Deloading practices in strength and physique sports.” *Sports Medicine — Open*.
- Chiu & Barnes (2003). “The fitness-fatigue model revisited.” *Strength & Conditioning Journal*, 25(6).

## 6. Fatigue management and the stimulus-to-fatigue ratio

### Core principle

Fatigue in resistance training exists on two axes: **systemic** (CNS, hormonal, metabolic) and **local** (muscle damage, joint stress, connective tissue).  These recover at different rates — acute neuromuscular fatigue resolves in 48–72 hours, while joint and connective tissue stress accumulates over weeks and requires a full deload to dissipate. The **Stimulus-to-Fatigue Ratio (SFR)** — how much adaptive stimulus an exercise provides relative to the fatigue it generates  — should guide exercise selection, especially during high-volume hypertrophy phases.  True overtraining syndrome from resistance training alone is **extremely rare**; most “overtraining” cases involve concurrent non-training stressors or nutritional insufficiency.  

### Evidence summary

**Fatigue recovery timelines:**

|Type                    |Peak                           |Resolution                 |
|------------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------------|
|Acute neuromuscular     |0–24 hours                     |48–72 hours                |
|Muscle damage (DOMS)    |24–72 hours                    |3–7 days                   |
|CNS voluntary activation|0–24 hours                     |24–48 hours                |
|Joint/connective tissue |Gradual accumulation over weeks|Requires deload week(s)    |
|Hormonal disruption     |Days to weeks                  |1–4 weeks with reduced load|

**SFR classification** (concept popularized by Israetel/RP): High-SFR exercises (machine hack squat, chest-supported row, cable lateral raise, leg extension) provide strong target-muscle stimulus with low systemic fatigue. Low-SFR exercises (conventional deadlift, heavy barbell back squat, bent-over row) generate massive systemic fatigue relative to single-muscle stimulus.  For hypertrophy, **high-SFR exercises allow more productive weekly volume** because they don’t eat into recovery budgets. 

**Overreaching classification** from the **Meeusen et al. (2013) ECSS/ACSM Joint Consensus**: Functional overreaching (FOR) causes performance decrements lasting days to ~2 weeks that rebound with supercompensation. Non-functional overreaching (NFOR) causes decrements lasting weeks to months with no subsequent supercompensation. Overtraining syndrome (OTS) causes decrements lasting months to years. Critically, **Bell et al. (2020)** found minimal evidence that true OTS has ever occurred from resistance training alone.   Rogerson et al. (2024) found that **92.5% of training maladaptation cases** were accompanied by additional non-training stressors. 

**Fatigue detection markers for monitoring:** RPE drift (same weights feeling harder week-to-week), performance decrements at matched RPE, grip strength decreases, HRV drops (daily morning RMSSD declining >1 SD from personal baseline), declining motivation, increased joint discomfort, and disrupted sleep. Williams et al. found HRV measures upon waking sensitive to changes in resistance training loads across microcycles. 

### Application guideline

The AI coach should anchor hypertrophy programs around **high-SFR exercises** (machines, cables, chest-supported variations) for volume accumulation, reserving low-SFR compounds (barbell squat, deadlift, bent-over row) for strength blocks where their total-body loading is the goal. Within each session, order exercises from highest to lowest neural demand. Track fatigue via a composite score: RPE creep at matched loads, session wellness ratings (sleep, motivation, joint pain on 1–5 scales), and optionally HRV. Trigger fatigue interventions when ≥2 markers deteriorate simultaneously for 2+ consecutive weeks. For intentional functional overreaching blocks (1–2 weeks at ~150% normal volume), ensure an immediate deload follows and monitor closely for NFOR warning signs.

### Key sources

- Meeusen et al. (2013). “Prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of the overtraining syndrome: joint consensus statement of ECSS and ACSM.” *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, 45(1), 186–205.
- Bell et al. (2020). “Overreaching and overtraining in strength sports and resistance training: a scoping review.” *Journal of Sports Sciences*, 38(16), 1897+.
- Halson & Jeukendrup (2004). “Does overtraining exist? An analysis of overreaching and overtraining research.” *Sports Medicine*, 34, 967–981.
- Thomas et al. (2018). “Neuromuscular fatigue and recovery after heavy resistance, jump, and sprint training.” *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*.
- Wan et al. (2017). “Central and peripheral fatigue during resistance exercise — a critical review.” *Journal of Exercise Science & Fitness*. PMC4723165.
- Israetel, Hoffmann & Smith. *Scientific Principles of Hypertrophy Training*. Renaissance Periodization.

## 7. Exercise selection for compound lifts

### Core principle

Compound lift selection should be driven by the intersection of **training goal** (hypertrophy vs. strength), **stimulus-to-fatigue ratio**, and **individual biomechanics/injury history**. For hypertrophy, high-SFR variations that maximally load the target muscle with minimal systemic fatigue are preferable. For strength, specificity to the competition or target movement pattern takes priority. Every movement pattern (squat, hinge, horizontal press, vertical press, horizontal pull) should include a primary, secondary, and tertiary exercise selection, with primaries carrying the bulk of volume and progression.

### Evidence summary by movement pattern

**Squat variations.** Front squats produce the **highest rectus femoris and vastus medialis activation** of barbell squat variations (Yavuz et al. 2015, ES = 0.62 for VM; Coratella et al. 2021, 24% greater RF, ES = 1.21) while generating less knee compressive force than back squats (Gullett et al. 2009). Low bar back squats produce the highest glute and hamstring activation due to greater hip moment. Bulgarian split squats at 50% RM match back squat glute/quad EMG at 85% RM (DeForest et al. 2014) with far less spinal loading. Hack squats and leg presses show the **highest SFR** — maximal quad overload with minimal spinal fatigue.

**Deadlift variations.** Conventional deadlifts produce the highest overall posterior chain activation but have the **lowest SFR** due to massive systemic fatigue. Sumo deadlifts show 8% reduced spinal shear (Cholewicki et al. 1991) with greater quad involvement (Escamilla et al. 2002: VL 48% vs. 40% MVIC). Trap bar deadlifts allow **5–10% heavier loads** with higher quad activation and reduced spinal flexion moment (Swinton et al. 2011: hip:knee moment ratio 1.78:1 vs. 3.68:1 for conventional). Romanian deadlifts provide **constant hamstring tension** at submaximal loads — the best SFR for hamstring hypertrophy.

**Bench press variations.** The **30° incline** is optimal for upper pec activation (Rodríguez-Ridao et al. 2020; Lauver et al. 2016: 122.5% MVIC at 30° vs. 98.2% at flat). Beyond 45°, anterior deltoid dominates. Grip width has **minimal effect** on pec activation (Saeterbakken et al. 2021); close-grip slightly increases triceps contribution. Flat bench press maximizes sternal pec activation and total load. Decline offers no advantage over flat for any muscle group.

**Overhead press variations.** Standing dumbbell press produces the **highest overall deltoid EMG** — 15% greater anterior delt than standing barbell (Saeterbakken & Fimland 2013, p < 0.001), 15% greater medial delt (p = 0.008 vs. seated) — but allows 7–10% lower 1RM than barbell. Standing barbell press produces **39% greater triceps activation** than standing dumbbell (p < 0.001). Behind-neck press uniquely targets medial and posterior deltoid (Coratella et al. 2022, ES: 4.56 medial, 8.65 posterior).

**Row variations.** Chest-supported rows produce **greater latissimus dorsi and rhomboid activation** than free-standing bent-over rows (Fry et al. 2003) while eliminating erector spinae fatigue — the highest SFR in the category. Bent-over barbell rows produce the **highest overall erector spinae activation** of all back exercises (Edelburg et al. 2018 ACE study) but have the worst SFR. Elbow path determines target emphasis: elbows tight to body = lats; elbows flared = rear delts/rhomboids. Grip type has minimal effect on lat activation specifically (Lehman 2004; Retos 2024).

### Exercise hierarchies

**For hypertrophy (prioritize SFR):**

|Pattern         |Primary               |Secondary                        |Tertiary               |
|----------------|----------------------|---------------------------------|-----------------------|

### Application guideline

The AI coach should select exercises based on the current training phase. During hypertrophy blocks (high volume, moderate intensity), bias toward high-SFR variations to maximize productive volume within recovery limits. During strength blocks (lower volume, high intensity), shift to low-SFR competition-style lifts for specificity. When programming deadlift and row movements in the same session or week, pair **chest-supported rows with conventional deadlifts** to avoid cumulative spinal fatigue. Rotate exercise variations every 1–2 mesocycles to manage connective tissue stress and prevent overuse. For trainees with injury history, substitute lower-stress alternatives: front squat or leg press for back squat (knee concerns), trap bar or sumo deadlift for conventional (lower back concerns), dumbbell bench for barbell (shoulder concerns).

## Conclusion: decision rules for the AI coach

This knowledge base reveals several clear principles that should anchor programming decisions. First, **volume is the primary driver of hypertrophy**, with 12–20 hard sets per muscle group per week as the evidence-based optimum, distributed across ≥2 weekly sessions. Second, **no periodization model is dramatically superior when volume is equated** — the critical decision is matching the model to training age (linear → double progression → undulating/block). Third, **RPE-based autoregulation produces equal or superior outcomes** to fixed percentages for intermediates and advanced trainees, but requires a familiarization period and carries a systematic ~1 rep underprediction error the system should account for. Fourth, **exercise selection should be driven by SFR** during hypertrophy phases and by specificity during strength phases, with the 30° incline for upper pec, Romanian deadlift for hamstrings, and chest-supported row for back emerging as clear SFR winners. Fifth, **deloads should be proactively scheduled** (4:1 ratio for most trainees) with reactive override triggers based on RPE drift, wellness scores, and performance trends. The system should treat every number in this document as a population-level starting estimate, then personalize aggressively based on the individual’s response data over time.
