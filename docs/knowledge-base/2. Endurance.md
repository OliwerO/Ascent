# Endurance training knowledge base for Ascent AI Coach

## How to use this document

Each topic follows a consistent structure: **Core Principle** (2–3 sentences), **Evidence Summary** (specific numbers and ranges), **Application Guideline** (decision rules for the AI coach), and **Key Sources**. All data is current through early 2026. Where evidence conflicts, the most robust finding (largest sample, most recent meta-analysis) is flagged.

## 1. Polarized training model

### 1.1 The 80/20 intensity distribution

**Core Principle.** The polarized model distributes training as ~75–80% below the first ventilatory threshold (VT1, blood lactate <2 mmol/L), ~0–5% between VT1 and VT2, and ~15–20% above VT2 (>4 mmol/L lactate).  The critical insight from Stephen Seiler’s work is that elite endurance athletes across sports converge on this pattern and spend remarkably little time at moderate/“threshold” intensity.  The 80/20 ratio was originally described by **session count** (80% of sessions are easy); measured by time-in-zone, the split is closer to **90/5/5** because high-intensity intervals occupy only minutes within a session.  

**Evidence Summary.**

- Stöggl and Sperlich (2014): 48 well-trained endurance athletes (VO2peak 62.6 ± 7.1 ml/kg/min), 9-week RCT comparing polarized (POL), threshold (THR), high-volume (HVT), and HIIT groups.   POL produced **+11.7% VO2peak, +17.4% time to exhaustion, +8.1% power at 4 mmol/L lactate, +5.1% peak power**.  THR and HVT showed no significant improvements. 
- Neal et al. (2013): 12 trained cyclists, 6-week crossover. POL outperformed THR on peak power (+8% vs +3%), lactate threshold power (+9% vs +2%), and high-intensity capacity (+85% vs +37%) — despite lower total training volume.
- Muñoz, Seiler et al. (2014): 30 recreational runners, 10 weeks. POL improved 10K time by 5.0% vs THR 3.6%.  Sub-analysis of most adherent athletes: POL **+7.0%** vs THR **+1.6%**  (Cohen’s d = 1.29, p = 0.038). 
- Rosenblat et al. (2019) meta-analysis: Moderate effect favoring POL over THR for time-trial performance (ES = −0.66). 
- Rosenblat, Seiler et al. (2025) individual-participant-data network meta-analysis (13 studies, 348 athletes): **No difference between POL and pyramidal (PYR) for VO2max or time-trial in recreational athletes.**  Competitive athletes may benefit more from POL.  This is the most recent and methodologically strongest evidence.
- Silva Oliveira et al. (2024) meta-analysis: POL superior to other distributions for VO2peak with small effect size (SMD = 0.46, p = 0.01) but only for highly trained/national-level athletes and interventions <12 weeks. 

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should default to a polarized distribution for all training plans: program ~80% of sessions as easy (below aerobic threshold/VT1) and ~20% as genuinely hard (above VT2). The most important rule is **avoid the gray zone** — do not let easy sessions drift into moderate intensity. For recreational mountain athletes, both POL and PYR produce equivalent outcomes,  so the coach should not be dogmatic about the exact split; the key principles are (1) keep easy truly easy, (2) make hard sessions genuinely hard, (3) accumulate sufficient volume, (4) limit threshold-zone work to ≤5% of total time. When weekly volume is low (<5 hours), ensure at least 1 dedicated high-intensity session; at <3 hours/week, a single weekly interval session may suffice for the high-intensity component.

**Key Sources.**

- Seiler S. “What is best practice for training intensity and duration distribution in endurance athletes?” *Int J Sports Physiol Perform*, 5(3):276–291, 2010. 
- Stöggl T, Sperlich B. “Polarized training has greater impact on key endurance variables than threshold, high intensity, or high volume training.” *Frontiers in Physiology*, 5:33, 2014. 
- Neal CM et al. “Six weeks of a polarized training-intensity distribution leads to greater physiological and performance adaptations than a threshold model in trained cyclists.” *J Appl Physiol*, 114(4):461–471, 2013.
- Muñoz I, Seiler S et al. “Does polarized training improve performance in recreational runners?” *Int J Sports Physiol Perform*, 9(2):265–272, 2014.
- Rosenblat MA, Seiler S et al. “Which training intensity distribution will produce the greatest improvements in VO2max and TT performance?” *Sports Medicine*, 55(3):655–673, 2025.

### 1.2 Application to recreational mountain athletes

**Core Principle.** For non-competitive mountain athletes training 5–10 hours per week, the specific ratio of polarized vs pyramidal matters less than three fundamentals: keeping most training below VT1, making hard sessions genuinely hard, and accumulating sufficient total volume. Mountain sports are naturally suited to polarized training because long days of ski touring or hiking at conversational pace perfectly match Zone 1 prescription.

**Evidence Summary.**

- The 2025 IPD meta-analysis (Rosenblat and Seiler) found no performance difference between POL and PYR for recreational athletes (SMD = −0.06 for VO2max, p = 0.68; SMD = −0.05 for time-trial, p = 0.34). 
- A 2025 study of 120 recreational marathon runners found POL produced 11.3 ± 3.2 min improvement vs PYR’s 8.7 ± 2.8 min,  but individual response clustering showed only 31.5% were “polarized responders” — 31.9% responded better to pyramidal training. 
- Uphill Athlete (Steve House, Scott Johnston, *Training for the New Alpinism*) recommends 80–95% aerobic base training for mountain athletes,  with initial 8–12 weeks of zero high-intensity work.  This is broadly consistent with polarized philosophy applied to mountain-specific context.

**Application Guideline.** For new users or those returning from a break, the AI coach should prescribe 8–12 weeks of pure aerobic base training (all sessions below VT1) before introducing any high-intensity intervals. After the base phase, add 1–2 weekly high-intensity sessions: hill intervals of 3–5 × 4–8 minutes at >90% HRmax, or steep uphill repeats with loaded pack. The coach should watch for the classic recreational error — every mountain session becomes a moderate-hard effort at 80–85% HRmax. Flag sessions where average HR falls in the 78–88% HRmax range as potential “gray zone” violations.

### 1.3 Translating polarized training to mountain touring

**Core Principle.** In mountain sports, the sport itself is the base training — a long ski tour at comfortable pace is Zone 1 work. The challenge is not finding enough low-intensity volume but preventing steep terrain, altitude, pack weight, and competitive instinct from pushing every session above VT1. Dedicated high-intensity sessions should be sport-specific: steep skinning intervals, loaded uphill repeats, or VO2max hill efforts.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Elite ski mountaineers train similarly to cross-country skiers: VO2max is the strongest performance predictor  (r values in Lasshofer et al., 2021: elite 71.2 ± 6.8 ml/kg/min vs sub-elite 62.5 ± 4.7 ml/kg/min, p = 0.003).  
- Schenk et al. (2011): Experienced ski mountaineers (VO2max 68.2 ± 6.1 ml/kg/min) had VT1 at 70.5% VO2max and VT2 at 90.9% VO2max. Competition was sustained near VT2. 
- Norwegian Olympic coaching uses 85–95% aerobic training volume with 2–3 high-intensity sessions per week for cross-country skiers.  Mountain athletes should adapt these principles.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should classify mountain sessions by HR data: if average HR for the uphill portion is <77% HRmax, count as Zone 1. If 77–88%, flag as potential gray zone and advise the user to slow down on subsequent easy tours. For high-intensity mountain sessions, prescribe sport-specific intervals: 4 × 4 min at 90–95% HRmax on steep terrain (>15% grade) with descent recovery. “Muscular endurance” sessions (heavy pack, steep terrain, near-threshold intensity) serve as the sport-specific high-intensity work.  Count these as Zone 3 time.

## 2. Zone-based training

### 2.1 Three-zone and five-zone models

**Core Principle.** The 3-zone model (Seiler) anchors to two physiological thresholds: Zone 1 below VT1/LT1 (<2 mmol/L lactate), Zone 2 between VT1 and VT2 (2–4 mmol/L), Zone 3 above VT2/LT2 (>4 mmol/L).   The 5-zone model subdivides further for more granular prescription. Both models are physiologically valid; the 3-zone model is simpler and aligns directly with polarized training philosophy.

**Evidence Summary.**

|Zone (3-zone)|Zone (5-zone)|%HRmax |%HRR   |Blood Lactate |RPE (Borg 6–20)|
|-------------|-------------|-------|-------|--------------|---------------|
|Zone 1       |Zones 1–2    |<77–82%|<60–75%|<2.0 mmol/L   |6–12           |
|Zone 2       |Zones 3–4    |82–88% |75–88% |2.0–4.0 mmol/L|12–16          |
|Zone 3       |Zone 5       |>88–92%|>88%   |>4.0 mmol/L   |16–20          |

- Individual variability is substantial: a 2025 study found CVs of **6–29%** across different Zone 2 markers, with fixed %HRmax ranges poorly representing individual metabolic responses. 
- The 5-zone Norwegian model defines: Z1 (60–72% HRmax, recovery), Z2 (72–82%, moderate aerobic), Z3 (82–87%, tempo), Z4 (88–92%, threshold), Z5 (93–100%, VO2max). 

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should use the **3-zone model as the primary framework** for session classification and polarized distribution tracking. Use the 5-zone model for prescribing specific workout intensities (e.g., “Z4 intervals” for threshold work). Always communicate zones to the user in simple terms: Zone 1 = “conversational,” Zone 2 = “comfortably hard,” Zone 3 = “race effort or harder.” Set initial zone boundaries from %HRmax (using Tanaka formula if no field test data), then refine with field test data when available.

### 2.2 Setting zones without power meters

**Core Principle.** For mountain sports without power meters, heart rate reserve (%HRR via Karvonen method) is more accurate than %HRmax for prescribing intensity  because %HRR maps to %VO2 reserve (r = 0.990, confirmed by Swain et al.). The Tanaka formula (208 − 0.7 × age) provides a better HRmax estimate than the Fox formula (220 − age), particularly for athletes over 40, but all formulas have a standard error of **~10 bpm**. 

**Evidence Summary.**

- Karvonen formula: THR = HRrest + (HRmax − HRrest) × %intensity.  Swain and Leutholtz (1997) confirmed %HRR ≈ %VO2R  (slope 1.00 ± 0.01, intercept −0.1 ± 0.6)  but NOT %VO2max.
- Tanaka et al. (2001) meta-analysis of 351 studies (18,712 subjects): HRmax = 208 − 0.7 × age, SEE ~10 bpm. No gender or activity-level differences.
- Fox formula (220 − age): SEE of ~12 bpm.  Overestimates HRmax in young adults by ~7 bpm, underestimates in older adults.
- HERITAGE Family Study (762 subjects): Fox SEE = 12.4 bpm, Tanaka SEE = 11.4 bpm.
- Field test recommendation: 3 × 3-min all-out hill repeats; peak HR on final repeat provides a practical HRmax estimate far more accurate than any formula. 

**Application Guideline.** On first use, the AI coach should set zones using the Tanaka formula (208 − 0.7 × age) and resting HR via Karvonen. Prominently flag that formula-based zones are approximations (±10 bpm error cascades into zone error). Prompt the user to complete a field test (steep hill repeats protocol: warm up 15 min, then 3 × 3-min maximal efforts on steep terrain with 3-min jog recovery, record peak HR on third effort) within the first 2 weeks, then recalibrate zones. If the user provides a lab-tested lactate threshold HR, use that as the primary anchor and derive all zones from LTHR.

### 2.3 RPE mapping to HR zones

**Core Principle.** Rating of perceived exertion correlates with heart rate (r = 0.74) and more strongly with blood lactate (r = 0.83),  making RPE a reliable complementary tool when HR data is unreliable — such as during variable mountain terrain, extreme cold, or at altitude. RPE is independent of age, gender, and fitness level for identifying metabolic thresholds. 

**Evidence Summary.**

- Scherr et al. (2013), n = 2,560: RPE at LT1 (aerobic threshold) = **10.8 ± 1.8** (Borg 6–20); RPE at LT2 (anaerobic threshold) = **13.6 ± 1.8**; RPE at 4 mmol/L lactate = **14.1 ± 2.0**. 
- Seiler and Kjerland (2006): Session RPE distribution matched HR-based distribution within 1–3%.  

|Zone (3-zone)   |Borg RPE (6–20)|Borg CR-10|Simple Cue                  |
|----------------|---------------|----------|----------------------------|
|Zone 1 (<VT1)   |6–12           |0–3       |“Can talk easily”           |
|Zone 2 (VT1–VT2)|12–16          |4–6       |“Can speak in short phrases”|
|Zone 3 (>VT2)   |16–20          |7–10      |“Can only say a few words”  |

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should collect session RPE (1–10 scale, which maps to Borg CR-10) after every session. Use RPE as a cross-check against HR data. When HR data is suspect (altitude days, cold weather, variable terrain), weight RPE more heavily for session classification. If session RPE is ≤3 (CR-10) and HR shows Zone 2, the HR reading is likely inflated by cardiac drift, altitude, or heat — classify the session as Zone 1. If RPE is ≥7 but HR is in Zone 1–2, suspect optical sensor error.

### 2.4 Cardiac drift during long uphill efforts

**Core Principle.** During sustained exercise, heart rate drifts upward at constant workload due to rising core temperature, plasma volume loss, and reduced stroke volume.  During multi-hour mountain days, cardiac drift is unavoidable and can shift HR by a full training zone, making raw HR data unreliable for zone classification in the second half of long efforts.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Typical magnitude: **10–20 bpm over 30–60 minutes** of sustained moderate exercise.  Rate: approximately **3–5 bpm per 30 minutes** in temperate conditions, accelerating with heat and dehydration.
- A drift of up to **15% of initial HR** has been documented (e.g., 117 → 135 bpm = one full zone shift). 
- Onset: typically after ~10–12 minutes of continuous exercise (Colakoglu et al., 2018). 
- Uphill Athlete aerobic drift test: <3.5% HR drift over 60 min = below aerobic threshold; **3.5–5% = aerobic threshold**; >5% = above aerobic threshold. 

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should base zone classification on **first-hour HR data** for long mountain sessions. For sessions >2 hours, accept 5–10 bpm drift as normal and do not reclassify the session to a higher zone unless RPE also increases. Implement a “drift-adjusted” zone calculation: for every hour beyond the first, subtract 3–5 bpm from recorded HR before zone classification. When prescribing long easy sessions, tell the user to target the lower end of Zone 1 HR at the start, allowing room for drift to stay within Zone 1.

### 2.5 Altitude effects on HR zones

**Core Principle.** Altitude compresses the usable heart rate range: resting HR rises (increased sympathetic drive) while HRmax may decrease, narrowing the gap between easy and maximal effort.  At moderate altitude (1,500–3,000 m), VO2max declines **6–7% per 1,000 m** for well-conditioned athletes, meaning the same absolute workload represents a higher percentage of maximum capacity.  

**Evidence Summary.**

- VO2max decline: **6.3% per 1,000 m** (Wehrlin and Hallén, 2006; 8 endurance athletes, 300–2,800 m). At 3,000 m: ~19–20% reduction for fit athletes.  
- Resting HR: increases **4–5 bpm at 1,500–2,000 m**,  scaling to **20–30 bpm above 3,000 m**. 
- HRmax: may decrease **~5 bpm at moderate altitude** (Lundby, 2012), though this is debated below 2,500 m. 
- Submaximal HR: rises for the same absolute workload.
- Acclimatization recovers **29–36% of the initial VO2max deficit** over 2 weeks (Wehrlin et al.). 

**Application Guideline.** When the user’s GPS data shows elevation >1,500 m, the AI coach should apply an altitude adjustment: **reduce all HR zone boundaries by 5 bpm for 1,500–2,500 m and by 8–10 bpm for 2,500–3,000 m**. If the user trains regularly at altitude (e.g., Innsbruck-based athlete doing weekly tours at 2,000–3,000 m), partial acclimatization reduces the adjustment needed — track the user’s average weekly altitude exposure and progressively reduce the correction factor over 2–3 weeks of consistent exposure. On first exposure to new altitude, increase prescribed recovery time by 20–30%. Always cross-reference with RPE.

## 3. Vertical-specific conditioning

### 3.1 Oxygen cost at incline

**Core Principle.** The metabolic cost of uphill walking increases nonlinearly with gradient, reaching nearly **5× flat-ground cost at 20% grade** and **~9× at 40% grade**. For recreational athletes, walking at moderate speeds on steep terrain (>15% grade) can approach or exceed VO2max, making gradient the single most powerful variable for manipulating exercise intensity in mountain training.

**Evidence Summary (Minetti et al., 2002 — the definitive study, 10 elite mountain runners).**

|Grade    |Walking Cost (J/kg/m)|Multiple of Flat|
|---------|---------------------|----------------|
|0% (flat)|1.64                 |1.0×            |
|+10%     |4.68                 |2.9×            |
|+15%     |~6.4                 |~3.9×           |
|+20%     |8.07                 |4.9×            |
|+25%     |~9.7                 |~5.9×           |
|+30%     |11.29                |6.9×            |
|+45%     |17.33                |10.6×           |

- ACSM metabolic equation for walking: VO2 (ml/kg/min) = 0.1 × speed(m/min) + 1.8 × speed × fractional grade + 3.5.
- At 5 km/h on a 15% grade: ~34 ml/kg/min (~9.7 METs). At 20% grade: ~41 ml/kg/min (~11.8 METs).
- For a recreational athlete with VO2max of 45 ml/kg/min, **walking at 5 km/h on a 20% grade demands ~91% of VO2max** — essentially maximal effort.
- Mechanical efficiency above 15% grade stabilizes at ~0.243 (walking), matching pure concentric contraction efficiency (~25%). 
- Optimal path gradient for minimizing energy cost per vertical meter: **20–30%** (Minetti, 1995). 

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should use the ACSM equation (or Minetti polynomial for higher precision) to estimate session intensity from GPS speed and grade data. When prescribing uphill workouts, use grade as the primary intensity lever: Zone 1 training on 10–15% grades at moderate speed; Zone 3 intervals on >20% grades. Flag sessions where the user’s speed-grade combination would demand >85% of estimated VO2max as high-intensity, even if the user perceives the pace as “slow.” Use the equivalence: **100 m vertical gain ≈ 1.0–1.5 km of flat running** in terms of energy cost and training stress.

### 3.2 Lactate threshold at incline vs flat

**Core Principle.** The lactate threshold occurs at a lower absolute speed on an incline but at a **similar relative intensity (%VO2max)**. Incline exercise may elicit a higher true VO2max due to greater muscle mass recruitment. This means threshold training on steep hills is effective at much lower speeds, reducing musculoskeletal impact while maintaining metabolic stress.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Langsetmo et al. (2002): Lactate threshold occurred at 47.9 ± 2.1% VO2max on a 10% incline vs 43.9 ± 4.5% VO2max on flat — not significantly different as a percentage. Absolute VO2 at LT was higher on incline (37.3 vs 26.9 L/min). 
- Padulo et al.: VO2 increases ~18.7% between 0% and 7% incline at the same running speed. 
- Incline treadmill protocols can produce VO2max values ~10% higher than flat protocols due to greater lower-extremity muscle recruitment.

**Application Guideline.** When prescribing threshold workouts, the AI coach can use steep terrain (10–15% grade) as a low-impact alternative to flat tempo runs. Set the target as a **HR-based threshold zone** (88–92% HRmax) rather than a pace target, since pace will be dramatically slower on inclines. This is particularly valuable for athletes with joint issues or those returning from injury.

### 3.3 Muscular demands of sustained climbing

**Core Principle.** Uphill exercise dramatically increases activation of the vastus group (quadriceps) and soleus compared to flat walking, while redistributing work away from the rectus femoris. A model using just soleus and vastus lateralis EMG explains **96% of the variance** in metabolic cost of incline walking, identifying these two muscle groups as the primary metabolic engines of uphill locomotion. 

**Evidence Summary (Sloniger et al., 1997 — MRI study, uphill vs horizontal running at 10% grade).**

|Muscle       |Flat Activation|Uphill Activation|Change                   |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|-------------------------|
|Vastus group |53%            |**75%**          |**+22 percentage points**|
|Soleus       |41%            |**55%**          |**+14 pp**               |
|Gluteals     |71%            |**79%**          |+8 pp                    |
|Gastrocnemius|71%            |**76%**          |+5 pp                    |

- Total lower extremity muscle volume activated: 67% flat vs **73% uphill** (p < 0.05). 
- Franz and Kram (2012): Hip, knee, and ankle extensor EMG all progressively increase with steeper grades.
- Quadriceps activation increases ~40% at 10° incline vs flat (Journal of Sports Sciences).

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should prescribe targeted strength work for the primary uphill muscles: (1) single-leg squats and step-ups for vastus group and gluteals, (2) single-leg calf raises (eccentric emphasis) for soleus and gastrocnemius, (3) weighted lunges for integrated uphill gait pattern. During base phases, 2–3 strength sessions per week targeting these muscle groups. During peak season, reduce to 1–2 sessions for maintenance. Always include hip flexor mobility work — tightened iliopsoas from prolonged uphill effort is a common issue.

### 3.4 Load carriage physiology

**Core Principle.** Carrying a 10–15 kg pack (typical for ski touring and hike-and-fly) increases metabolic cost by approximately **15–25%** compared to unloaded walking. The relationship between load and energy cost is **nonlinear** — heavier loads incur disproportionately greater costs. Military research provides the strongest evidence base for load carriage physiology.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Pandolf equation: M = 1.5W + 2.0(W+L)(L/W)² + η(W+L)[1.5V² + 0.35VG], where W = body mass, L = load, V = speed, G = grade, η = terrain factor.
- A 15 kg pack on a 75 kg person (20% body mass) increases energy expenditure by ~20–25% at any given speed and grade.
- Military load carriage research recommends sustaining ≤**45–47% VO2max** during prolonged loaded marches to delay fatigue. 
- The Pandolf equation under-predicts by **12–33%** for contemporary military loads (Drain et al., 2017).
- For a 75 kg person hiking at 4.5 km/h on 15% grade: unloaded ~550–600 kcal/hr; with 15 kg pack ~700–750 kcal/hr.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should factor pack weight into intensity calculations. When the user reports carrying >8 kg, apply a metabolic adjustment: multiply estimated energy expenditure by (1 + 0.015 × load_kg) as a simple approximation. For training progression, introduce pack weight gradually: start at 10% body weight, increase by ~1–2 kg per week until target carry weight. Do not increase pack weight and session duration simultaneously — change one variable at a time. 

### 3.5 Progressive overload of vertical gain

**Core Principle.** Vertical gain should be tracked as an independent load metric alongside duration and HR-based intensity. Safe progression follows the same principles as general endurance volume progression, but the high muscular demands of uphill work (especially eccentric loading on descent) warrant more conservative progression than flat terrain training.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Standard endurance progression: 5–10% increase per week in total volume  is supported by ACSM/NASM guidelines, though the 10% figure specifically is convention rather than evidence-based (see Section 6).
- From Minetti’s data, 100 m vertical gain costs approximately **4.9× the energy** of 500 m of flat walking at 20% grade.
- Elite ski mountaineers regularly train 4,000–8,000 m vertical per week.
- Suggested progression: Beginner 400–1,200 m/week → Building phase 1,000–2,000 m/week → Peak phase 2,000–4,000 m/week for serious recreational athletes.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should track weekly vertical meters as a primary volume metric. Apply ACWR principles specifically to vertical gain: keep the weekly vertical within **0.8–1.3× the 4-week rolling average**. When increasing both duration and vertical gain simultaneously, limit each to 5% increase rather than allowing 10% on both. Deload weeks should reduce vertical gain by 40–60%. Flag any week where vertical gain exceeds 1.5× the 4-week average as a spike risk.

## 4. Altitude considerations

### 4.1 Training at 1,500–3,000 m elevation

**Core Principle.** VO2max declines approximately **6–7% per 1,000 m** above sea level for well-conditioned athletes,   meaning a summit day at 3,000 m costs roughly 15–20% of aerobic capacity compared to valley level. The performance impact on time to exhaustion is even steeper, declining **~14.5% per 1,000 m**.  Altitude compresses the usable HR range and demands more conservative intensity prescription.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Wehrlin and Hallén (2006): VO2max declined linearly from 66 ± 1.6 to 55 ± 1.6 ml/kg/min between 300 m and 2,800 m, yielding **6.3% per 1,000 m** (range 4.6–7.5%). 
- Fulco et al. meta-analysis (146 studies): Well-conditioned athletes (VO2max >63 ml/kg/min) lose ~7% per 1,000 m; less-conditioned athletes lose ~4–5% per  1,000 m.
- Schuler et al. (2007): 8 elite cyclists at 2,340 m — Day 1 VO2max declined **12.8%** and time to exhaustion **25.8%**. Recovery rate: ~4% VO2max per week, ~6% TTE per week.
- Pühringer et al. (2022): Acclimatized Austrian mountain guides with lower baseline VO2max showed better preservation at altitude.

**Application Guideline.** For an Innsbruck-based user (valley at ~570 m) training at 1,500–3,000 m, the AI coach should apply the following: at 2,000 m (typical mid-mountain), VO2max is reduced ~9–10% acutely; at 3,000 m, ~15–17%. Adjust prescribed intensities downward accordingly. During the first 2–3 exposures to a new altitude tier, prescribe only Zone 1 work. If the user consistently trains above 2,000 m (≥2× per week for ≥2 weeks), begin tracking acclimatization status and progressively relax altitude adjustments. Never prescribe VO2max intervals above 2,500 m unless the user has ≥2 weeks of consistent exposure at that altitude.

### 4.2 Acclimatization patterns and timelines

**Core Principle.** Acclimatization follows a predictable timeline: acute sympathetic activation and performance loss (days 1–3), early ventilatory and hematological responses (days 3–7), substantial recovery of aerobic capacity (days 7–14), and near-complete physiological adaptation (days 14–21+). Full hematological adaptation requires approximately **11.4 days per 1,000 m** of altitude.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Days 1–3: Immediate VO2max decline, resting HR increase (+4–5 bpm), hyperventilation, sleep disturbances (doubled), HRV suppression, AMS symptoms may appear at 12–24 hours.
- Days 3–7: Ventilatory acclimatization begins, EPO release starts (requires ≥1,800 m), HRV begins normalizing (~day 5–8), sleep improving. Six days at 2,200 m substantially decreases AMS and improves work performance (Fulco et al.).
- Days 7–14: Red blood cell production increasing, VO2max recovery at ~4% per week, resting HR returning toward baseline.
- Days 14–21+: Recovery of **29–36% of initial VO2max deficit** (Wehrlin et al.). Full hematological adaptation to 3,000 m: ~34 days (Zubieta-Calleja, 2007).
- WHOOP case study: After 6 weeks at altitude (1,655 m), resting HR fell below baseline and HRV exceeded baseline.

**Application Guideline.** For Innsbruck users doing day trips to 2,000–3,000 m, each trip represents acute altitude exposure without overnight acclimatization. The AI coach should not expect meaningful acclimatization from day trips alone. However, frequent exposure (3+ times per week) provides cumulative benefit. For multi-day mountain trips, prescribe the first day as easy (Zone 1 only), allow normal training from day 3+. Track the user’s altitude exposure history (total hours above 2,000 m per week) and use it to modulate altitude HR adjustments — more exposure = smaller adjustment needed.

### 4.3 HR zone adjustments at altitude

**Core Principle.** At altitude, submaximal HR rises while HRmax may fall, compressing the effective training range. Zone boundaries derived at sea level overestimate the user’s true capacity at altitude. RPE becomes an essential cross-reference tool because it reflects actual physiological strain regardless of altitude-induced HR distortion.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Submaximal HR: rises significantly; Zone 2 at valley level can register as Zone 3–4 at altitude.
- HRmax: may decrease ~5 bpm at moderate altitude (>2,300 m, Lundby 2012), though this is debated below 2,500 m.
- Practical recommendation from altitude training guides: reduce zone targets by ~5–10 bpm at 1,500–2,500 m.
- Resting HR increase: +4–5 bpm at 1,500–2,000 m.
- SpO2 monitoring: target >95% at rest. If <92% post-exercise, increase recovery time.

**Application Guideline.** When session GPS shows altitude >1,500 m, the AI coach should display altitude-adjusted zones alongside standard zones. The adjustment formula: **Adjusted zone boundary = Standard boundary − (altitude_above_1500m / 1000 × 5 bpm)**. Example: for standard Zone 1 ceiling of 145 bpm, at 2,500 m the adjusted ceiling becomes 140 bpm. When RPE and HR disagree at altitude (RPE says easy, HR says hard), trust RPE. Monitor morning resting HR; if elevated >8 bpm above the user’s rolling baseline, recommend a rest day.

### 4.4 Recovery at altitude

**Core Principle.** Altitude impairs recovery through multiple pathways: disrupted sleep architecture, sympathetic nervous system activation, reduced oxygen delivery, and dehydration from increased respiratory water loss. Recovery times should be extended by 20–30% for training sessions conducted above 2,000 m.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Sleep at 1,800 m: total sleep time decreased by 9 min, light sleep decreased 12 min, slow-wave sleep increased 7.8 min, respiratory rate increased (Sleep Health Journal, 33 elite athletes).
- Periodic breathing (hyperventilation/apnea cycles) begins at >2,000 m during sleep.
- HRV: acute suppression of RMSSD and HF power; LF/HF ratio increases (sympathetic predominance). Non-linear HRV measures are most sensitive (PATCH-HA study, Frontiers).
- Hydration needs increase: ≥3–4 liters per day recommended at altitude.
- High-carbohydrate diet (>70% calories from CHO) recommended for altitude performance.

**Application Guideline.** After sessions above 2,000 m, the AI coach should prescribe 20–30% more recovery time than equivalent valley sessions. If the user’s HRV (RMSSD) drops >15% below their 7-day average on the morning after an altitude session, prescribe Zone 1 or rest for the following day. During periods of frequent altitude exposure, increase easy day prescriptions by one per week. Remind users to increase fluid and carbohydrate intake on altitude days.

## 5. Sport-specific demands

### 5a. Splitboarding and ski touring

**Core Principle.** Ski touring is one of the most demanding endurance sports, requiring sustained sub-threshold uphill effort for 2–4+ hours while carrying equipment in cold conditions at altitude. Racing demands approach 92–93% HRmax average, but recreational touring typically sits at 65–85% HRmax. The primary performance determinant is **VO2max**, followed by economy and velocity at VT2.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Elite SKIMO athletes: VO2max **71.2 ± 6.8 ml/kg/min** (Lasshofer et al., 2021). Sub-elite: **62.5 ± 4.7 ml/kg/min**.
- Mean HR during SKIMO racing: **92–93% HRmax** (Duc et al., 2011; Gaston et al., 2019; Lasshofer et al., 2021).
- Race intensity distribution: 7% Zone 1, 51% Zone 2, 42% Zone 3 (Duc et al., 2011).
- Energy expenditure: Recreational touring ~**400–700 kcal/hour**; racing intensity up to **1,000+ kcal/hour**.
- Per 1,000 m vertical: approximately **500–600 kcal** at moderate pace for an 80 kg person with gear.
- Total race energy: 19–23 MJ (4,600–5,400 kcal) for competitive multi-hour races (Praz et al.).
- Vertical climbing rate: Elite ~1,173 m/hr; sub-elite ~985 m/hr (Lasshofer et al., 2021).
- Cold exposure: Even 0.5°C core temperature drop measurably lowers self-paced endurance output (Ohio University, 2026). >50% of elite XC skiers experience exercise-induced bronchoconstriction. Optimal performance range: 4–20°C.
- During descents: HR stays ~85% HRmax due to psycho-emotional and physical demands.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should classify ski touring sessions as follows: estimate uphill time from GPS track, classify uphill portions by HR zone, add ~100–200 kcal/hr to flat-equivalent energy estimates for cold exposure and equipment weight. For winter periodization, use this framework:

- **Base phase (Oct–Nov):** High-volume Zone 1, 4–5 sessions/week. 2–3 strength sessions. Hiking with pack as primary mountain activity. Build to 6–8 hrs/week.
- **Build phase (Dec–Jan):** Add 1–2 weekly interval sessions (steep skinning repeats, 4 × 4 min at >90% HRmax). Sport-specific touring begins. Gradual altitude exposure. Reduce strength to 2 sessions.
- **Peak phase (Feb–Mar):** Highest specificity — long tours, race simulations. Maintain 1 interval session. Strength to 1–2 maintenance sessions. Target peak weekly vertical.
- **Maintain phase (Apr):** Reduce volume 20%, maintain intensity. Spring conditions allow longer days. Begin transition planning.

For cold exposure management: advise layering for uphill warmth without overheating; flag that rest periods during tours and lift rides create thermoregulatory vulnerability; recommend fueling every 45–60 minutes during cold-weather sessions.

### 5b. Hike-and-fly paragliding

**Core Principle.** Hike-and-fly combines fast uphill hiking under load (10–15 kg paraglider kit) with time pressure, transforming what would be Zone 1–2 leisure hiking into Zone 3–4 racing effort. The critical physical demands are aerobic capacity for fast loaded ascent, core and shoulder endurance for launch and active flying, and the ability to control a paraglider safely while physiologically fatigued.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Paraglider gear weight: standard hike-and-fly kits **12–22 kg** total; ultralight kits can be <5 kg.
- Military load carriage data: Walking with 20 kg backpack increases VO2 by **~4.45 ml/kg/min** vs unloaded (MDPI Nutrients, 2025, special forces operators).
- Speed hiking with 12–15 kg load at 5–6 km/h uphill: estimated **8–12 METs**, or **600–900+ kcal/hour** depending on gradient and altitude.
- Leisure hiking: Zone 1–2 (60–70% HRmax), sustainable for many hours. Speed hiking / race mode: Zone 3–4 (75–90% HRmax), intermittent high-intensity endurance.
- Flying phase: minimal energy expenditure (~2–3 METs), isometric core engagement, forearm endurance for brake toggle manipulation.
- Launch demands: short burst of running while controlling risers overhead; most demanding in strong wind conditions.
- X-Alps-style events require elite endurance fitness — multi-day events covering huge distances on foot and in the air.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should treat hike-and-fly training as loaded-uphill-specific work. Key training prescriptions: (1) build aerobic base with standard Zone 1 hiking, (2) add loaded speed hikes (10–15 kg, fast pace, targeting 80–85% HRmax) 1–2×/week, (3) include grip/forearm endurance work (dead hangs, farmer’s carries) and core stability exercises (planks, anti-rotation). For hike-and-fly race preparation, include “fatigued launch” practice — complete a hard hike then immediately practice wing inflation and ground handling. When logging hike-and-fly sessions, classify the uphill portion by HR/RPE zone and add the flying portion as active recovery (equivalent to easy Zone 1 time).

### 5c. Resort snowboarding

**Core Principle.** Resort snowboarding is an **intermittent sport** with short bursts of moderate-to-vigorous effort (downhill runs) separated by forced low-intensity recovery periods (chair lift rides). It is structurally similar to HIIT but with unstructured work intervals and passive rest periods. The primary physical demands are eccentric quadriceps control, hip/knee stability, and sustained isometric force generation — not VO2max.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Roberts (2020, CSU San Marcos, n = 25): Entire session average HR: **64 ± 9% HRmax** (121 ± 19 bpm) over ~6 hours. During active runs only: **76 ± 10% HRmax** (142 ± 20 bpm).
- Time in zones during sessions: 26% moderate intensity, 21% vigorous intensity. Only **33% of session time is active riding** (Stöggl et al., 2016); 56% is low-intensity lift/rest.
- Ainsworth Compendium: Snowboarding = **5.3 METs (moderate)** up to **8.0 METs (vigorous/racing)**.
- Energy expenditure: Active riding ~380–500 kcal/hr; session average including lifts ~**250–350 kcal/hr**.
- Deep powder increases demands by **20–40%** vs packed snow.
- Vernillo (2018, Austrian team snowboarders): **Isometric quadriceps strength** is a stronger performance predictor than VO2max (r = −0.93 to −0.97). Leg stiffness and force maintenance capacity also strongly correlated (r = −0.85 to −0.89). Explosive strength was NOT a strong correlate.
- Elite half-pipe competitors: achieved **92% HRmax** during competition (Kipp, 1998).
- Alpine skiing requires ~2.5 hours to match the energy expenditure of 1 hour of cross-country skiing.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should classify resort snowboarding as **moderate-intensity intermittent activity** for training load calculations. Use these load estimates: multiply active riding time (total session time × 0.33) by moderate-intensity TRIMP values; multiply remaining time by recovery TRIMP values. A 4-hour resort session equals roughly **60–90 minutes of moderate continuous exercise** in training load terms. Do not count snowboarding days toward high-intensity training quotas — the intensity is insufficient and unstructured for targeted cardiovascular stimulus. Instead, count them as active recovery or light aerobic work in the weekly plan. For users who snowboard frequently (3+ days/week), the coach should note the high eccentric quad loading and prescribe adequate recovery and complementary hamstring/glute strengthening to prevent muscle imbalances.

## 6. Endurance progression

### 6.1 The 10% rule: what evidence actually says

**Core Principle.** The 10% rule (increase weekly volume by no more than 10%) is a **convention, not an evidence-based threshold**. The only major RCT to test it directly found no difference in injury rates. Actual injury risk appears to be driven more by spikes in single-session load (acute:chronic workload ratio) than by weekly volume progression rate.

**Evidence Summary.**

- The 10% rule originated from Dr. Joan Ullyot (1980) as advice for novice runners. It was never derived from controlled research.
- **Buist et al. (2008):** RCT with 532 novice runners. 10% graded group: **20.8% injury rate**; standard group: **20.3%**. No significant difference.
- **Nielsen et al. (2014):** Increasing weekly volume >30% increased injury risk. Increases up to ~24% were no riskier than 10%.
- **Nielsen et al. (2025, BJSM):** 5,200+ runners, 18 months. The 10% rule applied more to **single run distance** than weekly volume — runners who increased their longest run by >10% vs their previous 30-day max had significantly higher injury risk.
- **Pollock et al.:** Novice runners in 15, 30, and 45-minute groups showed injury rates of 22%, 24%, and 54% respectively.
- Jack Daniels “Equilibrium” approach: Increase by 20–30%, then hold steady for 3–4 weeks to allow structural adaptation.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should NOT rigidly enforce a 10% weekly volume cap. Instead, use a tiered progression system:

|Training Level          |Weekly Volume Increase        |Build:Recover Ratio|
|------------------------|------------------------------|-------------------|
|Beginner (<6 months)    |10–20%, with equilibrium holds|2:1                |
|Intermediate (6 mo–3 yr)|5–15%                         |3:1                |
|Advanced (>3 years)     |3–10%                         |3:1 or 4:1         |

The more important constraint is preventing **spikes**: never allow a single session to exceed 1.5× the average of the previous 4 similar sessions. Flag any week where total load exceeds 1.3× the 4-week rolling average.

### 6.2 Acute:chronic workload ratio

**Core Principle.** The ACWR (acute workload ÷ chronic 4-week average workload) is the most validated framework for managing training load progression. The injury risk “sweet spot” is **ACWR 0.8–1.3**; risk spikes dramatically above 1.5. Critically, **high chronic workload is protective** when paired with moderate ACWR — training more reduces injury risk if progression is gradual.

**Evidence Summary.**

- Gabbett (2016): Developed the training-injury prevention paradox based on Banister’s fitness-fatigue model.
- Hulin, Gabbett et al. (2016): 53 elite rugby players, 2 seasons. ACWR sweet spot: **0.8–1.3** (lowest injury risk). ACWR ≥2.11: **16.7% injury risk** in current week. High chronic workload + moderate ACWR: **RR 0.3–0.7** (protective). High chronic workload + very high ACWR (≥1.54): **28.6% injury risk**.
- Maupin et al. (2020), systematic review of 27 studies: ACWR >2.0 with high-speed running: RR = 4.66 vs moderate ACWR.
- EWMA (exponentially weighted moving average) method is more sensitive than simple rolling average for detecting injury-associated spikes.
- Limitation: Most ACWR research is in team sports; direct validation for endurance/mountain athletes is limited. IOC (2016) endorses the approach.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should calculate ACWR weekly for each load metric (duration, vertical gain, TRIMP). Use the EWMA method for greater sensitivity. Target ACWR **0.8–1.3** for all metrics. When ACWR exceeds 1.3 for any metric, flag it and suggest reducing the upcoming week’s load. When ACWR drops below 0.8 (detraining risk), suggest a modest increase. When building chronic load over time, prioritize **consistency** (high chronic load) over acute spikes.

### 6.3 Mountain-specific load monitoring

**Core Principle.** Mountain athletes need a composite load metric because no single variable captures the full stress of mountain training. The most practical approach combines heart-rate-based TRIMP for cardiovascular load, vertical gain for musculoskeletal and metabolic load, and session RPE × duration (Foster method) as a cross-check.

**Evidence Summary.**

- TRIMP (Banister, 1975): Duration × HR intensity × weighting factor. Zone-based TRIMP (Edwards) uses 5 HR zones with multipliers 1–5.
- TSS (Coggan): Requires power data, not available for mountain sports. hrTSS (HR-based) decouples from actual work on steep ascents/descents.
- Session RPE × duration (Foster method): Simplest and most robust for mountain sports. RPE on 1–10 scale × session duration in minutes = session load in arbitrary units.
- Grade-adjusted pace (NGP): Attempts to normalize for elevation but is NOT validated for complex mountainous terrain.
- Training residuals (Issurin, 2008): Aerobic endurance maintained ~30 days; strength ~30 days; speed ~5 days.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should track three load metrics weekly: (1) total session RPE × duration (primary), (2) total vertical gain in meters (secondary), (3) total duration in hours. Apply ACWR calculations to all three. When metrics disagree (e.g., ACWR for duration is 1.1 but ACWR for vertical is 1.4), use the highest value for risk assessment. Display a simple weekly load summary showing current vs 4-week average for each metric.

### 6.4 Concurrent training management

**Core Principle.** The interference effect — where endurance training blunts strength gains — is real but **practically irrelevant for recreational athletes** when managed properly. The critical factors are session separation (≥6 hours, ideally 24 hours), sequencing (strength before endurance when on the same day), and endurance modality (hiking/cycling interfere less than running). Aerobic capacity is not compromised by concurrent training.

**Evidence Summary.**

- **Hickson (1980):** Original interference study. Concurrent group: strength gains plateaued at week 7 and then declined. VO2max gains were unaffected.
- **Wilson et al. (2012)** meta-analysis, 21 studies: Hypertrophy ES: strength-only **1.23** vs concurrent **0.85**. Strength ES: 1.76 vs 1.44. Power ES: 0.91 vs 0.55. Running produces greater interference than cycling. Endurance frequency (r = −0.26 to −0.35) and duration (r = −0.29 to −0.75) negatively correlate with strength outcomes.
- Three recent meta-analyses (including Schumann et al., 2022): Concurrent training does **NOT significantly compromise** strength or hypertrophy in recreational athletes. Explosive strength/power may be attenuated.
- **Robineau et al. (2016):** 58 rugby players, 7 weeks. Same-session concurrent < 6-hour gap < **24-hour gap ≈ strength-only** for maximal strength gains. VO2peak improvements also greatest with 24-hour separation.
- **Eddens et al. (2017)** meta-analysis: Strength→Endurance sequence: ~7% larger 1RM squat gains vs Endurance→Strength (p < 0.01).
- Molecular mechanism: AMPK (endurance signal) returns to baseline within ~1–3 hours post-exercise. mTOR (strength signal) remains elevated ~18 hours. Only high-intensity endurance close to resistance training inhibits mTOR; moderate-intensity endurance separated by ≥3 hours does not (Lundberg et al., 2012; Apró et al., 2013).

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should schedule strength and endurance sessions with ≥6-hour separation, ideally on separate days. When same-day is unavoidable, program strength first, then endurance. During high-endurance phases (peak mountain season), reduce strength to **minimum effective dose: 1–2 sessions/week, 2–3 compound exercises, 2–3 sets at ≥80% 1RM**. Spiering et al. (2021) showed strength can be maintained for up to **32 weeks** with as little as 1 session/week and 1 set per exercise, as long as load is maintained. The key variable is **intensity, not volume**: maintain loads even as sets and frequency drop. Never schedule a demanding mountain session within 8 hours after lower-body strength training — running economy and mountain performance decrease within this window (Doma et al., 2017).

### 6.5 Deload and recovery week patterns

**Core Principle.** Planned deload weeks reduce accumulated fatigue and enable supercompensation. The optimal deload involves **reducing volume by 40–60% while maintaining intensity and frequency**. Deload frequency should be every 3–4 weeks for intermediate athletes and every 2–3 weeks for beginners, adjusted based on individual recovery markers (HRV, resting HR, RPE trends).

**Evidence Summary.**

- **Bosquet et al. (2007)** meta-analysis, 27 studies: Optimal taper: 2-week exponential volume reduction of **41–60%**. Maintaining intensity was critical for performance gains (SMD = 0.33, p < 0.001). Reducing intensity did NOT improve performance.
- Bell et al. (2024) survey of competitive athletes: Average deload duration **6.4 ± 1.7 days**, frequency every **5.6 ± 2.3 weeks**.
- Recommended build:recover ratios: Beginners **2:1** (2 weeks up, 1 down), Intermediate **3:1**, Advanced 4:1.
- Functional overreaching (FOR): Intentional short-term performance decrement, recovery in days to ~2 weeks, followed by supercompensation. Non-functional overreaching (NFOR): Recovery requires up to 3–4 weeks. Overtraining syndrome (OTS): Months to years.

|Recovery Need                 |Volume Reduction|Intensity                  |
|------------------------------|----------------|---------------------------|
|Low (routine deload)          |25–45%          |Maintain                   |
|Moderate (accumulated fatigue)|40–60%          |Maintain or slight decrease|
|High (overreaching signs)     |60–90%          |Decrease                   |

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should auto-schedule a deload week based on the user’s training level: every 3rd week for beginners, every 4th week for intermediate/advanced. During deloads, reduce session duration and vertical gain by 40–60% but maintain intensity of any interval work (shorter intervals, same target HR). Monitor for overreaching signals: if session RPE trends upward at the same workload for >5 consecutive sessions, or if morning resting HR is >5 bpm above the 14-day rolling average for 3+ consecutive days, or if HRV (RMSSD weekly average) drops >10% below baseline, trigger an unscheduled deload. After a deload week, resume at the pre-deload volume level (not higher) for the first week back, then continue progression.

### 6.6 Detection and prevention of overtraining

**Core Principle.** No single biomarker diagnoses overtraining — it is a diagnosis of exclusion. The most reliable early warning signs are increased RPE at a given workload, persistent fatigue not resolving with 2–3 days of rest, mood disturbances, and sleep disruption. HRV-guided training has been shown to be **superior to pre-planned training** for developing aerobic performance.

**Evidence Summary.**

- ECSS/ACSM Joint Consensus (Meeusen et al., 2013): The overtraining spectrum runs from acute fatigue → functional overreaching → non-functional overreaching → overtraining syndrome. Difference is defined by recovery time required.
- Early warning signs (>70% of athletes with NFOR/OTS self-report): irritability, loss of motivation, recurrent illness, sleep disruption.
- HRV monitoring: Daily morning RMSSD, using **weekly averages and coefficient of variation**, is the most practical tracking method. Both persistent decreases AND paradoxical increases in HRV can signal overtraining (Le Meur et al.).
- Kiviniemi et al.: HRV-guided training (adjusting daily intensity based on morning HRV) produced superior aerobic gains vs pre-planned training.
- Profile of Mood States (POMS): Reducing training in response to mood disturbances decreased swimmer burnout from 10% to zero.

**Application Guideline.** The AI coach should implement a multi-signal fatigue monitoring system: (1) daily session RPE tracking — flag if RPE increases >1.5 points at the same workload over 5+ sessions; (2) daily resting HR — flag if >5 bpm above 14-day average for 3+ days; (3) weekly HRV trend — flag if RMSSD weekly average drops >10% or if CV exceeds 10%; (4) weekly wellness check-in (sleep quality, motivation, muscle soreness on 1–5 scales). When 2+ flags trigger simultaneously, override the scheduled plan and prescribe 3–5 days of Zone 1 or complete rest. Resume training only when markers return to baseline ranges.

## Conclusion

This knowledge base equips the Ascent AI coach with evidence-grounded decision rules across six domains. Several cross-cutting insights should guide system design. First, **intensity discipline matters more than the specific training model**: the single most impactful intervention for recreational mountain athletes is enforcing truly easy sessions below VT1, preventing gray-zone drift that plagues self-coached athletes. Second, **altitude and gradient are the invisible intensity multipliers** — a “casual” ski tour at 2,500 m on a 20% grade can demand 80–90% of VO2max in someone with moderate fitness, even at a walking pace. The coach must calculate and communicate this. Third, **concurrent training interference is manageable**: separate sessions by ≥6 hours, maintain strength intensity during endurance-heavy phases, and reduce strength volume rather than eliminating it. Fourth, **load management should use composite metrics** — tracking duration, vertical gain, and session RPE independently, applying ACWR to each, and using the most conservative signal for risk assessment. Finally, **individual response variation is large**: formula-based zones, altitude adjustments, and progression rates are starting points that must be rapidly calibrated against real user data. The system’s greatest value lies not in the initial prescription but in the feedback loop — sensing when the plan diverges from the athlete’s actual physiological response and adapting accordingly.
