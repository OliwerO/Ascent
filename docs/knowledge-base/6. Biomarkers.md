# Biomarker integration for training monitoring

**This knowledge base provides decision-grade thresholds for an AI coaching system serving strength and mountain endurance athletes.** Every marker below includes specific numeric ranges derived from meta-analyses, position stands, and consensus statements. Thresholds are calibrated for athletic populations—not general clinical reference ranges—because standard lab “normal” values frequently misclassify healthy athletes as pathological and miss genuine performance-limiting deficiencies. The document covers blood markers, retest intervals, red flags, and creatine supplementation with the precision needed for automated programming decisions.

-----

## 1. Blood markers relevant to training adaptation

### 1A. Ferritin and iron status drive endurance capacity

**Core principle.** Iron is the rate-limiting mineral for oxygen transport and aerobic enzyme function.  Athletes require ferritin levels substantially above the clinical “normal” floor of 12–15 ng/mL because even iron depletion without anemia (IDNA) measurably impairs VO2max adaptation and endurance performance.   Female athletes face **3× higher prevalence** of iron deficiency than males  (15–35% vs 5–11%)  due to menstrual losses and often lower energy intake.

**Evidence summary.**

- **Optimal ferritin for athletes:** 50–100 ng/mL for males, 50–70 ng/mL for females. Minimum performance threshold is **35–40 ng/mL**.  The clinical “normal” lower bound of 12–15 ng/mL is inadequate for athletic populations.  
- **IDNA diagnostic criteria:** Ferritin <30 ng/mL with transferrin saturation <20% and normal hemoglobin (>13.5 g/dL males, >12.0 g/dL females). 
- **Performance impact of IDNA:** Burden et al. (2015) meta-analysis of 17 studies found iron supplementation in IDNA athletes improved VO2max with a **Hedges’ g of 0.61** (moderate effect, p<0.001).  A 2025 systematic review of 23 studies in female athletes showed iron deficiency reduces endurance performance by **3–4%** at the high level.   Hinton et al. (2000) demonstrated IDNA impairs adaptation to aerobic training even when hemoglobin is normal. 
- **Hepcidin dynamics:** Peeling et al. (2014) showed athletes with ferritin 30–50 ng/mL experience post-exercise hepcidin spikes that block iron absorption, creating a cycle of marginal deficiency. Athletes below 30 ng/mL paradoxically show suppressed hepcidin, favoring absorption when supplementation is timed correctly. 
- **Gender differences:** Kohler et al. (2022) found **48% of female** marathon participants had clinical iron deficiency versus **15% of males**.  Menstrual blood loss adds ~1 mg/day iron demand during menses.
- **Pre-altitude requirement:** Ferritin must be ≥40 ng/mL before altitude exposure to support erythropoiesis  (Sim et al., 2019; Garvican-Lewis et al.). 

**Supplementation protocols.** Oral iron: 100 mg elemental iron/day (ferrous sulfate or iron bisglycinate) for 6–8 weeks minimum when ferritin <35 ng/mL.  Morning dosing preferred, ideally within 30 minutes after exercise when hepcidin is still low. Alternate-day dosing shows comparable efficacy with fewer GI side effects (McCormick et al., 2020). IV iron consideration when oral supplementation fails to raise ferritin above 35 ng/mL after 6–8 weeks.  Hinton (2007) showed even 30 mg/day for 6 weeks corrected IDNA and improved ventilatory threshold. 

**Application guideline.** The AI coach should flag ferritin <35 ng/mL as requiring dietary intervention plus supplementation, and ferritin <20 ng/mL as requiring aggressive supplementation with possible training load reduction. For mountain endurance athletes planning altitude camps (ski touring at elevation), verify ferritin ≥40 ng/mL at least 6–8 weeks prior.  Recheck ferritin every 6–8 weeks during supplementation, every 6 months for females, annually for males in maintenance. 

**Key sources.** Burden RJ et al., “Is iron treatment beneficial in IDNA endurance athletes?” *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 2015. Sim M et al., “Iron considerations for the athlete,” *European Journal of Applied Physiology*, 2019. Peeling P et al., “Iron status and the acute post-exercise hepcidin response,” *PLoS ONE*, 2014. Hinton PS et al., “Iron supplementation improves endurance after training in iron-depleted, nonanemic women,” *Journal of Applied Physiology*, 2000. DellaValle DM & Haas JD, “Iron supplementation improves energetic efficiency in iron-depleted female rowers,” *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, 2014. Clénin G et al., “Iron deficiency in sports,” *Swiss Medical Weekly*, 2015.

### 1B. Testosterone-to-cortisol ratio tracks recovery status but cannot diagnose overtraining alone

**Core principle.** The free testosterone-to-cortisol ratio (FTCR) reflects the balance between anabolic and catabolic hormonal states.  A **≥30% decline from individual baseline** has been the traditional threshold for excessive training stress (Adlercreutz et al., 1986), but the ECSS/ACSM 2013 consensus clarified that T:C ratio indicates physiological strain rather than diagnosing overtraining syndrome per se. Serial intra-individual monitoring is far more informative than any single absolute value.

**Evidence summary.**

- **30% decline threshold:** Originally proposed by Adlercreutz et al. (1986). Transient 30% drops are normal after acute heavy exercise (marathon, multi-day events) and typically normalize within 24–48 hours. Only persistent declines (>72 hours at rest) are concerning.
- **Absolute FTCR values:** FTCR <0.35 × 10⁻³ = high overtraining risk; 0.35–0.50 = moderate risk; >0.80 = well-rested state (Banfi et al., 2006, professional soccer).  However, absolute values vary enormously between individuals.
- **EROS study findings (Cadegiani & Kater, 2019):** Total testosterone <400 ng/dL in male athletes is a risk factor for overtraining syndrome. The testosterone-to-estradiol ratio (T:E <13.0) was more discriminating than T:C ratio for diagnosing OTS. Basal hormone levels are “mostly normal” in OTS—**stimulated (dynamic) hormonal responses** are what become blunted.
- **Endurance training suppression:** Hackney (2020) documented that long-term endurance training reduces testosterone by **20–40%** from pre-training levels, representing a stable adaptation rather than pathology.
- **Recovery time course:** After confirmed OTS, the EROS-LONGITUDINAL study showed significant testosterone recovery after 12 weeks of reduced training and nutritional intervention, but recovery was mixed across markers.
- **Gender limitations:** T:C monitoring is primarily validated in males. Women have ~10× lower testosterone, making ratio changes unreliable. For female athletes, cortisol dynamics, menstrual status, and fT3 are more sensitive indicators.
- **Free vs total testosterone:** Free testosterone is more responsive to training stress because SHBG fluctuations can mask changes in total testosterone. Track both, but use free testosterone for ratio calculations.

**Application guideline.** The AI coach should establish individual baseline T:C during a well-rested state (after recovery week). Flag 20–30% decline as a caution requiring recovery optimization. Flag ≥30% persistent decline as requiring training modification—reduce volume by 30–50% and increase recovery days. Never diagnose OTS from T:C alone; combine with performance trends, subjective wellness (DALDA/POMS), sleep quality, and other biomarkers. For female athletes, deprioritize T:C ratio and instead monitor cortisol, fT3, menstrual regularity, and energy availability.

**Key sources.** Meeusen R et al., “Prevention, diagnosis and treatment of the overtraining syndrome,” ECSS/ACSM Joint Consensus, *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, 2013. Cadegiani FA & Kater CE, “EROS-BASAL Study,” *Journal of Athletic Training*, 2019. Urhausen A et al., “Blood hormones as markers of training stress,” *Sports Medicine*, 1995. Hackney AC, “Hypogonadism in exercising males,” *Frontiers in Endocrinology*, 2020. Banfi G & Dolci A, “Free testosterone/cortisol ratio in soccer,” *Journal of Sports Medicine and Physical Fitness*, 2006.

### 1C. CRP distinguishes normal training inflammation from chronic overload

**Core principle.** High-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) is a hepatic acute-phase protein that rises in response to IL-6 released from exercising muscle. Well-trained athletes typically have **lower baseline hs-CRP** than sedentary controls (median ~0.1–0.5 mg/L vs ~0.8 mg/L). The critical distinction is between the massive but transient post-exercise spike (normal) and persistently elevated resting levels (pathological).

**Evidence summary.**

- **Athlete baselines:** Trained swimmers show median hs-CRP of **0.1 mg/L** (80% lower than sedentary controls); trained rowers ~0.26 mg/L (Kasapis & Thompson, 2005, referencing Liesen et al.).
- **Acute exercise response:** CRP increases up to **2,000% (20-fold)** after a marathon, peaking at 24–48 hours post-exercise (Siegel et al., 2001). Returns to baseline within 2–6 days.
- **Temporal cascade:** IL-6 rises first (during/immediately post-exercise, up to 100-fold after marathon), triggering IL-10 (anti-inflammatory), then CRP peaks at 24–48 hours. This is a healthy myokine-driven cascade.
- **Chronic training effect on CRP:** Fedewa et al. (2017) meta-analysis found exercise training reduces CRP with a **mean effect size of 0.26** (95% CI: 0.18–0.34, p<0.001). Regular training is anti-inflammatory at baseline.
- **Threshold interpretation for athletes at rest (≥48h post-exercise):** <0.5 mg/L = optimal; 0.5–1.0 = normal for training athletes; 1.0–3.0 = elevated, investigate recovery and training load; **3.0–10.0 mg/L = concerning**, suggests chronic inflammation, overtraining, underlying infection, or injury; **>10.0 mg/L = acute illness**, not a training response.

**Application guideline.** Always require ≥48 hours post-exercise for meaningful hs-CRP measurement. Two measurements 2 weeks apart establish true baseline. The AI coach should flag persistent hs-CRP >1.0 mg/L (measured on multiple occasions at rest) for training load review. Values >3.0 mg/L should trigger volume reduction and investigation. Values >10.0 mg/L should halt training and prompt medical evaluation. During heavy training blocks (e.g., pre-season for ski touring), expect mild CRP elevation that resolves during recovery weeks—this is normal supercompensation signaling.

**Key sources.** Kasapis C & Thompson PD, “Effects of physical activity on serum CRP,” *Journal of the American College of Cardiology*, 2005. Fedewa MV et al., “Exercise training and CRP meta-analysis,” *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 2017. Siegel AJ et al., CRP post-marathon data, 2001.

### 1D. Vitamin D levels above clinical sufficiency optimize athletic performance

**Core principle.** Athletes require 25(OH)D levels well above the clinical sufficiency threshold of 30 ng/mL. Peak neuromuscular performance and injury protection appear at **40–60 ng/mL** (100–150 nmol/L).  Deficiency prevalence is strikingly high: **33–90%** of collegiate and professional athletes have suboptimal vitamin D,  with highest risk at latitudes >35° during winter months—directly relevant to mountain athletes in alpine environments.

**Evidence summary.**

- **Athlete-specific optimal range:** 40–60 ng/mL (Cannell et al., 2009; Larson-Meyer GSSI SSE #148, 2015).  Peak neuromuscular function at ~50 ng/mL.  No demonstrated benefit above 60 ng/mL.
- **Muscle function:** Close et al. (2013) found 5,000 IU D3/day improved 10-m sprint times and vertical jump height. Owens et al. (2015) showed 4,000 IU/day improved muscle force recovery after eccentric exercise.  Bischoff-Ferrari et al. (2004) documented strength increases up to a 25(OH)D threshold of ~**38 ng/mL**, then plateau.  Vitamin D deficiency causes type II (fast-twitch) muscle fiber atrophy.  
- **Stress fracture risk:** Ruohola et al. (2006) found **3.6× higher** stress fracture risk with 25(OH)D <30 ng/mL. Lappe et al. (2008) RCT of 5,201 Navy recruits showed **20% lower stress fracture incidence** with calcium + 800 IU vitamin D/day.   An NCAA study (Miller et al., 2020) found stress fracture incidence dropped from **7.51% to 1.69%** when athletes maintained ≥40 ng/mL. 
- **Immune function:** He et al. (2016) established ≥30 ng/mL as minimum for optimal immunity in athletes. Martineau et al. (2017) meta-analysis showed vitamin D supplementation reduced respiratory infections (OR 0.88 overall; **OR 0.30 in deficient individuals**). Daily/weekly dosing is superior to bolus.
- **IOC position:** Maughan et al. (2018) IOC Consensus identified vitamin D and probiotics as the most promising immune-supportive supplements for athletes,  recommending 1,000 IU/day D3 autumn through spring. 

**Supplementation protocols by deficiency level:**

- Severe deficiency (<10 ng/mL): 50,000 IU/week for 8 weeks, then 1,500–2,000 IU/day maintenance
- Deficiency (10–20 ng/mL): 4,000–6,000 IU/day for 8–12 weeks, then 1,500–2,000 IU/day 
- Insufficiency (20–30 ng/mL): 2,000–4,000 IU/day for 8–10 weeks, then 1,000–2,000 IU/day
- Suboptimal for athletes (30–40 ng/mL): 1,500–2,000 IU/day year-round 
- Optimal (40–60 ng/mL): 1,000 IU/day maintenance in winter

Use vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol), taken with a fat-containing meal. Avoid bolus dosing (>50,000 IU single dose). Toxicity threshold is >150 ng/mL; practical ceiling for supplementation target is 60 ng/mL.

**Application guideline.** The AI coach should test vitamin D twice yearly (autumn and spring) at minimum. For mountain athletes at northern latitudes, assume winter insufficiency unless supplementing. Flag <30 ng/mL for supplementation initiation and <20 ng/mL for aggressive repletion with recheck at 8–12 weeks. Athletes with stress fracture history should maintain ≥40 ng/mL  with concurrent calcium intake of 1,500 mg/day. During winter training blocks, default to 1,000–2,000 IU/day maintenance even without lab data.

**Key sources.** Cannell JJ et al., “Athletic performance and vitamin D,” *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, 2009. Close GL et al., “Assessment of vitamin D in non-supplemented professional athletes,” *Journal of Sports Sciences*, 2013. Owens DJ et al., vitamin D and skeletal muscle repair, *American Journal of Physiology*, 2015. He CS et al., “Is there an optimal vitamin D status for immunity?” *Exercise Immunology Review*, 2016. Lappe J et al., calcium and vitamin D decreases stress fracture incidence, *Journal of Bone and Mineral Research*, 2008. Martineau AR et al., vitamin D and respiratory infections meta-analysis, *BMJ*, 2017. Maughan RJ et al., IOC Consensus Statement on dietary supplements, *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 2018.

### 1E. Thyroid markers reveal energy availability, not thyroid disease, in most athletes

**Core principle.** Heavy training frequently produces “low T3 syndrome” (euthyroid sick syndrome)—low free T3, elevated reverse T3, with near-normal TSH and T4. This is a **hypothalamic adaptation to energy deficit**, not thyroid gland disease, and **does not warrant thyroid hormone replacement**.  The critical clinical question is whether low T3 reflects acceptable training adaptation or problematic low energy availability (RED-S).

**Evidence summary.**

- **Energy availability threshold:** Loucks & Heath (1994) identified the critical breakpoint: T3 reduction of **16%** and fT3 reduction of **9%** occurred abruptly between energy availability of 19 and **25 kcal/kg lean body mass/day**.  This landmark study established that exercise itself does not alter thyroid hormones—**energy availability is the sole driver**. 
- **Loucks & Callister (1993):** Low energy availability (8 vs 30 kcal/kg/day) reduced T3 by **15%**, free T3 by **18%**, and increased reverse T3 by **24%** within just 4 days.  
- **Sex differences:** Males appear more resistant to acute low-T3 induction. Short-term EA reductions to 15 kcal/kg LBM/day through dietary restriction did not significantly impact T3 in males (Koehler et al., 2016; Papageorgiou et al., 2017). However, chronic low EA affects both sexes. 
- **IOC 2023 RED-S Consensus (Mountjoy et al.):** Energy availability <30 kcal/kg FFM/day is the established threshold below which physiological disruption occurs, including thyroid suppression.   T3 is identified as a key biomarker in the IOC REDs CAT2 assessment tool.  The dose-response for T3 is nonlinear, with largest reductions occurring between EA of 20–30 kcal/kg FFM/day. 
- **Differentiating patterns:**
  - Adaptive/euthyroid sick: Low T3, high rT3, normal/low-normal TSH → reversible with adequate nutrition 
  - RED-S: Low T3 + amenorrhea (females) or low testosterone (males) + low IGF-1 + elevated cortisol + reduced resting metabolic rate → requires energy deficit correction 
  - Primary hypothyroidism: TSH >4.5 mIU/L with low fT4, often TPO antibodies positive → requires medical treatment 
- **EROS study:** Free T3 and IGF-1 were proposed as “sentinel markers of recovery from OTS” (Cadegiani, 2020), showing significant increases during 12-week recovery intervention.

**Reference ranges for athletes:** Standard ranges are TSH 0.4–4.5 mIU/L, free T4 0.9–1.7 ng/dL, free T3 2.0–4.4 pg/mL.  Concerning patterns for athletes include fT3 <3.5 pmol/L (or dropping >15% from individual baseline), especially combined with amenorrhea, low testosterone, or progressive weight loss. TSH <0.5 mIU/L with low fT3 suggests central hypothalamic suppression rather than thyroid disease.

**Application guideline.** The AI coach should never recommend thyroid hormone replacement for low T3 in the context of heavy training.   Instead, flag low T3 patterns as energy availability red flags. When fT3 drops >15% from baseline or falls below 2.5 pg/mL, prompt assessment of energy intake relative to training expenditure. For mountain endurance athletes with high energy expenditure (ski touring, splitboarding days can burn 3,000–5,000+ kcal), low T3 strongly suggests caloric deficit. The intervention is increased energy intake, not reduced training, unless other RED-S markers are also present.   Always interpret thyroid markers alongside menstrual status (females), testosterone (males), body weight trends, and subjective energy.

**Key sources.** Loucks AB & Heath EM, “Induction of low-T3 syndrome occurs at a threshold of energy availability,” *American Journal of Physiology*, 1994. Loucks AB & Callister R, “Induction and prevention of low-T3 syndrome,” *American Journal of Physiology*, 1993. Mountjoy M et al., “2023 IOC Consensus Statement on REDs,” *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 2023. Cadegiani FA et al., “EROS-LONGITUDINAL Study,” *Journal of Athletic Training*, 2020.

## 2. Retest intervals calibrated by phase and marker

**Core principle.** Testing frequency should escalate through three phases: baseline screening (establishing reference values), periodic monitoring (tracking adaptation), and issue-tracking (following treatment response). Pre-analytic conditions profoundly affect results: blood draws must occur **7:00–10:00 AM, fasted 8–12 hours, ≥48 hours after strenuous exercise**  (≥7 days for CK and liver enzymes after heavy resistance training). Seated for 15 minutes before venipuncture. Ensure euhydration.

**Evidence summary and recommended frequencies.**

**Baseline/screening phase (recreational athletes):** Comprehensive panel **1–2× per year** minimum. Best timing: pre-season (autumn) and mid-season (spring) for endurance athletes; pre- and post-training cycle for strength athletes. This establishes individual reference ranges. Elite athletes test quarterly.

**Monitoring phase (during structured training blocks):** Key performance markers every **6–8 weeks** for recreational athletes. During intensified training blocks, critical markers (ferritin, hs-CRP) can be checked every 4–6 weeks.

**Issue-tracking phase (managing a deficiency or concern):**

- Iron repletion: Recheck ferritin at **6–8 weeks** after oral supplementation initiation; **4 weeks** after IV iron
- Vitamin D repletion: Recheck at **8–12 weeks** after starting supplementation
- Thyroid follow-up: Recheck at **6–8 weeks** after intervention
- Hormonal recovery (T:C ratio): Recheck at **4–8 weeks** after training load modification
- CRP normalization: Recheck at **2–4 weeks** after training modification

**Recommended panels by athlete type.** Endurance athletes (ski touring, splitboarding, hike-and-fly): CBC with differential, full iron panel (ferritin, TSAT, serum iron, TIBC), vitamin D, thyroid panel (TSH, fT3, fT4), hs-CRP, AM cortisol, testosterone (total + free for males), metabolic panel. Strength athletes: CBC, iron panel, vitamin D, testosterone (total + free), cortisol, liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT), CK, creatinine/eGFR (essential for creatine users), metabolic panel. Hybrid athletes (the typical user combining strength and mountain endurance) should get the full combined panel at baseline, then monitor the most relevant markers based on training phase emphasis.

**Critical pre-analytic notes.** Biotin supplements (>1,000 mcg) interfere with immunoassays for TSH, T3, and T4—discontinue 3–5 days before draws. For females, standardize timing relative to menstrual cycle. Avoid blood sampling during acute illness (ferritin is an acute phase reactant and will be falsely elevated).  

**Application guideline.** The AI coach should prompt blood work at the start of each major training phase (e.g., base building, build phase, peak/taper) and schedule follow-up based on findings. Default to semi-annual for healthy athletes. Escalate to 6–8 week intervals when any marker is out of range. After two consecutive normal results during issue-tracking, return to screening frequency.

**Key sources.** Pedlar CR et al., “Blood biomarker profiling and monitoring for high-performance physiology and nutrition,” *Sports Medicine*, 2019. Hecksteden A et al., “Blood-based biomarkers for managing workload in athletes,” *Sports Medicine*, 2023. Pettersson J et al., “Muscular exercise can cause highly pathological liver function tests,” *British Journal of Clinical Pharmacology*, 2008.

## 3. Red flag thresholds for automated decision-making

**Core principle.** These thresholds trigger three levels of response: training modification (reduce volume/intensity by 30–50%), medical referral (consult physician before continuing normal training), and supplementation/dietary intervention. All thresholds assume resting samples drawn under proper pre-analytic conditions. Individual trends matter more than single values— a rapid decline within reference range can be more meaningful than a stable value slightly outside it.

**Tier 1: Triggers for immediate training modification**

|Marker                     |Threshold                                          |Action                                                     |
|---------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------|
|Ferritin                   |<35 ng/mL (either sex)                             |Reduce high-intensity volume; initiate iron supplementation|
|Hemoglobin                 |<13.5 g/dL (M) / <12.0 g/dL (F)                    |Reduce training intensity; investigate iron status         |
|Testosterone (males, total)|<400 ng/dL                                         |Assess energy availability; reduce training load 30%       |
|T:C ratio                  |>20% decline from individual baseline              |Increase recovery; reduce training intensity               |
|hs-CRP                     |>3.0 mg/L (persistent, at rest, ≥48h post-exercise)|Reduce training volume; review recovery quality            |
|Free T3                    |<2.5 pg/mL or >15% decline from baseline           |Increase caloric intake; assess energy availability        |
|CK                         |>1,000 U/L (resting, 72h+ post-exercise)           |Reduce eccentric load; extend recovery between sessions    |
|WBC                        |<3.5 × 10⁹/L (persistent)                          |Reduce training load; assess immune function               |

**Application guideline.** The AI coach should implement a tiered alert system. Tier 1 findings should automatically reduce prescribed training load within the next programming cycle. Tier 2 findings should generate a prominent alert to seek medical evaluation, with training held at reduced levels until clearance. Tier 3 findings should trigger supplementation recommendations alongside training plan adjustments. Always interpret multiple markers together—isolated borderline values rarely warrant intervention, but **two or more markers trending in the same direction** (e.g., low ferritin + declining hemoglobin + increasing fatigue scores) demand immediate action.

## 4. Creatine supplementation: performance, dosing, and lab interference

### Creatine raises creatinine without impairing kidneys

**Core principle.** Creatine supplementation reliably increases serum creatinine because creatine spontaneously converts to creatinine at ~1.7%/day of total body stores. Supplementation increases intramuscular creatine by **20–40%**,  proportionally increasing creatinine production. This creates false positives for kidney dysfunction on standard blood panels because eGFR equations assume creatinine rises only when filtration fails. **Cystatin C** is the definitive alternative marker, unaffected by creatine intake or muscle mass. 

**Evidence summary.**

- **Magnitude of creatinine increase:** Typical elevation with maintenance dosing (3–5 g/day): **+0.1–0.3 mg/dL** above baseline. During loading (20 g/day): **+0.2–0.4 mg/dL**. Creatine users commonly present at 1.2–1.5 mg/dL, often flagged as “high” on standard panels.
- **Meta-analytic confirmation (Naeini et al., 2025, *BMC Nephrology*, 21 studies):** Pooled creatinine increase of **0.07 µmol/L** (≈0.0008 mg/dL)—statistically significant but clinically trivial. **No significant change in GFR** between creatine and control groups.
- **Gold-standard evidence:** In a controlled study using 51Cr-EDTA clearance (direct measured GFR), serum creatinine rose from 1.03 to 1.27 mg/dL (+23%) with 20 g/day loading, but **actual kidney filtration was unchanged**.
- **Cystatin C validation:** Gualano et al. (2008) showed cystatin C actually **decreased** (0.82 → 0.71 mg/L) during 12 weeks of creatine supplementation, confirming preserved or improved kidney function. Domingues et al. (2020) found novel kidney injury biomarkers KIM-1 and MCP-1 showed **no differences** between creatine and control groups.
- **Washout:** Creatinine normalizes to baseline within **2–4 weeks** of cessation.

**Application guideline.** The AI coach must annotate any blood work interpretation with creatine supplementation status. When an athlete reports creatine use and shows creatinine 1.2–1.5 mg/dL with no other concerning findings, the system should note this as expected and not flag kidney concern. If creatinine exceeds 1.5 mg/dL in males or 1.2 mg/dL in females on creatine, recommend cystatin C testing before any clinical concern. True red flags: creatinine rising progressively after dose stabilization, proteinuria/albuminuria, or cystatin C-based eGFR also reduced.

### Strength training benefits are robust and well-quantified

**Evidence summary.**

- **Strength gains:** Rawson & Volek (2003) meta-analysis of 22 studies: average strength increase **20% with creatine vs 12% with placebo** (8 percentage points greater). Lanhers et al. (2017): global upper limb **effect size 0.317** (p<0.001); bench press ES 0.265. A 2025 comprehensive meta-analysis: overall muscle strength **SMD 0.43** (95% CI: 0.25–0.61). For athletic populations specifically: muscle power **ES 1.19** (large effect).
- **Absolute strength gains:** Dempsey et al. (2002): bench press **+6.85 kg** and squat **+9.76 kg** greater than placebo. Wang et al. (2024): upper-body strength **+4.43 kg** (p<0.001).
- **Lean body mass:** Delpino et al. (2022) meta-analysis of 35 RCTs: **+1.10 kg** with resistance training (95% CI: 0.56–1.65). Males: **+1.46 kg**. Females: +0.29 kg (not significant). Desai et al. (2024): **+1.14 kg LBM**, −0.88% body fat. Creatine without exercise produces no meaningful lean mass change.
- **Effective for exercises <3 minutes** (ATP-PCr and glycolytic systems). Greatest benefits in repeated high-intensity efforts, not single maximal attempts.

### Endurance benefits are context-dependent and relevant for mountain athletes

**Evidence summary.**

- **Steady-state endurance:** Creatine is **ineffective for steady-state VO2max performance** in trained athletes (meta-analysis, 2023). May slightly decrease VO2max due to body mass increase.
- **Intermittent high-intensity within endurance:** Forbes et al. (2023, JISSN) found creatine **increases time to exhaustion** during high-intensity endurance activities and benefits performances requiring **multiple surges, pace changes, and finishing kicks**—the exact metabolic demands of ski touring (steep skin tracks), splitboarding (repeated high-intensity climbs), and hike-and-fly paragliding (rapid ascents).
- **Body mass consideration:** Creatine causes **~1–2 kg** body mass increase (water + lean mass). Study on 8% incline hill climbing at 90% VO2max found creatine-mediated mass gain had **no detrimental effect** on cycling hill climbing. For weight-bearing activities like hiking, the mass penalty is real but may be offset by power benefits during steep technical terrain.
- **Net assessment for mountain athletes:** Likely beneficial for ski touring and splitboarding where terrain varies between steady uphills and steep ramps requiring explosive effort. For hike-and-fly where every gram of pack weight matters and approaches are often sustained steady-state, the 1–2 kg mass increase may outweigh benefits during the ascent phase, though launch-site power benefits exist. Individual assessment required.

### Safety profile is the strongest in sports nutrition

**Evidence summary.**

- **ISSN Position Stand (Kreider et al., 2017):** Short and long-term supplementation up to **30 g/day for 5 years** is safe and well-tolerated in healthy individuals. Safe across populations from infants to elderly. Creatine monohydrate is the **most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement currently available**.
- **Antonio et al. (2021) ISSN update** reaffirmed all safety conclusions and addressed persistent myths about kidney damage.
- **Kidney function:** No controlled trial has demonstrated kidney impairment in healthy individuals. Lugaresi et al. (2013) confirmed safety in resistance-trained individuals consuming high-protein diets. Gualano et al. (2011) showed safety even in type 2 diabetic patients. The only caution: insufficient evidence in pre-existing kidney disease—recommend caution and medical supervision.
- **No evidence of liver damage** in any controlled trial.

### Dosing protocols

**Loading protocol:** **0.3 g/kg/day** (typically 20 g/day split into 4 × 5g doses) for **5–7 days**. Increases muscle creatine stores by 20–40%. Co-ingest with ~50g carbohydrate for enhanced retention.

**Maintenance protocol:** **3–5 g/day** (0.03–0.05 g/kg/day). Larger athletes (>90 kg): 5–10 g/day. ISSN recommends 3 g/day as general health dose throughout lifespan.

**No-loading alternative:** **3 g/day for 28+ days** achieves the same saturation as loading but with slower onset. Preferred for athletes wanting to avoid GI discomfort or rapid water weight gain.

**Timing:** Post-exercise slightly superior for uptake (enhanced muscle blood flow, insulin sensitivity). Co-ingest with carbohydrate + protein. Consistent daily intake matters more than precise timing.

**Form:** Creatine monohydrate exclusively. No other form (ethyl ester, HCl, buffered) has demonstrated superiority; most contain less creatine per gram and cost more. Creatine ethyl ester specifically degrades rapidly to creatinine, causing disproportionate creatinine elevation without ergogenic benefit.

**Application guideline.** The AI coach should recommend creatine monohydrate for all strength-focused programming at 3–5 g/day maintenance. For mountain endurance athletes, recommend creatine during strength-building mesocycles and withdraw during peak endurance/weight-sensitive phases (e.g., weeks before a major ski touring race) if the 1–2 kg mass gain is a concern. Always flag creatine use in the athlete’s profile so blood work interpretation accounts for creatinine elevation. Recommend the no-loading protocol for athletes who are weight-sensitive or experience GI issues.

## Conclusion: building an integrated biomarker decision engine

The most actionable insight across all these markers is that **individual longitudinal tracking outperforms any single-point absolute threshold**. An AI coaching system should establish personal baselines for every athlete during a well-rested state and then track deviations—a 25% decline in ferritin within an individual’s normal range is more significant than a stable value that happens to sit below a population threshold. The multi-marker approach is essential: no single biomarker diagnoses overtraining, RED-S, or inadequate recovery. Instead, converging patterns—falling T:C ratio + rising CRP + declining fT3 + stagnating performance—create high-confidence signals for intervention.

For the specific population of mountain endurance and strength hybrid athletes, **iron status and vitamin D are the highest-yield screening markers**, given the intersection of high-volume aerobic training, altitude exposure, seasonal light restriction, and the caloric demands of long mountain days. Thyroid markers serve as the best early-warning system for energy availability problems—particularly relevant when athletes underestimate the enormous caloric cost of backcountry ski touring or splitboarding (often 600–800 kcal/hour). Creatine supplementation offers well-evidenced strength benefits with minimal endurance penalty when managed intelligently around training phases, but requires explicit annotation in the athlete profile to prevent false kidney alarms on every blood panel.
